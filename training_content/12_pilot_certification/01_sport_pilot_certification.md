# Sport Pilot Certification for Powered Parachute Pilots
## Becoming, and Staying, Legal to Fly Two-Place PPCs

---

## Introduction

If your powered parachute meets the Part 103 ultralight definition — single
seat, under 254 lb empty, 5 gallons of fuel, 55-knot top speed — you can
fly it without a pilot certificate, a medical, or aircraft registration.
Most people reading this will not stop there. The moment you want to carry
a passenger, you have crossed out of Part 103 and into the world of light-
sport aircraft (LSA), and the simplest certificate that lets you fly an
LSA-category PPC is the **sport pilot certificate**.

This module covers everything you need to know about earning and
maintaining that certificate: who can get one, what training is required,
what you can and cannot do as a sport pilot, how to stay legal year over
year, and how to add new privileges as your skills grow. The actual
regulatory text lives in 14 CFR Part 61. We will cite the sections so you
can pull them up in the eCFR viewer, but the goal of this chapter is to
explain *why* the rules look the way they do and *how* a working PPC pilot
applies them in the real world.

---

## Section 1: What a Sport Pilot Certificate Is

The FAA created the sport pilot certificate in 2004 specifically to lower
the barrier to recreational flying. It sits below private and commercial
in the certification hierarchy: less training, less medical paperwork,
and a tighter set of operating limitations.

A sport pilot certificate is **category-specific**. The certificate itself
is generic; the privileges you earn attach to it as ratings or
endorsements for a specific category of aircraft. The category-and-class
combinations available at the sport-pilot level include:

- Airplane (single-engine, land or sea)
- Glider
- Lighter-than-air (balloon or airship)
- Rotorcraft (gyroplane)
- Powered parachute (land or sea)
- Weight-shift control (land or sea)

For our purposes, the relevant rating is **powered parachute land**, and
in this module that is what "sport pilot" will mean unless we say
otherwise.

The certificate is the credential. To exercise its privileges in the air,
you also need:

1. The certificate itself (a plastic card from the FAA Airman Certification
   Branch)
2. Photo ID (driver's license or passport)
3. Either a current FAA medical certificate **or** a valid U.S. driver's
   license (we'll get to which, and when, in Section 2)
4. A current flight review (every 24 calendar months)
5. Recency of experience for any passengers you carry (3 takeoffs and 3
   landings within the preceding 90 days)

The certificate itself does not expire. The privileges to exercise it can
lapse — and we'll cover exactly how to keep them current.

## Section 2: Who Can Become a Sport Pilot

The FAA's eligibility rules for sport pilot are deliberately accessible.
There are four boxes to check:

### Age

You can begin training as a **student sport pilot** at **16**. You must
be at least **17** to take the practical test and be issued the sport
pilot certificate itself. (For glider and balloon students, the floor is
14, but PPCs are powered, so 16 is your starting line.) See § 61.83 for
student eligibility and § 61.303 for the certificate itself.

### English Language

You must be able to read, speak, write, and understand English. The FAA
takes this seriously — it underpins the safety of radio communications.
Limited exceptions exist for medical reasons (with a corresponding
limitation written onto the certificate), but for the typical applicant
this is a yes/no requirement. See § 61.83(c) and § 61.103(c).

### Medical

Sport pilot is the only certificate the FAA issues that does **not**
require a third-class FAA medical examination. Instead, you may exercise
sport pilot privileges using either:

1. A current and valid U.S. driver's license, **or**
2. An FAA-issued medical certificate of any class

You self-certify your fitness on every flight using the IMSAFE checklist
(Illness, Medication, Stress, Alcohol, Fatigue, Emotion). The driver's
license is not a guarantee of fitness — it's a placeholder for the
ongoing self-assessment the FAA expects you to perform.

> **Important catch**: The driver's-license medical option is **only
> available** if the most recently issued FAA medical certificate (if
> any) was *not* denied, suspended, or revoked. If your last FAA medical
> was a "no," the driver's license cannot rescue you — you need to hold
> a current FAA medical to fly as a sport pilot. See § 61.23(c)(2).

A common misconception is that the absence of a medical exam means
sport pilots can fly with conditions that would disqualify a standard
private pilot. That is wrong. § 61.53 prohibits flying with any known
medical condition that would impair safe operation, regardless of which
medical pathway you used. A sport pilot with uncontrolled diabetes who
hasn't had an FAA medical is not exempt — they are simply self-grounding
on the same standards a private pilot would be examined against.

### Citizenship and TSA

There is no U.S. citizenship requirement, but non-U.S. citizens taking
flight training in the United States must comply with the TSA's Alien
Flight Student Program (AFSP) before training begins. Most U.S.-based
flight schools handle the paperwork.

## Section 3: The Training Path

The minimum training for a sport pilot certificate with a powered
parachute rating is laid out in § 61.313(c). It is meaningfully shorter
than the 40 hours needed for a private pilot certificate, reflecting the
narrower scope of sport-pilot privileges.

| Requirement | Minimum |
|---|---|
| Total flight time | 12 hours |
| Flight training in a PPC | 10 hours, including ≥ 20 takeoffs and landings |
| Solo flight time | 3 hours |
| Cross-country solo flight | 1 flight, ≥ 25 NM straight-line, full-stop landing |
| Pre-test training within 60 days of practical | 2 hours of test prep |

Those are minimums. In practice, most students take more — the FAA's
numbers describe what's required to pass, not what's required to be safe
and confident. A typical complete training program is 15–25 hours of
flight time spread over 2–6 months, depending on weather and how often
you fly.

### The pre-solo phase

Before your first solo flight, your instructor will:

1. Endorse your **logbook** for solo flight in the **specific make and
   model** of PPC you've been training on (§ 61.87)
2. Administer a **pre-solo written test** covering basic regulations,
   local airspace, and aircraft systems
3. Verify proficiency in pre-solo maneuvers including normal and
   crosswind takeoffs and landings, traffic patterns, emergency
   procedures, and aborted takeoffs

The make-and-model endorsement is significant. If you trained on a
Six Chuter and your buddy lets you fly his Powrachute Pegasus, your
solo endorsement does not transfer — your instructor must endorse you
for the new aircraft. Solo endorsements expire after **90 days**; if you
haven't soloed in that aircraft within 90 days of the endorsement, your
instructor must re-endorse you.

### The solo cross-country

The single solo cross-country requirement (1 flight, at least 25 NM
straight-line distance, with a full-stop landing at an airport) is the
flight where most students find out what their training actually was for.
You're alone in the aircraft, navigating by chart and pilotage, talking
on a CTAF, dealing with whatever the day's wind and weather hand you.

Common pitfalls: under-fueling because the chart distance looks short
(remember groundspeed effects on a 30-mph PPC); skipping the diversion
plan because "I'll just see how I feel"; not getting an instructor
endorsement *for that specific cross-country flight* before launching.
The solo XC endorsement is per-flight, not blanket. See § 61.93(c).

## Section 4: The Sport Pilot Knowledge Test

The knowledge test ("written test") is the FAA's check on your book
learning. For sport pilot - powered parachute, you'll take the **PAR**
(Powered Parachute) test at an FAA-approved testing center.

- 60 multiple-choice questions
- 2 hours, 30 minutes
- 70% to pass
- Computer-based, immediate score
- Your instructor must endorse your logbook to authorize you to take it

The test draws from the FAA Sport Pilot Airman Certification Standards
(ACS) for Powered Parachute, which defines exactly what topics are fair
game and at what depth. The 10 ACS subject areas are the same ones the
practice test in this app organizes around: pilot qualifications,
regulations, aeromedical factors and ADM, aerodynamics, aircraft
systems, weight and balance / performance, weather, airspace and
navigation, communications and airport operations, and cross-country
planning.

A passing score is good for **24 calendar months**. You must pass the
practical test within that window or you'll have to re-take the
knowledge test. There is no point in racing through the written and
then putting off the checkride for a year — you'll just shorten your
own runway.

## Section 5: The Practical Test (Checkride)

The practical test ("checkride") is a single-day event, typically 3–4
hours total, with two parts:

1. **Oral exam** (1–2 hours): the Designated Pilot Examiner (DPE) asks
   you about regulations, aeromedical, weather, performance, airspace,
   and any topic in the ACS. They are looking for *understanding*, not
   memorization. Expect "what would you do if..." scenarios.
2. **Flight test** (1–2 hours): a series of maneuvers and procedures
   from the ACS, judged against the specific tolerances published there
   (e.g. altitude maintained within ±100 feet, headings within ±10°).

You arrive with a complete cross-country flight plan the DPE assigns by
phone or email a few days in advance. They will challenge you on it
during the oral and may ask you to fly the first leg.

Your instructor must sign you off as ready, both in your logbook and on
the FAA application (Form 8710-11). The DPE has the authority to fail
you on any single ACS task; in practice, most failures are clustered in
weather decision-making, weight & balance reasoning, and the same
maneuver the candidate has been weak on all along.

## Section 6: Privileges and Limitations

This is the part many sport pilots are surprised by. The certificate
gives you a meaningful, but bounded, set of privileges. Knowing exactly
where the boundaries are protects you legally and operationally.

### What you may do (§ 61.315(b))

- Act as pilot in command of a light-sport powered parachute
- Carry one passenger
- Fly during the day under VFR
- Operate within the United States and U.S. territories

### What you may NOT do (§ 61.315(c))

You may not...

1. ...act as PIC of an aircraft carrying a passenger or property for
   compensation or hire
2. ...operate in furtherance of a business
3. ...carry more than one passenger
4. ...operate at night (sunset to sunrise)
5. ...operate above 10,000 feet MSL or 2,000 feet AGL, whichever is
   higher
6. ...operate when flight visibility is less than 3 statute miles or
   without visual reference to the surface
7. ...operate an aircraft towing any object (banner, glider, anything)
8. ...operate in Class A airspace
9. ...operate in Class B, C, or D airspace, or in airspace having an
   airport with an operating control tower, *without an additional
   instructor endorsement*
10. ...operate in foreign airspace unless authorized by that country
11. ...operate at an aircraft maximum speed in level flight greater than
    87 knots CAS for powered parachutes (and weight-shift-control)

The compensation prohibition deserves a closer look. **Sport pilots
cannot pro-rata cost-share** the way private pilots can under § 61.113.
If your friend offers to pay for fuel and the rental fee in exchange
for a ride, accepting that money is a violation. The FAA reads the
sport-pilot privilege rules narrowly because the certificate has a
lower training and medical bar than the private — the lower bar comes
with a corresponding limit on what you can charge for your time at the
controls.

The "in furtherance of a business" rule is similarly broad. You can't
fly your sport-pilot PPC to a real-estate showing or a job site, even
if no one pays you to fly, because the flight serves a business purpose.
The intent is to keep sport-pilot operations recreational.

The 87-knot speed limit is one most PPC pilots will never bump into —
trim airspeeds run 30–40 mph (26–35 knots) — but the rule exists in
the LSA category definition itself, not just the sport pilot privileges.

## Section 7: Staying Current

A pilot certificate doesn't lapse. Your *privileges* to use it can. Two
recurring requirements matter:

### The 24-Month Flight Review (§ 61.56)

Every 24 calendar months, you must complete a flight review with an
authorized flight instructor. The minimums:

- 1 hour of ground training, plus
- 1 hour of flight training

There is no pass/fail. Your instructor either signs your logbook
endorsing you as having "satisfactorily accomplished" the review, or
recommends additional training. Most CFIs use the BFR as a chance to
clean up rust — go-arounds, emergency procedures, soft-field technique.

A few events satisfy the flight review automatically and reset the
24-month clock without a separate BFR:

- Successfully completing a checkride for any pilot certificate or
  rating
- A pilot proficiency check for an FAA-recognized program (rare for
  sport pilots)

If your last checkride was within 24 months, you're current — no
separate BFR needed. The day after the 24th calendar month, your
privileges lapse until you complete one.

### 90-Day Passenger Currency (§ 61.57)

To carry a passenger, you must have completed within the preceding 90
days **three takeoffs and three landings** as the sole manipulator of
the controls in an aircraft of the **same category and class**.

For PPCs that means three takeoffs and three landings in *any* powered
parachute (the rule is not make-and-model specific). They do not need
to be to a full stop — that requirement applies only to night
operations and tailwheel airplanes, neither of which is on the
sport-pilot menu.

Watch the calendar carefully. If you flew on April 1 and don't fly
again until July 5, you've gone 95 days. Your 90-day currency lapsed
on June 30 — your first three takeoffs and landings on July 5 must be
solo, and only after the third can you carry a passenger.

### Two-pilot common scenarios

| Situation | Are you legal to carry a passenger? |
|---|---|
| Last flight 60 days ago, 3 t/o + 3 ldg that day | Yes |
| Last flight 95 days ago, 3 t/o + 3 ldg that day | No — solo first, then passenger |
| BFR done 23 months ago, last flight last week | Yes |
| BFR done 25 months ago, last flight yesterday | No — flight review lapsed |
| BFR is current; haven't flown in 6 months | Solo only until you regain 90-day currency |

## Section 8: Adding Privileges

The sport pilot certificate is the entry point. As you grow, you can
add privileges with logbook endorsements rather than full re-tests.

### New make and model

Per § 61.321, a sport pilot wishing to operate a *different make and
model* of light-sport aircraft (within the same category and class)
needs an instructor endorsement specific to that make and model. The
endorsement covers the differences — control feel, performance,
inflation technique, instrumentation. It is not a new checkride; it is
a documented sign-off after sufficient training.

A "different make and model" is interpreted broadly. A Pegasus and a
Six Chuter are different. So are a Pegasus 912 and a Pegasus 582 in
practice — the engines and reserved capabilities differ enough that
many CFIs require a separate endorsement. When in doubt, fly with an
instructor and get the endorsement.

### Class B, C, D, and Class E surface area airspace

Per § 61.325, a sport pilot wishing to operate in Class B, C, D, or
Class E surface-area airspace needs ground and flight training on the
specific airspace operations involved, plus a logbook endorsement from
the instructor. Without it, the only airspace you can legally enter is
Class E (above the surface area) and Class G — which covers most of
what a typical PPC pilot does anyway, but boxes you out of any
towered-airport visit.

This is one of the most commonly recommended endorsements for new
sport pilots. The training is straightforward (a few hours of ground
on radio procedures and airspace boundaries, plus a flight or two
through a Class C or D airport with the instructor), and it
dramatically expands where you can fly.

### Stepping up to higher certificates

Sport pilot training counts toward higher certificates. If you later
pursue a private pilot certificate, your sport pilot hours generally
apply. The training you completed for sport pilot — pre-solo, solo,
solo cross-country, knowledge test — overlaps significantly with
private pilot requirements. The path "sport pilot first, then private
later" is well-trodden, especially for pilots who want the time and
budget flexibility of sport pilot up front.

## Section 9: Logbooks and Records

Your logbook is your primary proof that you are who you say you are
as a pilot. § 61.51 lists the required entries:

- Date of flight
- Total flight time
- Type of aircraft (make, model, registration)
- Conditions of flight (day, night, actual or simulated instrument)
- Type of pilot experience (PIC, dual received, solo, etc.)
- Departure point and destination

Notably **not** required: the weather conditions, your pilot certificate
number, fuel quantities, or who paid for the flight. Many pilots record
those anyway because they're useful for personal records, but they're
not legally required.

You may log time as **pilot in command** when you are the sole
manipulator of the controls of an aircraft for which you are rated.
For most sport pilots in their own PPC, this is straightforward — every
flight where you're flying is PIC time. It is **not** PIC time when
you're sitting in the right seat as a safety observer, when your
instructor is doing a demo flight, or when you're receiving ground
instruction in a simulator.

The endorsements your instructor signs (pre-solo, solo, make-and-model,
flight review, airspace, etc.) also live in the logbook. Keep them
preserved and legible — DPEs will want to see them on your checkride,
and ramp checks (rare but possible) can request them.

## Section 10: When Things Change

A few events require action on your part to stay legal:

### Change of address

§ 61.60: any change to your permanent mailing address must be reported
to the FAA Airmen Certification Branch within **30 days**. After 30
days without notification, you may not exercise pilot privileges until
you've notified them.

The FAA provides several easy options: online via the IACRA portal, by
mail, or by phone. It takes 5 minutes. The penalty for not doing it is
losing your privileges, so don't postpone it after a move.

### Lost or destroyed certificate

If you lose your physical certificate, you can request a replacement
online for a small fee. In the interim, you can fly using a temporary
authorization that the FAA emails you. Document what you have and
don't fly without proof of certification — a ramp check with no
certificate is a violation.

### Changes in medical status

If something changes — a new diagnosis, a new medication, a new
disqualifying condition — § 61.53 obligates you to ground yourself.
The driver's-license-medical option for sport pilots does not exempt
you from this. The IMSAFE check before every flight is your formal
moment to confront whether you're fit to fly today.

### Failure to fly

There is no "you must fly N hours per year to stay current" rule for
recreation. The 90-day passenger currency and 24-month flight review
are the only recurring requirements. You could fly once every two
years and still be legal — though obviously not safe or proficient.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Sport pilot means you can never carry passengers for hire."**
  Correct, but the rule goes farther: you can't accept *any*
  compensation for carrying a person, even fuel reimbursement. Pro-rata
  cost-sharing is a private-pilot privilege, not a sport-pilot one.

- **"The driver's license medical means I don't have to think about
  fitness."**
  Wrong. The driver's license is a placeholder for the IMSAFE check.
  You must self-ground for any condition that would impair safe
  operation, including a head cold serious enough to mess with your
  ears on descent.

- **"My solo endorsement covers any PPC I want to fly."**
  No — it's specific to the make and model your instructor endorsed
  you for, and it's only valid for 90 days from the endorsement date.

- **"My flight review and 90-day currency are the same thing."**
  Different requirements. Flight review is every 24 calendar months
  (instructor sign-off, no pass/fail). 90-day currency is 3 takeoffs
  and 3 landings to be PIC with a passenger. Both are required; one
  doesn't substitute for the other.

- **"I can fly business trips as long as I don't get paid for the
  flight itself."**
  No. § 61.315(c)(4) prohibits operating "in furtherance of a
  business." Even uncompensated flights that serve a business purpose
  are off-limits to sport pilots.

- **"I'm grounded if I don't have a current FAA medical."**
  Sport pilots, no — the driver's license is a valid medical
  alternative, *unless* you've ever had an FAA medical denied,
  suspended, or revoked.

## Worked Scenario 1: The first passenger

You earned your sport pilot certificate six months ago and have been
flying solo every weekend. Your spouse asks to come up with you on
Saturday. You haven't flown in 12 days. Are you legal?

**Check the boxes:**

1. Certificate in possession? Yes.
2. Photo ID? Yes.
3. Driver's license valid (or current FAA medical)? Yes.
4. Flight review within 24 months? Yes (you got the cert 6 months ago,
   and the practical test counts as a flight review).
5. 90-day passenger currency: 3 takeoffs and 3 landings within the last
   90 days, sole manipulator, in a PPC? You've been flying every
   weekend for 6 months — yes.

**Verdict**: Legal to take your spouse flying. Run the IMSAFE check the
morning of the flight, check weather, do the preflight, and have a
great Saturday.

## Worked Scenario 2: The lapsed currency

You finished training in March. April through June you flew often,
including with your spouse. July, August, September you didn't fly at
all. October 2 you finally have a clear weekend. Your sister flies in
from out of state and asks for a sightseeing flight. Legal?

**Check the boxes:**

1. Last passenger-eligible flight: late June, ~95 days ago.
2. 90-day currency: **lapsed**.
3. You may fly solo today, but you must do at least 3 takeoffs and 3
   landings before carrying your sister.

**The right move**: take the morning to fly solo, log 3 takeoffs and 3
landings, then bring her up after lunch. You don't need an instructor;
you don't need to re-take any test; you just need to refresh your
currency.

## Worked Scenario 3: The Class D request

You've earned your sport pilot certificate (with the PPC rating) but
you don't have the Class B/C/D airspace endorsement. A friend's
birthday party is at a small Class D airport an hour's drive away.
You'd like to fly in. Legal?

**Without the endorsement**: no. Class D operations require the § 61.325
endorsement. Driving is your option this weekend.

**To fix it**: schedule 2–3 hours with a CFI for ground (radio
procedures, airspace boundaries, pattern entry) and a flight or two
through a Class C or D airport. Get the endorsement, then go fly to
the party.

## Section 11: Putting It All Together

The sport pilot certificate is the simplest legal pathway into two-
place powered parachutes. It requires modest training, a manageable
written and practical test, and a small set of recurring obligations
to maintain. The privilege list is narrower than a private pilot's,
deliberately — the FAA traded scope for accessibility.

Your job, once certified, is twofold:

1. **Stay current.** 90-day passenger currency, 24-month flight review,
   address-change reporting, ongoing IMSAFE self-assessment.
2. **Stay within your privileges.** Day, VFR, single passenger, no
   compensation, no business use. When you want to expand —
   make-and-model endorsements, controlled-airspace endorsements,
   eventually a private pilot certificate — the path is well-marked.

Most sport-pilot violations are not dramatic. They are calendar
oversights — a flight review that lapsed by a week, a 91-day gap
since the last takeoff and landing. The remedy is also small: a few
takeoffs and landings, an hour with a CFI, and you're back in the
clean column. The pilots who get into real trouble are usually the
ones who push edges (carrying passengers without currency, flying
business trips, flying at night) on the assumption that no one will
notice. Sometimes no one does. When something happens, the FAA's
investigation reads logbooks line by line.

The recurring theme of this whole certification framework is *you are
the safety system*. There is no medical examiner watching you, no
dispatcher second-guessing your weather call, no co-pilot to catch a
slipped checklist item. You are it. The certificate is only the
beginning of that responsibility.
