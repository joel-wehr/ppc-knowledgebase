# Emergency Procedures for Powered Parachutes
## Preparing for and Responding to Emergencies

---

## Introduction

Emergency procedures are the skills and knowledge needed when something goes wrong. While powered parachutes are generally safe and forgiving aircraft, pilots must be prepared for emergencies and know how to respond correctly.

**Key Principles**:
1. **Stay Calm**: Panic causes poor decisions
2. **Fly the Aircraft**: Maintain control first
3. **Analyze the Situation**: What's wrong?
4. **Take Appropriate Action**: Use trained responses
5. **Land as Soon as Practical**: Get on the ground safely

**Remember**: Most emergencies are survivable if handled correctly. Training and preparation make the difference.

---

## Emergency Mindset and Preparation

### Mental Preparation

**Before Every Flight**:
- Identify emergency landing areas
- Plan escape routes
- Review emergency procedures mentally
- Know your aircraft's capabilities
- Accept that emergencies CAN happen

**Continuous Awareness**:
- Always know where you can land
- Maintain safe altitude (within glide distance of fields)
- Stay oriented (know your position)
- Monitor aircraft systems
- Watch for changing conditions

### Emergency Equipment

**Minimum Equipment**:
- [ ] Cell phone (charged, accessible)
- [ ] First aid kit
- [ ] Fire extinguisher
- [ ] Emergency contact information
- [ ] Personal locator beacon (recommended for remote areas)

**Optional but Recommended**:
- [ ] Emergency locator transmitter (ELT)
- [ ] Handheld radio
- [ ] Survival kit (if flying over remote areas)
- [ ] Water and basic supplies

## Engine Failures

### Complete Engine Failure

**Immediate Actions**:
1. **Fly the Aircraft**: Maintain control, don't panic
2. **Establish Best Glide**: Trim for best glide speed (~25 mph)
3. **Select Landing Area**: Choose best available field
4. **Attempt Restart** (if altitude permits):
   - Check mag switches ON
   - Check fuel
   - Check primer (try priming)
   - Check throttle position
5. **If No Restart**: Commit to emergency landing

**Landing Area Selection**:
**Good Choices**:
- Large open fields (hay, pasture, mowed grass)
- Into the wind
- Level ground
- No obstacles on approach
- Adequate length

**Avoid**:
- Trees, power lines, buildings
- Rough terrain
- Standing water
- Crops (if alternatives available)
- Populated areas

**Planning the Approach**:
1. **Altitude Management**: Don't waste altitude
2. **Pattern**: Plan standard pattern if possible
3. **Into Wind**: Always land into wind if possible
4. **Approach Path**: Clear of obstacles
5. **Commit**: Once committed, complete the landing

**Final Approach**:
- Maintain safe airspeed
- Use flare to minimize impact
- Full flare just before touchdown
- Collapse wing after touchdown
- Evacuate if necessary

**After Landing**:
1. **Secure Aircraft**: Mags off, master off
2. **Assess Injuries**: Check yourself and passengers
3. **Evacuate if Needed**: Fire risk, fuel leak
4. **Call for Help**: Use cell phone
5. **Secure Scene**: Prevent further hazards
6. **Notify Authorities**: Per NTSB requirements if needed

### Partial Power Loss

**Recognition**:
- RPM drops but engine still running
- Reduced climb performance
- Difficulty maintaining altitude

**Immediate Actions**:
1. **Maintain Control**: Fly the aircraft
2. **Assess Severity**: How much power remains?
3. **Troubleshoot**:
   - Check mixture (carb heat/adjustment)
   - Check for obstructions (air filter, exhaust)
   - Check fuel (switch tanks if applicable)
   - Check mags (try switching)
4. **Decision**:
   - If adequate power: Land at airport
   - If inadequate power: Land immediately

**Landing with Partial Power**:
- Choose field conservatively
- Don't stretch glide
- Accept that landing is necessary
- Use power available to control approach
- Be prepared for full engine failure

### Engine Failure on Takeoff

**Most Critical Emergency**: Low altitude, high risk

**Before Liftoff** (Wing inflating, rolling):
1. **Throttle Closed**: Immediately
2. **Mags Off**: Turn off both mags
3. **Steering Forward**: Collapse wing
4. **Brake**: Stop aircraft
5. **Assess**: Determine problem

**Just After Liftoff** (0-50 feet AGL):
1. **Land Straight Ahead**: Don't turn!
2. **Maintain Control**: Keep wing overhead
3. **Flare**: Normal landing flare
4. **Accept Landing**: Might be rough
5. **Collapse Wing**: After touchdown

**Higher Altitude** (50+ feet AGL):
1. **Fly the Aircraft**: Maintain control
2. **Land Ahead**: Slight turns OK if needed
3. **Best Available Field**: Choose quickly
4. **Standard Approach**: If possible
5. **Commit**: Complete the landing

**NEVER**:
- Don't try to return to takeoff area (turn back)
- Don't stretch glide (maintain airspeed)
- Don't make steep turns at low altitude

## In-Flight Emergencies

### Wing Collapse

**Causes**:
- Severe turbulence
- Improper control inputs
- Damaged wing
- Extreme maneuvers

**Types**:

#### **Partial Collapse** (Some cells deflated):
**Response**:
1. **Release Brakes**: Let go of steering
2. **Allow Recovery**: Wing will usually reinflate
3. **Don't Fight It**: Don't make aggressive inputs
4. **Maintain Airspeed**: Keep flying speed
5. **Land Soon**: As soon as practical

#### **Full Collapse** (Entire wing deflated):
**Response**:
1. **Release Brakes**: Immediately
2. **Full Forward Steering**: May help reinflate
3. **Wait**: Allow time to reinflate (may take several seconds)
4. **If No Reinflate**: Prepare for impact
5. **Protection**: Assume crash position

**Prevention**:
- Avoid turbulent conditions
- Smooth control inputs
- Proper wing maintenance
- Stay within operating limits
- Avoid extreme maneuvers

### Line Break or Riser Failure

**Recognition**:
- Sudden pulling to one side
- Uncontrolled turn
- Asymmetric wing shape

**Response**:
1. **Counteract**: Apply opposite steering
2. **Assess**: Can you maintain control?
3. **Controlled Descent**: Plan landing immediately
4. **Spiral In**: May need to spiral down
5. **Flare on Good Side**: Use functioning side to slow

**Landing**:
- Choose area quickly
- Accept that landing will be difficult
- Protect yourself
- Flare as able
- Brace for impact

### Steering Line Failure

**Recognition**:
- No response from one steering line
- Steering line loose or broken

**Response**:
1. **Weight Shift**: Use body weight to turn
2. **Throttle Steering**: Power changes affect direction slightly
3. **Asymmetric Trim**: May help if adjustable
4. **Plan Landing**: As soon as practical
5. **Straight-In Approach**: Minimize turns

**Landing**:
- Straight final approach
- May not be able to flare fully
- Accept firmer touchdown
- Collapse wing with working side

### Uncontrollable Spin

**Causes**:
- Excessive steering input
- Stall combined with control input
- Wing damage

**Response**:
1. **Release Brakes**: Let go of all steering inputs
2. **Neutral**: Return steering to neutral
3. **Wait**: Allow aircraft to recover
4. **Gentle Input**: When recovered, gently resume control
5. **Land Soon**: Don't continue normal flight

**Prevention**:
- Avoid aggressive steering inputs
- Maintain adequate airspeed
- Coordinated control inputs
- Stay within envelope

## Equipment Failures

### Fuel System Problems

#### **Fuel Starvation**:
**Symptoms**:
- Engine running rough
- Power loss
- Eventually engine stops

**Causes**:
- Out of fuel
- Fuel line blocked
- Fuel pump failure
- Water in fuel

**Response**:
1. **Switch Tanks** (if applicable)
2. **Check Fuel Selector** (if applicable)
3. **Prime**: Try primer
4. **If Continues**: Treat as engine failure
5. **Land Immediately**

#### **Fuel Leak**:
**Recognition**:
- Smell of fuel
- Visible fuel
- Dropping fuel quantity

**Response**:
1. **Turn Off Fuel** (if valve accessible)
2. **Land Immediately**
3. **Evacuate After Landing**
4. **Fire Risk**: Be prepared
5. **No Smoking**: Keep ignition sources away

### Electrical System Failure

**Recognition**:
- Instruments fail
- Low voltage indication
- Battery warning light (if equipped)

**Response**:
1. **Don't Panic**: Engine will continue running (mag ignition)
2. **Reduce Load**: Turn off unnecessary electrical items
3. **Navigate by Landmarks**: GPS may fail
4. **Land Soon**: Before complete loss

**Implications**:
- Can't restart engine if shut down
- Loss of instruments (if electric)
- Loss of lighting (if at twilight)
- Loss of communication

### Mag Switch Failure

**Recognition**:
- One ignition not functioning
- Discovered during mag check

**Response**:
1. **Don't Fly**: If discovered before flight
2. **If In Flight**: Land as soon as practical
3. **Monitor Closely**: Watch for complete failure
4. **Smooth Operation**: Avoid stressing engine

**Note**: Engine will run on one mag but:
- Reduced power
- Rougher operation
- Increased risk of complete failure

## Weather Emergencies

### Unexpected Weather Deterioration

**Situations**:
- Clouds lowering
- Visibility decreasing
- Wind increasing
- Weather approaching

**Response**:
1. **Land Immediately**: Don't wait
2. **Choose Any Safe Area**: Field, even rough areas better than continuing
3. **Don't Push Limits**: Legal minimums are minimums, not goals
4. **Call for Ride**: Arrange pickup after landing safely

**Prevention**:
- Check weather before flight
- Monitor conditions continuously
- Turn back early if conditions worsen
- Don't rely on forecast being accurate

### Flying Into IMC (Clouds/Fog)

**Immediate Actions**:
1. **Turn Back**: 180° turn immediately
2. **Descend** (if safe): May be clear below
3. **Fly Level**: Don't try to climb through
4. **Slow Down**: Reduce to safe speed
5. **Stay Calm**: Focus on control

**If Can't Escape**:
1. **Maintain Control**: Focus on keeping wings level
2. **Steady Power**: Don't make rapid changes
3. **Gentle Inputs**: Smooth control
4. **Break Out**: Eventually you'll descend to ground
5. **Land Carefully**: Visibility may still be limited

**Prevention**:
- Never deliberately enter clouds
- Turn back at first sign of deteriorating visibility
- Stay well clear of clouds
- Respect cloud clearance requirements

### Severe Turbulence

**Response**:
1. **Slow Down**: Reduce to safe maneuvering speed
2. **Gentle Inputs**: Don't fight small bumps
3. **Maintain Control**: Keep wings relatively level
4. **Land Immediately**: Don't continue
5. **Choose Area**: Find area with better conditions or just land

**Protection**:
- Tighten seat belt
- Secure loose items
- Protect head if severe

### Thunderstorm Encounter

**If Near Thunderstorm**:
1. **Turn Away**: 180° turn immediately
2. **Maximum Distance**: Put as much distance between you and storm as possible
3. **Land Immediately**: Don't try to outrun it
4. **Any Field**: Accept any landing area
5. **Evacuate If Needed**: After landing, seek shelter

**If Caught in Storm**:
- Unlikely to survive
- Extreme turbulence
- Possible structural failure
- Lightning risk
- Try to maintain control
- Survive any way possible

**Prevention**: Never fly near thunderstorms (20+ mile minimum)

## Structural Failures

### Propeller Failure

**Indications**:
- Loud bang or vibration
- Loss of thrust
- Unusual sounds

**Response**:
1. **Mags Off**: Shut down engine immediately
2. **Assess Damage**: Check behind if safe
3. **Fly the Wing**: Glide to landing
4. **Choose Field**: Best available
5. **Standard Approach**: Normal landing procedure

### Frame Damage

**Causes**:
- Hard landing
- Ground collision
- Structural failure

**Recognition**:
- Visual observation
- Unusual sounds
- Change in aircraft handling

**Response**:
1. **Assess Severity**: Can you maintain control?
2. **Gentle Inputs**: Don't stress structure further
3. **Land Immediately**: First available field
4. **Slow Approach**: Minimize loads on structure

## Fire Emergencies

### Engine Fire

**On Ground**:
1. **Mags Off**: Shut down immediately
2. **Evacuate**: Get away from aircraft
3. **Extinguisher**: If safe to approach
4. **Call 911**: Report fire
5. **Stay Clear**: Fuel tank can explode

**In Flight**:
1. **Mags Off**: Shut down engine
2. **Dive**: Increase airspeed (may blow out fire)
3. **Land Immediately**: Emergency landing
4. **Evacuate**: As soon as stopped
5. **Distance**: Get away from aircraft

### Electrical Fire

**Recognition**:
- Smoke from electrical components
- Burning smell
- Sparks

**Response**:
1. **Master Off**: Kill electrical power
2. **Ventilate**: If smoke present
3. **Land Soon**: As soon as practical
4. **Inspect After Landing**: Determine source

## Collision Avoidance

### Near Miss or Conflict

**If Another Aircraft Appears**:
1. **See and Avoid**: Continuously scan
2. **Right-of-Way Rules**: Know and follow
3. **Defensive Action**: Don't rely on other pilot seeing you
4. **Communicate**: If radio equipped
5. **Report**: Consider reporting near miss

**Right-of-Way Hierarchy**:
1. Aircraft in distress (highest)
2. Balloons
3. Gliders
4. Airships
5. All aircraft on final approach
6. Certificated aircraft
7. Powered ultralights/PPCs
8. Unpowered ultralights

**Convergence Rules**:
- Aircraft on right has right-of-way
- Overtaking: Pass on right
- Head-on: Both turn right

### Bird Strike

**Prevention**:
- Scan for birds
- Avoid flocks
- Climb/descend/turn to avoid
- Slow if birds present

**If Strike Occurs**:
1. **Maintain Control**: Fly the aircraft
2. **Assess Damage**: Engine? Wing? Structure?
3. **Land Soon**: If any damage suspected
4. **Inspect**: Complete inspection before next flight

## Forced Landing Procedures

### Choosing a Landing Site

**Priority Order**:
1. **Airport**: If reachable
2. **Large Open Field**: Hay, pasture, mowed
3. **Smaller Field**: If needed
4. **Rough Field**: Better than obstacles
5. **Trees**: Absolute last resort (slow, controlled entry into treetops)

**Surface Evaluation**:
- Tall grass: Usually OK
- Short grass/pasture: Good
- Plowed field: Rough but usually survivable
- Crop field: May be OK (avoid corn if possible)
- Rough terrain: High risk
- Water: Risky (swamped by wing)
- Trees: Very high risk

### Emergency Landing Technique

**Approach**:
1. **Choose Field**: Best available
2. **Plan Pattern**: Try for normal approach
3. **Into Wind**: If possible
4. **Avoid Obstacles**: Clear all obstacles
5. **Stabilized Final**: Normal approach speed

**Touchdown**:
1. **Normal Flare**: Use full flare
2. **Main Wheels First**: Normal touchdown point
3. **Collapse Wing**: Immediately
4. **Straight**: Maintain directional control
5. **Stop**: Allow aircraft to stop

**After Landing**:
1. **Secure**: Mags off, master off
2. **Assess**: Check for injuries
3. **Evacuate**: If fire risk
4. **Cell Phone**: Call for help
5. **Wait**: Stay with aircraft if safe

## Passenger Emergencies

### Passenger Illness or Incapacitation

**Response**:
1. **Assess**: Determine severity
2. **Land Soon**: As soon as practical
3. **Call Ahead** (if able): Request medical assistance
4. **Safe Landing**: Fly safely, don't rush
5. **After Landing**: Provide first aid, call 911

### Passenger Panic

**Prevention**:
- Thorough pre-flight briefing
- Explain sensations
- Build confidence
- Show controls

**If Occurs**:
1. **Stay Calm**: Don't react to panic
2. **Reassure**: Calm, confident voice
3. **Land Soon**: End flight
4. **Safe Landing**: Maintain control
5. **Debrief**: After landing, discuss

## Survival After Forced Landing

### Immediate Actions

1. **Scene Safety**:
   - Fire risk?
   - Fuel leak?
   - Power lines?
   - Traffic?

2. **First Aid**:
   - Check yourself
   - Check passengers
   - Treat injuries
   - Call 911 if needed

3. **Communication**:
   - Cell phone 911
   - ELT if equipped
   - Text location if phone works

4. **Aircraft Security**:
   - Shut off all systems
   - Remove valuables if time
   - Prevent further damage

### If Injured and Alone

1. **First Aid**: Self-treat if able
2. **Stay Warm**: Use aircraft for shelter
3. **Signal**: Use anything bright
4. **Wait**: Rescue will come
5. **Conserve**: Energy, water (if available)

### Remote Area Survival

**If Landing in Remote Area**:
- **Stay with Aircraft**: Easier to spot than person
- **Signal**: Use bright colors, mirror, fire smoke
- **Shelter**: Use aircraft
- **Water**: Priority after safety
- **Food**: Lower priority than water/shelter

## Emergency Communications

### Radio (If Equipped)

**Emergency Frequency**: 121.5 MHz (emergency)

**Mayday Call**:
"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday"
"This is [your callsign], powered parachute"
"[Location]"
"[Nature of emergency]"
"[Intentions]"
"[People on board]"

**Example**:
"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday"
"This is November One Two Three Four Bravo, powered parachute"
"Five miles west of Centerville"
"Engine failure"
"Landing in a field"
"Two people on board"

### Cell Phone

**911 Call**:
- State emergency clearly
- Give location (GPS coordinates if possible)
- Describe aircraft (powered parachute)
- Number of people
- Injuries
- Fire risk
- Stay on line if possible

## Emergency Training and Practice

### Skills to Practice

**With Instructor**:
- Simulated engine failure and landing approaches
- Simulated forced landing patterns
- Slow flight and stall recognition
- Emergency landing site selection
- Aborted takeoff

**Mental Practice**:
- Review procedures regularly
- Visualize responses
- "What if" scenarios during flights
- Identify landing sites every flight

**Don't Practice Alone**:
- Actual engine-off landings
- Stall/spin recovery
- Any dangerous maneuvers

## NTSB Reporting Requirements

### When to Report

**Accidents** (Report immediately):
- Any injury requiring hospitalization over 48 hours (within 7 days)
- Any fatality
- Substantial damage to aircraft

**Incidents** (Report if requested):
- Less serious events
- As requested by NTSB

### How to Report

1. **Call NTSB**: Nearest field office or 800-877-6799
2. **Within 24 Hours**: For accidents
3. **Written Report**: Within 10 days
4. **Preserve Evidence**: Don't move wreckage except for safety

## Summary

**Key Emergency Principles**:

1. **Prevention is Best**: Avoid emergencies through good judgment and maintenance

2. **Stay Current**: Review procedures regularly

3. **Fly the Aircraft**: Maintain control first, always

4. **Practice Decision Making**: Consider "what if" continuously

5. **Know Your Limits**: Don't exceed your capabilities

6. **Training Matters**: Get proper training, practice with instructor

7. **Equipment Helps**: Carry emergency equipment

8. **Plan Ahead**: Always know where you can land

9. **Stay Calm**: Panic is the enemy

10. **Land Soon**: When in doubt, get on the ground

**Remember**: Most emergencies are survivable if handled correctly. The pilot who is prepared, trained, and keeps a calm head will almost always walk away. Your training and judgment are your best emergency equipment.
