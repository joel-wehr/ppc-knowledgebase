# Canopy Malfunctions, Ballistic Reserve, and Water Landings
## PPC-Specific Emergencies the Standard Engine-Out Drill Doesn't Cover

---

## Introduction

The companion lesson (`01_emergency_operations.md`) covers the emergencies
a powered parachute shares with every other aircraft: engine failure,
fire, weather, structural damage, forced landings. This lesson covers
three emergency areas that are **specific to a ram-air canopy and the way
a PPC is built** — and that are the difference between a survivable day
and a fatal one:

1. **Ballistic reserve / BRS** — the whole-aircraft emergency parachute
   many two-place carts carry, and when (and when *not*) to use it.
2. **Canopy malfunctions** — line-overs, collapses, surge/dive,
   pendulum/parachutal stall, and riser twist. These are the failure
   modes unique to the wing, and the right response is rarely obvious.
3. **Water landings (ditching)** — a known PPC fatality mode because the
   wing settles *on top of* the pilot.

**Key Principle**: A PPC has no "glide to the airport" option and no
control surfaces in the airplane sense. When the wing is flying, you
fly the wing. When the wing stops being a wing, your remaining options
are very few — which is exactly why you decide about them *before* the
emergency, not during it.

> **This lesson teaches recognition and decision-making, not a
> substitute for hands-on training.** Canopy recoveries and BRS handling
> must be learned with a qualified PPC instructor and from your specific
> equipment's manuals. Never practice a malfunction deliberately.

---

## Part 1: Ballistic Reserve / BRS

### What a ballistic reserve is

A **ballistic recovery system (BRS)** — also called a ballistic reserve,
emergency parachute, or "the rocket" — is a separate emergency canopy
that lowers the **entire aircraft and occupants** to the ground under a
round reserve parachute. On a PPC it is mounted on the cart (commonly
behind or above the seats) and deployed by a handle.

Two deployment mechanisms exist:

- **Rocket-deployed**: a small solid-fuel rocket pulls the reserve out
  and inflates it fast, even at low altitude and low airspeed. This is
  the most common system on modern two-place carts.
- **Hand-deployed / spring-deployed**: the pilot throws or a spring
  ejects the reserve. Slower to inflate; needs more altitude and some
  airspeed/airflow to open.

Know **which one you have**, where the handle is, how much force it
takes to fire, and its published **minimum deployment altitude**. That
information lives in the BRS manufacturer's manual and the cart POH —
not in this lesson, because it is equipment-specific.

> **Part 103 note**: a ballistic reserve is a *safety device intended
> for deployment in a potentially catastrophic situation*, so its weight
> is **excluded** from the 254-lb Part 103 empty-weight limit (§ 103.1).
> You are never penalized under Part 103 for carrying one.

### When the BRS is the right answer

The BRS is for situations where **the aircraft can no longer fly you to
a survivable landing**. Classic triggers:

- **Structural failure of the wing or cart** — a canopy you cannot
  reinflate, a major line/riser failure that leaves the wing
  uncontrollable, a broken mast or cage.
- **Mid-air collision** or loss of a major component.
- **Unrecoverable spin/spiral** with altitude remaining.
- **Pilot incapacitation** scenarios in some two-place training contexts
  (rare, equipment-dependent).
- **Engine failure or fire over terrain with no survivable landing
  option** (water, dense forest, canyon, city) — *if* you are above the
  system's minimum altitude.

### When the BRS is the WRONG answer

This is just as important. A BRS is **not** a substitute for flying the
aircraft when the wing is still flying.

- **A normal engine failure over a usable field**: fly the wing to a
  controlled landing. A glide landing under your own canopy is almost
  always softer and more controllable than a BRS descent.
- **Below the minimum deployment altitude**: there is not enough height
  for the reserve to open. Continue flying/landing the aircraft. Firing
  a reserve that can't inflate wastes the seconds you needed.
- **When you have a controllable problem**: a recoverable collapse, a
  steering-line failure you can manage with weight shift — handle the
  malfunction; don't pull the handle reflexively.

### The decision, made in advance

Set a personal **"hard deck"** — an altitude at which, if the aircraft
is not flying and recoverable, you pull. Decide it on the ground, brief
it, and commit. In the air there is no time to weigh options; you
execute the rule you already made.

### Deployment technique (concept)

The specific motion is equipment-specific, but the universal elements:

1. **Reduce power / shut down** if a turning prop could foul the reserve
   bridle or you're on fire (your POH dictates the exact sequence).
2. **Look, reach, pull** the handle firmly and fully — these handles are
   deliberately stiff so they don't fire by accident. Use both hands if
   needed. Pull *all the way*.
3. **Assume the body position** your equipment specifies — generally feet
   and knees together, chin tucked, bracing for a firm vertical impact.
   You are now a falling object under a round canopy; you will land with
   roughly the energy of a jump off a modest height.
4. **Protect your head and spine** on touchdown; expect to land hard and
   possibly be dragged if there's wind.
5. **After landing**: secure the aircraft (fuel/ignition off), get clear
   of fire risk, deflate/collect canopy so wind can't drag you, then
   first aid and call for help (see `01_emergency_operations.md`).

### Maintenance and repack

- A BRS has a **finite service life** and a **repack/inspection
  interval** (commonly measured in years — follow the manufacturer's
  exact schedule). An expired or unmaintained reserve may not fire or
  may not open.
- The **rocket charge** also has a replacement interval.
- Have the system inspected and repacked **by an authorized facility**
  on schedule, and log it. A BRS you "have" but never serviced is a
  weight penalty, not a safety device.

## Part 2: Canopy Malfunctions

A PPC's wing is a flexible ram-air canopy held open by airflow and the
weight of the cart hanging beneath it on lines and risers. Most of the
time it is remarkably stable and self-correcting. But it can deform,
collapse, twist, or stop flying — and because it is your *only* lifting
surface, those events are emergencies. The common thread in recovery is
**don't fight a recovering wing, and protect your airspeed/altitude.**

> **This section replaces the thin "line break → spiral in" guidance in
> the companion lesson.** That single line oversimplified a complex and
> dangerous situation; use the recognition-and-response treatment here.

### Asymmetric (side) collapse

**What it is**: one side of the canopy deflates — usually from
turbulence, a sharp control input, or wake. The wing pulls and yaws
toward the collapsed side.

**Recognition**: sudden roll/turn toward one side, a "soft" or fluttering
feel on that side, the cart swinging.

**Response**:

1. **Don't stab the controls.** A ram-air wing wants to reinflate.
2. **Ease off** any input on the collapsed side; small, smooth correction
   on the *flying* side only as needed to check the turn.
3. **Let airspeed rebuild** — the airflow is what reopens the cells.
4. **Don't chase it** with aggressive opposite input, which can stall the
   flying side or induce oscillation.
5. **Land as soon as practical** and inspect before flying again.

### Line-over / cravat

**What it is**: a line lies *over* the top of the canopy (a "line-over")
or a wingtip is sucked into the lines and held there (a "cravat"),
deforming the wing into a partial, often steadily turning, configuration.

**Recognition**: a persistent turn that won't stop with control input, a
visibly distorted canopy, a tip tucked into the lines.

**Response**:

1. **Maintain control authority** with the working side; stop the turn
   from tightening.
2. **Try a firm, smooth pump** of the affected steering line if your
   training/equipment supports it — sometimes this clears a cravat.
3. **If it does not clear** and the aircraft is descending in a
   controllable spiral, **manage the descent** toward the best available
   surface; do not force a flat recovery you can't get.
4. **If the turn is unrecoverable and tightening toward a spiral, and you
   are above the BRS minimum altitude → deploy the reserve.** A
   tightening spiral is exactly what the BRS exists for.

### Canopy surge / dive on power application

**What it is**: an abrupt power increase (or a recovery from reduced
power) can make the canopy **surge forward and dive**, pitching the cart
under the wing — a pendulum effect. Worst case it can lead to the wing
diving in front of the cart.

**Recognition**: the wing pitches forward ahead of you, the nose drops,
sink increases.

**Response**:

1. **Apply power smoothly**, never abruptly — this is mostly a
   *prevention* item. Sharp throttle handling is the cause.
2. If a surge starts, **avoid a second sharp input**; let the pendulum
   damp out as the wing returns overhead.
3. **Brief power changes near the ground** — a surge low on approach has
   no room to recover.

### Pendulum/parachutal stall and deep stall (PIO)

**What it is**: too much brake/steering input (or holding deep brakes)
can slow the wing below flying speed into a **parachutal/deep stall** —
the canopy stops flying forward and descends nearly vertically, mushing.
Pilot-induced oscillation (PIO) is the related trap: chasing a pitch
oscillation with out-of-phase inputs makes it worse.

**Recognition**: loss of forward speed, a "mushy" feel, high sink with
the wing still overhead, controls feeling soft.

**Response**:

1. **Release the brakes / ease the input** to let the wing regain flying
   speed and surge gently back to normal flight.
2. **Do not hold deep brakes** trying to "slow down" — that sustains the
   stall.
3. **Stop chasing oscillations** — neutralize, let it settle, then make
   one smooth correction.
4. **Maintain altitude awareness**: a deep stall sinks fast. If it won't
   recover and you're above the BRS floor, deploy.

### Riser twist

**What it is**: the risers/lines twist (often after a hard turn,
turbulence, or an asymmetric event), reducing or jamming your steering
authority until they untwist.

**Recognition**: steering feels restricted or crossed; you can see the
risers wound.

**Response**:

1. **Reduce power** if a twist plus power is driving an uncommanded turn.
2. **Use weight shift** and whatever steering remains to keep the wing
   tracking straight.
3. **Allow the twist to unwind** as the cart counter-rotates; avoid
   adding input that winds it tighter.
4. **Land straight-in** as soon as practical; minimize turns.

### Line break or steering-line failure (cross-reference)

A broken steering line or risen-side line is covered procedurally in
`01_emergency_operations.md` (weight-shift steering, throttle steering,
straight-in approach). The key addition here: **a major line/riser
failure that leaves the wing uncontrollable is a BRS scenario** if you
have the system and the altitude. "Spiral in and flare on the good side"
is a last resort for *low-altitude* failures with no reserve option — it
is not the preferred plan when a reserve and altitude exist.

### The unifying rules for canopy events

- **A flying wing wants to fly.** Most collapses self-recover if you stop
  fighting and let airspeed rebuild.
- **Altitude is time.** Every recovery technique needs height. Fly with a
  margin; don't maneuver aggressively down low.
- **Smooth, not sharp.** Abrupt control and throttle inputs *cause* most
  of these. Smoothness prevents and recovers them.
- **Know your hard deck.** If the wing is not flying and not recovering
  and you're above the BRS minimum, deploy. Hesitation kills.

*Sources: FAA-H-8083-29 Powered Parachute Flying Handbook (Ch. 1
aerodynamics; abnormal & emergency procedures); BRS/canopy manufacturer
operating manuals. Specific deployment altitudes, forces, and pump
techniques are equipment-specific — follow your POH and instructor.*

## Part 3: Water Landings (Ditching)

### Why water is more dangerous than it looks

A PPC over water has a deadly twist that fixed-wing pilots don't face:
after touchdown, **the canopy keeps flying forward and settles down on
top of the pilot and cart**, in the water, often with lines draping over
you. Pilots have drowned tangled under their own wing in water that was
otherwise survivable. The cart can also flip or sink. "Water: risky" is
an understatement — treat overwater flight as a deliberate risk decision,
not a casual route choice.

### Prevention first

- **Plan around water.** Don't route over open water, large lakes, or
  along coastlines unless you have a reason and a plan. Stay within
  glide of land.
- **If you must overfly water, wear a PFD** (life jacket) appropriate for
  aviation use, and brief egress before launch.
- **Carry a hook knife** accessible on your harness to cut lines if you
  end up under the canopy.
- **Tell someone** your route and time.

### If a water landing is unavoidable

1. **Aim for the shallowest, closest-to-shore water** you can reach;
   land **into the wind** as in any forced landing.
2. **Fly a normal approach and full flare** — you want the slowest
   possible touchdown.
3. **Just before/at touchdown, plan to get the wing AHEAD or to the SIDE
   of you, not on top of you** — release/steer so the canopy collapses
   away from your position as much as the situation allows. (Technique is
   equipment- and wind-dependent; this is a brief-it-beforehand item.)
4. **Brace** for the impact and immersion.

### After touchdown in water

1. **Release the harness/seatbelt immediately** once you've stopped —
   know the buckle by feel, because you may be inverted and disoriented.
2. **Get out from under the canopy.** If lines or fabric are over you,
   **use the hook knife** and follow the lines/risers to clear water.
   Do not thrash; orient by feel to where the surface/light is.
3. **Inflate your PFD once clear** of the wreckage (inflating under fabric
   can trap you against it).
4. **Get clear of the sinking cart**, then signal and call for help.

> **The single most important water-ditching fact for PPC pilots:** the
> wing comes down on top of you. Egress training, a hook knife, and a PFD
> are what turn a survivable water landing into a survived one. If you
> don't carry those, don't fly over water you couldn't glide clear of.

*Sources: FAA-H-8083-29 (emergency procedures); PHAK FAA-H-8083-25C
(survival/ditching considerations); PPC accident-pattern guidance.
Egress and canopy-clearing technique must be trained hands-on.*

## Summary

| Situation | First instinct that's WRONG | What to actually do |
|---|---|---|
| Asymmetric collapse | Stab opposite control | Ease off, let airspeed rebuild, small correction only |
| Line-over / cravat | Force a flat recovery | Pump the line; if unrecoverable spiral and above floor, BRS |
| Surge/dive on power | Add another sharp input | Smooth power; let the pendulum damp |
| Deep/parachutal stall | Hold brakes to "slow down" | Release brakes, regain flying speed |
| Riser twist | Add steering input | Reduce power, weight-shift, let it unwind, land straight |
| Wing unrecoverable, altitude remaining | Try to "save it" all the way down | Deploy BRS above its minimum altitude |
| Engine out over a usable field | Pull the BRS reflexively | Fly the wing to a normal landing |
| Water landing | Inflate PFD before egress; let wing settle on you | Egress first, knife if tangled, PFD once clear |

**Remember**: these are the emergencies that *don't* respond to
"establish best glide and pick a field." They respond to recognition,
smoothness, an altitude margin, a pre-decided BRS hard deck, and — over
water — egress gear and a plan made before you launched.
