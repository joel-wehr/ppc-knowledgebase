# Part 91 — Sport Pilot and Powered Parachute Overlays
## How the Shared Operating Rules Apply to a PPC

---

## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **General Operating Rules (14 CFR Part 91)** chapter covers each Part 91 section — PIC authority, preflight, right-of-way, speed, minimum altitudes, altimeter, fuel, weather minimums, cruising altitudes, documents, alcohol/drugs, and careless operation. Those rules are the same for everyone. This chapter adds the **sport-pilot and PPC-specific overlays** that change how some of them apply to you.

---

## Sport-Pilot Limits That Override Part 91 (§ 61.315)

The sport-pilot operating limits in § 61.315 are often **stricter** than the Part 91 baseline, and the stricter rule wins:

- **Visibility floor (§ 61.315(c)(7))**: a sport pilot may not operate when flight visibility is less than **3 statute miles** — even in Class G, where § 91.155 would allow 1 SM. So the practical sport-pilot floor is **3 SM flight visibility, anywhere, anytime.**
  > **Common misconception**: "Class G means I can fly in 1 mile visibility." True for a Part 103 ultralight; **not** for a sport-pilot LSA — § 61.315 holds you to 3 miles.
- **Altitude cap (§ 61.315(c)(11))**: 10,000 ft MSL or 2,000 ft AGL, whichever is higher. The § 91.155 "above 10,000 MSL" minimums and much of the § 91.159 hemispherical rule are therefore academic for most PPC flying (you're usually below 3,000 AGL, where you may choose any safe altitude). They bind only on a long cross-country in mountainous terrain where 9,500 MSL is just ~1,500 AGL.
- **Speed**: § 61.315(c)(13) caps sport-pilot PPC operations at **87 knots CAS**, well below any § 91.117 limit — so the Part 91 speed limits never bind a PPC in practice.

## Preflight (§ 91.103) for a Wind-Sensitive PPC

The shared § 91.103 pattern applies, with PPC emphasis: check the **winds-aloft forecast** at your planned cruise altitude — PPCs are wind-sensitive, and even forecast wind makes a ~30-minute difference on a 50-mile leg. Calculate fuel as **time-to-destination + 30-minute reserve + contingency**, and compute density-altitude takeoff distance for the day.

---

## Right-of-Way — Don't Conflate § 91.113 with Part 103

A PPC is classified as an **airplane** for right-of-way purposes, so a sport-pilot PPC follows the standard § 91.113 same-category rule (the aircraft on the right has priority).

> **Common misconception**: "Powered parachutes always yield to other aircraft." That's the **Part 103** rule (§ 103.13) for *ultralight vehicles*. A sport-pilot PPC operating as a light-sport aircraft is governed by **§ 91.113**, where standard same-category right-of-way applies. Many PPC pilots conflate the two regimes.

Example: at 1,500 AGL you spot a glider 200 ft above tracking opposite-direction. Per § 91.113(d) the glider (less maneuverable category) has right-of-way over your PPC — alter course to keep clear; don't count on the glider seeing you.

## Fuel (§ 91.151) With a Small PPC Tank

The 30-minute day-VFR reserve applies to every PPC flight, including pattern work. With **5-10 gallon tanks burning 3-5 gph**, fuel is a constant attention point: a 5-gallon tank at 4 gph gives 75 minutes total; subtract the 30-minute reserve and you have **45 minutes of usable flight per fill-up.** Plan accordingly, especially in headwinds.

---

## Documents — Part 103 vs. LSA (§ 91.203 / § 103.7)

The **ARROW** documents (shared chapter) are required when you operate as a **registered LSA** (two-place, airworthiness-certificated), and they must be in the *aircraft*, not the hangar.

For a **Part 103 ultralight PPC**, none of ARROW applies — **§ 103.7 explicitly exempts ultralights** from registration, airworthiness, and operating-limitation requirements. The ARROW rule kicks in the moment you operate as an LSA.

---

## Night (§ 61.329, MOSAIC)

Sport-pilot night flight is now permitted under **§ 61.329** (MOSAIC) with the required training, endorsement, and Part 67 medical *or* BasicMed (not the driver's-license path). If you fly at night, the § 91.151 **45-minute** night fuel reserve and the higher night VFR weather minimums in the shared chapter apply. Get night-qualified and current before planning any night PPC flight.

## Worked Scenario 1: The marginal weather day

Planning a 40-mile PPC cross-country. Departure METAR: 4 SM visibility, BKN030; destination TAF the same. You'll be in Class G under 1,200 AGL the whole way.
- **Visibility**: § 91.155 allows 1 SM here, but sport-pilot § 61.315(c)(7) requires 3 SM. You have 4 SM — legal.
- **Cloud clearance**: Class G under 1,200 AGL requires only "clear of clouds"; with a 3,000 AGL ceiling you're well below — legal.
- **Altitude**: 1,200 AGL is within the § 61.315(c)(11) cap.
- **Verdict**: legal, but § 91.13 (and your gut) may say wait — 4 SM is marginal, and a slow PPC gives you less time to react if visibility drops. Conservative call: wait for 6+ SM. Legal and wise aren't always the same line.

## Worked Scenario 2: Right-of-way at an uncontrolled field

Entering a 45° downwind for runway 27 at a non-towered field, you spot another aircraft on left base for runway 9 (opposite direction) at your altitude. § 91.113(g) gives the lower aircraft priority when landing, but neither of you is landing yet — the runway-in-use ambiguity is the real issue. Announce on CTAF, listen, and deconflict by altitude or by one of you switching runways / extending. Right-of-way is a tiebreaker, not a license to insist on priority into a near-miss.

## Worked Scenario 3: Planning fuel for a leg

A 60-NM leg at 30 mph TAS into a forecast 10 mph headwind; 5-gallon tank, 4 gph.
- **Groundspeed** = 30 - 10 = 20 mph; 60 NM × 1.15 = 69 SM; 69 ÷ 20 = **3.45 hours**.
- **Fuel** = 3.45 × 4 = **13.8 gallons — already over the 5-gallon capacity.**
- **Verdict**: not flyable on this fuel. Plan a fuel stop ~30-35 NM out, or wait for the wind to relax. Don't "stretch it" with reduced power — § 91.151 and § 91.13 both apply.
