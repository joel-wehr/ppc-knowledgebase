# General Operating Rules for Sport Pilots
## Part 91 Topics Every PPC Pilot Should Know in Their Sleep

---

## Introduction

Once you've earned a sport pilot certificate, your day-to-day legal
framework shifts from Part 61 (the certification rules) to Part 91 (the
operating rules). Part 91 covers everything about *how* aircraft operate
in the National Airspace System — right-of-way, fuel reserves, weather
minimums, careless operation, what documents must be on board. Some of
its 200-plus sections are written for jets and IFR operations and will
never apply to a sport-pilot PPC. Others are bedrock and apply to every
flight you'll ever take.

This module focuses on the latter. We'll walk through each Part 91
section that matters for sport-pilot PPC operations, explain *why* it
exists, show *how* it applies in real flying, and call out the common
misconceptions that get pilots into trouble. We'll cite the regulation
numbers so you can pull the verbatim text from the eCFR viewer; the goal
of this chapter is the working knowledge that lets you apply the rules
correctly in the cockpit.

---

## Section 1: Pilot in Command — Authority and Responsibility

§ 91.3 puts the pilot in command (PIC) at the top of the chain. The
language is direct: the PIC is *directly responsible for, and the final
authority as to, the operation of the aircraft.* Every other rule in
Part 91 sits inside that frame.

Three practical implications:

**First, you can't delegate the decision.** Your CFI, your friend in the
right seat, the airport manager, an FAA inspector on the ramp — none of
them can override your judgment as PIC about whether to fly. Their advice
is welcome; the call is yours. Conversely, you cannot defer responsibility
either. "My instructor said it was fine" is not a defense if you, as PIC,
flew into conditions you should have refused.

**Second, in an emergency you may deviate from any rule of Part 91 to
the extent required to handle it.** § 91.3(b). If declaring an emergency
gets you faster vectors through Class B without the normal clearance
process, that is acceptable. After the fact, the FAA may ask for a
written report (§ 91.3(c)) — file it candidly, focusing on what you saw
and what you decided.

**Third, "responsibility" is not just legal — it's operational.** When
a passenger asks "can we fly closer to my house?" and your gut says no,
the answer is no. When the wind is gusting harder than your trim
airspeed, you don't take off because someone drove an hour to watch.
The certificate gives you the authority. § 91.3 reminds you the
authority comes with the obligation to use it.

## Section 2: Preflight Action

§ 91.103 lists what the PIC must become familiar with before each flight.
For a typical VFR PPC flight, this includes:

- Weather reports and forecasts
- Fuel requirements
- Alternates available if the flight cannot be completed as planned
- Any known traffic delays
- Runway lengths at airports of intended use
- Takeoff and landing distance information

The phrasing of the rule — "all available information" — is broader than
the bullet list. NOTAMs (Notices to Air Missions) covering runway
closures, TFRs, and lighting outages are part of that, even though they
don't appear by name in the section. Modern EFBs (ForeFlight, Garmin
Pilot) bundle the relevant data; the FAA's free FSS service via 1-800-
WX-BRIEF or 1800wxbrief.com does the same.

> **Common misconception**: "Preflight action means walking around the
> aircraft." That's the *preflight inspection* (§ 91.7 and the POH).
> § 91.103 is the *information* preflight: weather, fuel, alternates,
> NOTAMs, charts. Both are required. The walk-around without the
> information review is incomplete.

A working preflight pattern that satisfies § 91.103 for a sport-pilot
PPC flight:

1. Pull METARs and TAFs for departure, destination, and any en-route
   point you'd consider as an emergency stop
2. Check the wind aloft forecast at your planned cruise altitude (PPCs
   are wind-sensitive — even forecast wind makes a 30-minute difference
   on a 50-mile leg)
3. Check NOTAMs for departure, destination, and any controlled airspace
   you'll cross
4. Calculate fuel: time-to-destination + 30-minute reserve + contingency
5. Identify a divert airport for each leg
6. Compute weight & balance and density-altitude takeoff distance for
   today's conditions

Skipping any of these is a violation of § 91.103, even if no harm
results. Most enforcement actions for "inadequate preflight" follow an
incident — a runway-overrun, a forced landing for fuel — and the
investigators reconstruct what the pilot didn't check.

## Section 3: Right-of-Way Rules

§ 91.113 is the core mid-air-collision-prevention rule. It establishes
who yields to whom in any traffic-conflict situation, regardless of
whether either pilot is talking to ATC.

The rules apply in this priority order:

1. **An aircraft in distress has the right-of-way over all other air
   traffic.** A Mayday call (or visible emergency) overrides every other
   priority. § 91.113(c).

2. **When aircraft of *different categories* are converging at
   approximately the same altitude:** the order from highest priority
   to lowest is balloon, glider, airship, then airplane/rotorcraft.
   The intent is to give right-of-way to the less maneuverable category.
   § 91.113(d).

3. **When aircraft of the *same category* are converging at
   approximately the same altitude:** the aircraft on the *other's
   right* has the right of way. (Easy memory aid: "right has right.")

4. **In a head-on or near-head-on convergence:** *both* pilots alter
   course to the right. § 91.113(e). Neither has priority — the rule is
   that both move out of each other's path simultaneously.

5. **When one aircraft is overtaking another:** the aircraft being
   overtaken has the right of way. The overtaking aircraft alters
   course to the *right* to pass. § 91.113(f).

6. **On final approach or while landing:** the aircraft at the lower
   altitude has the right of way, but it shall not take advantage of
   that priority to cut in front of another aircraft on final or
   overtake it. § 91.113(g).

A practical PPC example: you're on a sightseeing flight at 1,500 AGL,
and you spot a glider 200 feet above you tracking opposite-direction.
Per § 91.113(d), the glider has right-of-way over your PPC (PPCs are
classified as airplanes for right-of-way purposes — same category as
fixed-wing airplanes for this rule). Your action is to alter course to
keep clear, not to count on the glider seeing you.

> **Common misconception**: "Powered parachutes always yield to other
> aircraft." That's a Part 103 rule (§ 103.13) for ultralight vehicles.
> A sport-pilot PPC operating as a light-sport aircraft is governed by
> § 91.113, where the standard same-category right-of-way rules apply
> (the aircraft on the right has priority). Many PPC pilots conflate
> the two regimes.

The deeper principle: § 91.113 also says, "When weather conditions
permit, regardless of whether an operation is conducted under
instrument flight rules or visual flight rules, vigilance shall be
maintained by each person operating an aircraft so as to see and avoid
other aircraft." See-and-avoid is the underlying responsibility. The
right-of-way rules are tiebreakers when both pilots see each other.

## Section 4: Speed Limits

§ 91.117 lays out maximum indicated airspeeds. PPCs cruising in the
30-knot range will never approach any of these in normal flight, but
they're worth knowing because they form the framework for thinking
about busy airspace:

- **Below 10,000 ft MSL**: 250 knots indicated airspeed (§ 91.117(a))
- **Below 2,500 ft AGL within 4 NM of a Class C or D primary airport**:
  200 knots indicated airspeed (§ 91.117(b))
- **In Class B airspace, or in a VFR corridor through Class B, or
  underneath the lateral limits of Class B**: 200 knots indicated
  airspeed (§ 91.117(c))

The *underneath Class B* rule is where most general aviation pilots
encounter it. If you're flying VFR at 1,500 feet under the floor of an
overlying Class B shelf, your speed limit is 200 knots, not 250.

For PPC pilots, the speed limit you have to actually watch is your
*own*: § 61.315(c)(13) caps sport-pilot PPC operations at 87 knots CAS,
which is well below any Part 91 limit anyway.

## Section 5: Minimum Safe Altitudes

§ 91.119 governs how low you can legally fly. For PPCs operating low
and slow, this is one of the most important Part 91 sections.

**Anywhere**: never operate so low that, in an engine failure, you
could not make an emergency landing without "undue hazard to persons or
property on the surface." § 91.119(a). This is the catch-all — even if
you meet the specific altitude minimums below, you can still violate
§ 91.119(a) if your low flight wouldn't allow a safe forced landing.

**Over a congested area** (city, town, settlement, open-air assembly):
1,000 feet above the highest obstacle within a horizontal radius of
2,000 feet. § 91.119(b).

**Over open water or sparsely populated areas**: 500 feet from any
person, vessel, vehicle, or structure. § 91.119(c).

**Other areas**: no specific minimum altitude required, but § 91.119(a)
still applies — you must be high enough that an emergency landing is
feasible.

For typical PPC pilots flying recreationally over rural fields and
back-country, § 91.119(c) is the usual rule. You can fly at 500 feet
AGL over open farmland; you must climb to higher altitudes when
crossing populated areas; and you should always have a plan for where
you'd land if the engine quit at this exact moment.

> **Common misconception**: "PPCs are low and slow, so the altitude
> rules don't really apply to us." § 91.119 applies to *every* aircraft,
> including PPCs. The rule isn't about category — it's about not
> creating hazards on the surface. A PPC overflying a soccer game at
> 300 feet is just as much in violation as a Cessna would be.

## Section 6: Altimeter Settings

§ 91.121 specifies how the altimeter is set:

- **Below 18,000 feet MSL**: set to the current local altimeter setting
  of a station within 100 nautical miles, or to airport elevation if
  no nearby station is reporting.
- **At or above 18,000 feet MSL** (Class A): set to 29.92 inHg.

For sport-pilot PPCs operating below 10,000 MSL or 2,000 AGL, only the
local-setting rule applies. The "29.92 always" mode is reserved for
Class A IFR operations you can't legally enter as a sport pilot.

How to get the local setting:

- ATIS at a towered airport
- AWOS or ASOS broadcast at most non-towered airports
- ATC if you're talking to a controller
- Flight Service via 122.2 or 1-800-WX-BRIEF

Why it matters: the altimeter measures pressure, not altitude. As
weather systems move through, sea-level pressure changes, and an
altimeter set yesterday will read incorrectly today. A 0.10 inHg error
≈ 100 feet of altitude error — small enough to not matter for most VFR
flying, large enough to matter for instrument approaches and for
clearing terrain you'd otherwise just barely miss.

Routine practice: set the altimeter to the field elevation before
takeoff (sanity check), confirm with the local setting from ATIS or
AWOS, and update at least every 100 NM or whenever you cross a
significant pressure boundary.

## Section 7: Fuel Requirements

§ 91.151 specifies the minimum fuel reserves for VFR flight:

- **Day VFR**: enough fuel to fly to the first point of intended
  landing, plus 30 minutes at normal cruise consumption.
- **Night VFR**: enough fuel to fly to the first point of intended
  landing, plus 45 minutes at normal cruise consumption.

Sport pilots can't fly at night, so the day-VFR 30-minute reserve is
the operative number.

Two important framing points:

**This is a regulatory minimum, not a planning target.** A pilot who
plans to land with exactly 30 minutes' reserve is one unforecast
headwind away from a violation, or worse, a forced landing. Best
practice is to plan for 45 minutes to 1 hour of reserve and treat the
30-minute number as the line you never want to cross.

**"First point of intended landing"** is the destination of the *current
leg*, not the final destination of a multi-leg trip. If you're flying
from A to B to C, the rule applies to leg A→B (you must have fuel to
reach B + 30 min). At B you refuel, then leg B→C resets the clock.

For PPCs with 5–10 gallon tanks burning 3–5 gallons per hour, fuel is a
constant attention point. A 5-gallon tank at 4 gph gives 75 minutes of
flight; subtract a 30-minute reserve and you have 45 minutes of usable
flight time per fill-up. Plan accordingly, especially in headwinds.

> **Common misconception**: "If I'm only flying patterns at my home
> field, the 30-minute reserve doesn't apply." It applies to every
> flight, including pattern work. The "first point of intended landing"
> if you're staying in the pattern is your home field — but you still
> need the fuel margin if anything goes sideways. Pattern fuel
> exhaustion forces a real emergency landing on a real surface.

## Section 8: VFR Weather Minimums

§ 91.155 sets the visibility and cloud-clearance requirements for VFR
flight. The numbers depend on the airspace class and altitude. Sport
pilots operate almost entirely in Classes G and E below 10,000 feet
MSL, so those are the ones to know cold.

**Class G airspace, day, less than 1,200 ft AGL**:
1 statute mile visibility, clear of clouds.

**Class G airspace, day, more than 1,200 ft AGL but below 10,000 ft MSL**:
1 statute mile visibility, 500 ft below clouds, 1,000 ft above clouds,
2,000 ft horizontal from clouds.

**Class E airspace, day, below 10,000 ft MSL**:
3 statute miles visibility, 500 ft below, 1,000 ft above, 2,000 ft
horizontal. (Often memorized as the "5-1-2" rule for cloud clearance,
or the "3-152" rule for the full set.)

**Class E or G, day, above 10,000 ft MSL**:
5 statute miles visibility, 1,000 ft below, 1,000 ft above, 1 statute
mile horizontal.

Sport pilots can't be above 10,000 MSL anyway (§ 61.315(c)(11)), so the
"above 10,000" rules are academic for us.

A sport-pilot-specific overlay (§ 61.315(c)(7)): regardless of
airspace, sport pilots may not operate when flight visibility is less
than 3 statute miles — even in Class G where § 91.155 would allow 1.
The sport-pilot rule is more restrictive and wins.

So the practical sport-pilot floor is **3 statute miles flight
visibility, anywhere, anytime**.

> **Common misconception**: "Class G means I can fly in 1 mile
> visibility." For a Part 103 ultralight, yes. For a sport-pilot LSA,
> no — § 61.315 holds you to 3 miles even in Class G.

The cloud-clearance rules exist to give VFR and IFR aircraft enough
buffer to see and avoid each other in marginal conditions. Cutting it
close to the minimums on a low-cloud day puts you in the same
two-statute-mile cylinder where IFR traffic is breaking out of cloud
on instrument approaches. Stay generous.

## Section 9: VFR Cruising Altitudes

§ 91.159 sets the hemispherical altitude rule. Above 3,000 feet AGL,
VFR cruising altitude is determined by your magnetic course:

- **Magnetic course 0° to 179°** (eastbound): odd thousands plus 500
  (e.g., 3,500, 5,500, 7,500 feet MSL)
- **Magnetic course 180° to 359°** (westbound): even thousands plus 500
  (e.g., 4,500, 6,500, 8,500 feet MSL)

The rule prevents head-on conflicts: aircraft flying opposite directions
naturally end up at altitudes 1,000 feet apart.

Sport pilots are capped at 10,000 MSL or 2,000 AGL whichever is
higher (§ 61.315(c)(11)). For most sport-pilot flying — under 2,000 AGL
or in flat-land where 10,000 MSL = 9,500 AGL or so — the
hemispherical rule rarely binds. But on a long cross-country in
mountainous terrain where 9,500 MSL is just 1,500 AGL, you're playing
by the cruising-altitude rule.

The rule applies above 3,000 AGL. Below that altitude (which is
where most PPC flights happen), you choose any altitude that's safe.

## Section 10: Required Documents — ARROW

Every powered LSA in flight must carry certain documents. The mnemonic
**ARROW** captures most of them:

- **A**irworthiness Certificate (§ 91.203(a)(1))
- **R**egistration Certificate (§ 91.203(a)(2))
- **R**adio Station License (§ 91.203(a) — only for international
  operations; not required for U.S. domestic flight)
- **O**perating Limitations (§ 91.9 — POH or placards)
- **W**eight and Balance data (current)

The airworthiness and registration certificates must be displayed
where they can be seen by passengers or crew. Operating limitations
must be aboard and current. Weight and balance data (a recent
calculation showing the loaded aircraft is within limits) must be
aboard.

For Part 103 ultralight PPCs, none of this applies — § 103.7 explicitly
exempts ultralights from registration, airworthiness, and operating
limitation requirements. The ARROW rule kicks in the moment you
operate as an LSA (two-place, registered, with an airworthiness
certificate).

> **Common misconception**: "I keep all my documents in my hangar." If
> you're flying a registered LSA, the ARROW documents must be in the
> *aircraft*, not the hangar. Many ramp checks have caught pilots who
> assumed otherwise.

## Section 11: Alcohol, Drugs, and Medication

§ 91.17 is one of the most strictly enforced regulations in aviation.
The rule prohibits acting as a crewmember:

- Within **8 hours** of having consumed alcohol
- With a blood alcohol concentration of **0.04% or higher**
- While "under the influence" of alcohol
- While using any drug that affects faculties in a way contrary to
  safety

The three thresholds are independent and additive — meeting any one
disqualifies you. The "8 hours bottle to throttle" rule is the most
commonly cited, but it's the floor, not the ceiling. A pilot with a
high tolerance might still be impaired 10 hours after drinking; the
8-hour rule is a regulatory minimum, not a guarantee of fitness.

§ 61.53 ties this back to the certificate: you may not act as PIC, or
exercise the privileges of any pilot certificate, while you have a
known medical condition (or are taking medication) that would make you
unable to meet medical certification requirements *or* operate the
aircraft safely. This applies to sport pilots even though they don't
hold a medical certificate — the test is whether the condition would
*prevent* certification, not whether you've actually been examined.

Practical applications:

- **Sleeping pills the night before**: many over-the-counter sleep aids
  (diphenhydramine, doxylamine) impair pilot performance for 12+ hours.
  Don't fly the next morning.
- **Decongestants for a head cold**: pseudoephedrine is generally
  acceptable; antihistamine-decongestant combos with first-generation
  antihistamines (e.g., Benadryl) are not.
- **Antibiotics for a routine infection**: usually fine, but the
  underlying illness might not be — see § 61.53.
- **Cannabis**: federally illegal and prohibited regardless of state
  legalization. The FAA position has not changed.

> **Common misconception**: "I'm under the FAA limit, so I'm legal."
> The 0.04% BAC limit is one of three independent tests. You might be
> under 0.04% but still within 8 hours of consumption (violation), or
> still "under the influence" by the broader definition (also a
> violation). The safest rule is full sobriety for at least 12 hours
> before flying — the regulation is the floor, not the target.

## Section 12: Careless or Reckless Operation

§ 91.13 prohibits operating an aircraft "in a careless or reckless
manner so as to endanger the life or property of another." It's one of
the most-cited sections in FAA enforcement actions, often paired with
a more specific violation as a catch-all.

What's "careless"? Anything that a reasonable, prudent pilot wouldn't
do. Examples that have generated § 91.13 actions:

- Buzzing a friend's house at low altitude
- Taking off downwind without a compelling reason
- Carrying a passenger when not 90-day current
- Operating with a known mechanical issue
- Continuing VFR into deteriorating weather

The rule is deliberately broad. The FAA reads it expansively because
it has to cover behavior that doesn't fit a more specific section but
clearly falls below the standard expected of a pilot.

If you find yourself rationalizing a flight ("I'm only going up for ten
minutes, the wind is just a little above my comfort level..."), § 91.13
is the rule that hangs over your decision. Pilots rarely get cited for
§ 91.13 in isolation — but every fatal accident investigation includes
it as a factor, alongside whatever specific rule was broken.

## Worked Scenario 1: The marginal weather day

You're planning a 40-mile cross-country in a sport-pilot PPC. The METAR
at your departure shows 4 SM visibility, BKN030, and the destination
TAF predicts the same all afternoon. You'll be in Class G airspace
under 1,200 AGL the whole way. Are you legal to launch?

**Check the visibility**: § 91.155 allows 1 SM in this airspace; sport-
pilot rule § 61.315(c)(7) requires 3 SM minimum. You have 4 SM —
legal.

**Check the cloud clearance**: § 91.155 in Class G under 1,200 AGL
requires only "clear of clouds." With BKN030 (3,000 AGL ceiling), you'll
be well below. Legal.

**Sport-pilot altitude limits**: § 61.315(c)(11) caps you at 10,000 MSL
or 2,000 AGL whichever is higher. 1,200 AGL is fine.

**Verdict**: Legal. But — § 91.13 (and your gut) might still say wait.
4 SM is marginal, and PPC pilots are particularly vulnerable to
visibility loss because slow flight gives you less time to react when
viz drops further. The conservative call is to wait for 6+ SM. Legal
and wise are not always the same line.

## Worked Scenario 2: Right-of-way at uncontrolled fields

You're entering a 45-degree downwind for runway 27 at a non-towered
airport. As you start your turn to downwind you spot another aircraft
on a left base for runway 9 (the opposite direction). They're at about
your altitude. Who has the right-of-way?

**Check § 91.113**: when both aircraft are landing, the lower one has
the right of way (§ 91.113(g)) — but you're not landing yet, you're
entering the pattern. The runway-in-use ambiguity is the real issue.

The practical move: announce on CTAF, listen for their announcement,
deconflict by altitude or vector. Right-of-way rules are tiebreakers
when both pilots see each other; in this case the better answer is
"one of you should switch runways or extend your pattern." Don't be
the pilot who insists on right-of-way at the cost of a near-miss.

## Worked Scenario 3: Planning fuel for a leg

A 60-NM cross-country leg in a PPC cruising at 30 mph TAS, into a
forecast 10 mph headwind. Tank holds 5 gallons, burn rate 4 gph.

**Time enroute**: GS = 30 - 10 = 20 mph. 60 NM × 1.15 SM/NM = 69 SM.
69 ÷ 20 = 3.45 hours.

**Fuel for leg**: 3.45 × 4 gph = 13.8 gallons. **Already over the 5-
gallon tank capacity.**

**Verdict**: This leg is not flyable in this aircraft on this fuel.
You'll need a fuel stop somewhere around 30-35 NM out, or you'll need
to wait for the wind to relax. Don't be the pilot who tries to
"stretch it" with reduced power — § 91.151's 30-minute reserve plus
§ 91.13's careless-operation prohibition would both apply.

## Section 13: Putting It All Together

Part 91 is the rulebook for sharing the sky with everyone else. The
sections covered here are not the most exciting reading the FAA has
ever produced, but they're the ones the agency reaches for first when
something goes wrong.

A working approach to internalizing them:

1. **Make § 91.103 (preflight) and § 91.151 (fuel) into a habit.** Every
   flight, every time, no exceptions. Most regulatory trouble flows
   from these two being skipped.
2. **Memorize the right-of-way priorities (§ 91.113) cold.** You won't
   have time to look them up at 1,500 AGL with another aircraft
   converging.
3. **Internalize the sport-pilot 3-mile visibility floor** as your hard
   minimum, even when § 91.155 would allow less.
4. **Treat § 91.13 (careless or reckless) as the conscience clause.**
   When the specific rules don't seem to cover a question, ask whether
   a reasonable prudent pilot would do what you're considering.
5. **Build § 91.17's sobriety standard into your life, not just your
   flight planning.** "8 hours bottle to throttle" is too late as a
   pre-flight check; the decision happens the night before.

The certificate is the credential. Part 91 is how you earn the
privilege to keep using it.
