# General Operating Rules (14 CFR Part 91)
## The Part 91 Topics Every Pilot Should Know Cold

---

## Introduction

Once you hold a certificate, your day-to-day legal framework shifts from Part 61 (certification) to **Part 91** (operating rules). Part 91 covers everything about *how* aircraft operate in the National Airspace System — right-of-way, fuel reserves, weather minimums, careless operation, what documents must be on board. Some of its 200-plus sections are written for jets and IFR operations; others are bedrock and apply to every flight.

This chapter walks through each Part 91 section that matters across categories, explains *why* it exists, shows *how* it applies, and calls out common misconceptions. The reg numbers are cited so you can pull the verbatim text from eCFR. The **category-specific overlays** — the stricter limits, medical basis, and equipment specifics for your aircraft — live in your aircraft's general-operating-rules chapter.

---

## Section 1: Pilot in Command — § 91.3

§ 91.3 puts the PIC at the top of the chain: *directly responsible for, and the final authority as to, the operation of the aircraft.*
- **You can't delegate the decision.** Advice is welcome; the call is yours. "My instructor said it was fine" is not a defense.
- **In an emergency you may deviate from any rule of Part 91** to the extent required (§ 91.3(b)); the FAA may request a written report afterward (§ 91.3(c)) — file it candidly.
- **Responsibility is operational, not just legal.** The authority comes with the obligation to use it.

---

## Section 2: Preflight Action — § 91.103

The PIC must become familiar with **all available information** before each flight, including weather reports and forecasts, fuel requirements, alternates, known traffic delays, runway lengths at airports of intended use, and takeoff/landing distance.

NOTAMs (runway closures, TFRs, lighting outages) are part of "all available information" even though not named. EFBs (ForeFlight, Garmin Pilot) and Flight Service (1-800-WX-BRIEF / 1800wxbrief.com) bundle the data.

> **Common misconception**: "Preflight action means walking around the aircraft." That's the *preflight inspection* (§ 91.7 and the POH). § 91.103 is the *information* preflight: weather, fuel, alternates, NOTAMs, charts. Both are required.

A working § 91.103 pattern: pull METARs/TAFs for departure, destination, and an en-route emergency stop; check winds aloft at cruise altitude; check NOTAMs; calculate fuel (time + reserve + contingency); identify a divert for each leg; compute weight & balance and density-altitude takeoff distance.

## Section 3: Right-of-Way — § 91.113

The core mid-air-collision-prevention rule, in priority order:
1. **Aircraft in distress** have right-of-way over all others (§ 91.113(c)).
2. **Different categories converging** (same altitude): balloon > glider > airship > airplane/rotorcraft — priority to the less maneuverable (§ 91.113(d)).
3. **Same category converging**: the aircraft on the **other's right** has right of way ("right has right").
4. **Head-on**: *both* alter course to the right (§ 91.113(e)).
5. **Overtaking**: the aircraft being overtaken has right of way; the overtaking aircraft passes to the right (§ 91.113(f)).
6. **On final / landing**: the lower aircraft has right of way but may not cut in front of or overtake another on final (§ 91.113(g)).

The underlying duty: *"vigilance shall be maintained by each person operating an aircraft so as to see and avoid other aircraft."* The right-of-way rules are tiebreakers when both pilots see each other.

## Section 4: Speed Limits — § 91.117

Maximum indicated airspeeds:
- **Below 10,000 ft MSL**: 250 KIAS (§ 91.117(a)).
- **Below 2,500 ft AGL within 4 NM of a Class C or D primary airport**: 200 KIAS (§ 91.117(b)).
- **In Class B, a VFR corridor through Class B, or beneath Class B**: 200 KIAS (§ 91.117(c)).

The *underneath Class B* rule is where most GA pilots meet it: VFR under an overlying Class B shelf, the limit is 200, not 250.

---

## Section 5: Minimum Safe Altitudes — § 91.119

- **Anywhere** (§ 91.119(a)): never so low that an engine failure couldn't be handled without undue hazard to persons/property on the surface — the catch-all.
- **Over a congested area** (§ 91.119(b)): 1,000 ft above the highest obstacle within a 2,000 ft horizontal radius.
- **Over open water or sparsely populated areas** (§ 91.119(c)): 500 ft from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure.
- **Other areas**: no specific minimum, but § 91.119(a) still applies.

> **Common misconception**: "Low-and-slow aircraft are exempt from the altitude rules." § 91.119 applies to *every* aircraft. It's about not creating surface hazards — overflying a soccer game at 300 ft is a violation regardless of category.

---

## Section 6: Altimeter Settings — § 91.121

- **Below 18,000 ft MSL**: current local altimeter setting of a station within 100 NM (or field elevation if none reporting).
- **At/above 18,000 ft MSL** (Class A): 29.92 inHg.

Get the local setting from ATIS (towered), AWOS/ASOS (non-towered), ATC, or Flight Service (122.2 / 1-800-WX-BRIEF). The altimeter measures pressure, not altitude — a ~0.10 inHg error ≈ 100 ft. Set field elevation before takeoff as a sanity check, confirm with the reported setting, and update at least every 100 NM.

---

## Section 7: Fuel Requirements — § 91.151

VFR minimum reserves:
- **Day VFR**: fuel to the first point of intended landing **+ 30 minutes** at normal cruise.
- **Night VFR**: fuel to the first point of intended landing **+ 45 minutes**.

**This is a regulatory minimum, not a planning target** — plan 45 min to 1 hour and treat 30 minutes as the line you never cross. **"First point of intended landing"** is the destination of the *current leg*; refueling resets the clock.

> **Common misconception**: "Pattern work doesn't need the reserve." It applies to every flight, including pattern work — fuel exhaustion in the pattern forces a real emergency landing.

## Section 8: VFR Weather Minimums — § 91.155

Visibility and cloud-clearance by airspace/altitude. The most-used (below 10,000 MSL):
- **Class G, day, < 1,200 ft AGL**: 1 SM, clear of clouds.
- **Class G, day, > 1,200 ft AGL (below 10,000 MSL)**: 1 SM; 500 below / 1,000 above / 2,000 horizontal.
- **Class E, day, below 10,000 MSL**: 3 SM; 500 / 1,000 / 2,000.
- **Class E/G, day, above 10,000 MSL**: 5 SM; 1,000 below / 1,000 above / 1 SM horizontal.
- **Night** minimums are generally higher than the day Class G figures (typically 3 SM with the standard cloud clearances below 10,000 MSL).

The cloud-clearance buffers give VFR and IFR aircraft room to see and avoid each other in marginal conditions — stay generous.

---

## Section 9: VFR Cruising Altitudes — § 91.159

Above 3,000 ft AGL, cruising altitude is set by magnetic course:
- **0°-179°** (eastbound): odd thousands + 500 (3,500, 5,500, 7,500…).
- **180°-359°** (westbound): even thousands + 500 (4,500, 6,500, 8,500…).

This keeps opposite-direction traffic 1,000 ft apart. Below 3,000 AGL, choose any safe altitude.

## Section 10: Required Documents — ARROW (§ 91.203, § 91.9)

A registered, airworthiness-certificated aircraft must carry:
- **A**irworthiness Certificate (§ 91.203(a)(1)) — displayed.
- **R**egistration Certificate (§ 91.203(a)(2)) — displayed.
- **R**adio Station License (international ops only; not for U.S. domestic).
- **O**perating Limitations (§ 91.9 — POH/placards).
- **W**eight and Balance data (current).

> **Common misconception**: "I keep my documents in the hangar." For a registered aircraft, the ARROW documents must be in the *aircraft*. Ramp checks catch this.

## Section 11: Alcohol, Drugs, and Medication — § 91.17 / § 61.53

§ 91.17 prohibits acting as a crewmember:
- Within **8 hours** of consuming alcohol;
- With a **BAC ≥ 0.04%**;
- While **"under the influence"** of alcohol;
- While using any drug that affects faculties contrary to safety.

The thresholds are independent and additive — meeting any one disqualifies you. "8 hours bottle to throttle" is the floor, not a fitness guarantee. § 61.53 adds that you may not exercise certificate privileges with a known medical condition (or medication) that would make you unable to meet medical requirements *or* operate safely — applying even to pilots who don't hold a medical certificate.

Practical: many OTC sleep aids and first-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine/Benadryl, doxylamine) impair performance 12+ hours; the underlying illness may ground you on its own (§ 61.53); cannabis is federally prohibited regardless of state law.

---

## Section 12: Careless or Reckless Operation — § 91.13

§ 91.13 prohibits operating "in a careless or reckless manner so as to endanger the life or property of another" — broad by design and a frequent catch-all in enforcement. Examples that have drawn § 91.13 actions: buzzing a house, taking off downwind without reason, carrying a passenger when not 90-day current, operating with a known mechanical issue, continuing VFR into deteriorating weather. When the specific rules don't clearly cover a question, ask whether a reasonable, prudent pilot would do what you're considering.

---

## Putting It Together

1. Make **§ 91.103 (preflight)** and **§ 91.151 (fuel)** habits — every flight.
2. Memorize the **§ 91.113 right-of-way** priorities cold.
3. Treat **§ 91.13** as the conscience clause.
4. Build **§ 91.17's** sobriety standard into your life, not just your flight planning.

Part 91 is how you earn the privilege to keep using your certificate.
