## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **General Operating Rules (14 CFR Part 91)** chapter covers each Part 91 section in category-neutral terms. This chapter adds the **airplane-specific overlays**: the equipment, medical, and operating realities that make those rules bite differently for a fixed-wing airplane.

Sources: 14 CFR §§ 61.23, 61.56, 61.57, 91.107, 91.151, 91.159, 91.205, 91.215, 91.225; the *Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge* (FAA-H-8083-25C); and the *Private Pilot — Airplane ACS* (FAA-S-ACS-6C). Verify regulatory specifics against eCFR.

## Medical Basis (§ 61.23) and Currency

Unlike a sport pilot's driver's-license eligibility, a **Private Pilot exercises privileges under a third-class medical certificate or BasicMed** (§ 61.23). Two airplane currency rules that ride on top of Part 91:
- **Flight review (§ 61.56)**: every 24 calendar months.
- **Passenger-carrying recency (§ 61.57)**: three takeoffs and landings in the preceding 90 days (to a full stop at night, in the same category/class) before carrying passengers.

These don't appear in Part 91 itself but gate your legal operation under it.

## Required Equipment (§ 91.205) and Transponder/ADS-B (§ 91.215 / § 91.225)

Beyond the **ARROW** documents (shared chapter), an airplane must carry the **§ 91.205** instrument/equipment list for the kind of flight:
- **Day VFR** ("A TOMATO FLAMES" mnemonic): airspeed indicator, tachometer, oil pressure/temperature, manifold pressure (if applicable), altimeter, temperature gauge (liquid-cooled), fuel gauge, landing gear position indicator (if retractable), anti-collision lights, magnetic compass, ELT, seatbelts/shoulder harnesses.
- **Night VFR** adds ("FLAPS"): fuses/spare, landing light (if for hire), anti-collision lights, position/nav lights, source of electrical power.
- **Transponder + ADS-B Out (§§ 91.215 / 91.225)** where the airspace requires it (Class A/B/C, Mode C veil, at/above 10,000 MSL) — see the airplane airspace chapter.

## Seatbelts and Briefing (§ 91.107)

The PIC must ensure each occupant is **briefed on and uses safety belts** (and shoulder harnesses where installed) for taxi, takeoff, and landing (§ 91.107). Routine in an airplane with multiple seats and passengers.

## Speed, Altitude, and Cruising Altitudes — Airplanes Actually Use Them

Because airplanes cruise fast and high, the Part 91 limits that are "academic" for an ultralight are **operative**:
- **§ 91.117** speed limits (250 KIAS below 10,000 MSL; 200 KIAS near Class C/D and under Class B) genuinely constrain a fast airplane.
- **§ 91.159 hemispherical cruising altitudes** (odd+500 eastbound / even+500 westbound above 3,000 AGL) are flown on essentially every cross-country.

---

## Fuel (§ 91.151) — Day and Night Both Matter

The VFR reserve is **30 minutes day / 45 minutes night** to the first point of intended landing. Airplanes routinely fly at night and over long legs, so the **night 45-minute** reserve is a real planning number, computed from the POH fuel-flow tables corrected for winds aloft.

---

## Airplane § 91.13 Realities

The careless/reckless catch-all applies as in the shared chapter; airplane-specific examples that have drawn § 91.13 (often paired with a specific rule): low passes/buzzing, gear-up landings from a skipped checklist, fuel exhaustion, and VFR-into-IMC. The defenses are checklists, the § 91.103 information preflight, and conservative weather decisions.
