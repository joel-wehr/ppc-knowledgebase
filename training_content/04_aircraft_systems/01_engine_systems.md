# Powered Parachute Aircraft Systems
## Understanding Your Aircraft from Cart to Canopy

---

## Introduction

Powered parachutes consist of several integrated systems that work together to provide safe, reliable flight. Understanding each system—how it works, its limitations, and maintenance requirements—is essential for safe operation. This module covers the major systems found in typical powered parachute aircraft.

---

## The Complete PPC System

A powered parachute consists of four major components:

1. **Cart (Trike/Chassis)**: The wheeled frame that carries pilot, engine, and fuel
2. **Engine and Propulsion System**: Provides thrust for flight
3. **Wing (Ram-Air Parachute)**: Generates lift
4. **Control Systems**: Allows pilot to control the aircraft

---

## Section 1: The Cart/Chassis

### Frame Construction

**Materials**:
- **Chromoly Steel (4130)**: High-strength steel alloy
  - Used for main frame members
  - Excellent strength-to-weight ratio
  - Can be welded for repairs
  - Susceptible to rust (requires protective coating)

- **Aluminum (6061-T6)**: Aircraft-grade aluminum
  - Used for secondary structures
  - Lighter than steel
  - Corrosion-resistant
  - Cannot be welded without special equipment

**Frame Design**:
- **Main Rails**: Run length of cart, primary structural members
- **Cross Members**: Provide lateral strength
- **Motor Mount**: Supports engine
- **Seat Frames**: Pilot and passenger seating
- **Suspension Mounting Points**: Attachment for suspension system

**Inspection Points**:
- Welds for cracks (especially high-stress areas)
- Corrosion on steel components
- Loose or missing bolts
- Frame deformation or damage
- Paint condition (protects from rust)

### Suspension System

**Purpose**: Absorb landing impacts and smooth ground operations

**Components**:
- **Springs**: Coil springs at each wheel
- **Shock Absorbers**: Dampen spring oscillations
- **Suspension Arms**: Connect wheels to frame
- **Pivot Points**: Allow suspension movement

**Types**:
- **Spring-Over-Shock**: Common design, shock absorber inside spring
- **Independent Suspension**: Each wheel moves independently
- **Trailing Arm**: Arm trails behind pivot point

**Maintenance**:
- Check for smooth operation
- Inspect for leaks (shock absorbers)
- Verify secure mounting
- Lubricate pivot points
- Check spring condition and rate

### Landing Gear

**Main Wheels** (typically two):
- **Size**: 15"-20" diameter common
- **Type**: Low-pressure turf tires typical
- **Pressure**: 8-12 psi recommended
- **Axles**: Secured with castle nuts and cotter pins

**Nose Wheel**:
- **Size**: Usually smaller than main wheels (10"-15")
- **Steering**: Connected to steering bar
- **Brake**: May have disk brake
- **Castering**: Some designs allow free castering

**Brake System** (if equipped):
- **Type**: Mechanical disk brake on nose wheel
- **Actuation**: Hand lever on steering bar
- **Effectiveness**: Limited due to light weight on nose
- **Use**: Taxiing speed control, parking

**Tire Maintenance**:
- **Pressure**: Check before every flight
- **Wear**: Inspect tread and sidewalls
- **Damage**: Check for cuts, bulges, embedded objects
- **Age**: Replace tires over 5 years old regardless of wear

### Seats and Restraints

**Seat Design**:
- **Material**: Typically nylon mesh or padded seat
- **Mounting**: Bolted or strapped to frame
- **Configuration**: Tandem (front and rear) most common
- **Adjustability**: May have adjustable positions

**Seat Belts**:
- **Type**: 4-point or 5-point harness typical
- **Material**: Automotive-grade nylon webbing
- **Buckle**: Quick-release buckle
- **Attachment**: Through-bolted to frame

**Safety Requirements**:
- Inspect for fraying or damage
- Check buckle operation
- Verify secure attachment points
- Ensure proper fit for pilot
- Never fly without wearing restraints

### Propeller Guard/Cage

**Purpose**: Protects pilot, passengers, and wing from propeller

**Design**:
- **Ring Structure**: Circular or oval ring surrounding propeller
- **Tubes/Bars**: Connect ring to frame
- **Coverage**: Must completely surround propeller arc

**Materials**:
- **Aluminum tubing**: Lightweight, common
- **Steel tubing**: Heavier but stronger
- **Composite materials**: Modern designs

**Inspection**:
- Check for cracks (especially at welds)
- Verify secure attachment
- Ensure adequate clearance to propeller
- Check for deformation from impact
- Verify wing line clearances

## Section 2: Engine and Propulsion System

### Two-Stroke Engines

Most PPCs use two-stroke engines (Rotax 447, 503, 582, etc.)

**Advantages of Two-Strokes**:
- High power-to-weight ratio
- Simple design
- Fewer moving parts
- Lower cost
- Easy maintenance

**Disadvantages**:
- Higher fuel consumption
- Oil consumption (mixed fuel)
- Higher emissions
- More frequent maintenance
- Shorter TBO (Time Between Overhaul)

**How Two-Stroke Engines Work**:

1. **Intake/Compression**: Piston moves up
   - Fuel/air mixture enters crankcase
   - Mixture compressed in cylinder

2. **Power/Exhaust**: Piston moves down
   - Spark plug fires
   - Combustion pushes piston down
   - Exhaust ports open, exhaust exits
   - Fresh mixture enters cylinder

**Key Points**:
- Every stroke is a power stroke (vs. every other stroke in 4-stroke)
- Lubrication by oil mixed with fuel
- No valves (ports in cylinder wall)
- Power pulses every revolution

### Four-Stroke Engines

Some modern PPCs use four-stroke engines (Rotax 912, 914)

**Advantages of Four-Strokes**:
- Better fuel economy
- Lower emissions
- Longer TBO
- Smoother operation
- Separate oil system

**Disadvantages**:
- More complex
- Heavier
- Higher cost
- More expensive maintenance

**Four-Stroke Cycle**:
1. **Intake**: Piston down, intake valve open
2. **Compression**: Piston up, both valves closed
3. **Power**: Combustion, piston down
4. **Exhaust**: Piston up, exhaust valve open

### Common Rotax Engines for PPCs

#### **Rotax 447** (40 hp)
- 2-cylinder, 2-stroke
- Air-cooled
- Pull-start (typically)
- Simplest option
- Adequate for light pilots and smaller wings

#### **Rotax 503** (50 hp)
- 2-cylinder, 2-stroke
- Dual ignition
- Electric start available
- Fan-cooled
- Popular for single-seat PPCs

#### **Rotax 582** (65 hp)
- 2-cylinder, 2-stroke
- Liquid-cooled
- Dual ignition
- Electric start
- Most common PPC engine
- **Details below**

#### **Rotax 912 ULS** (100 hp)
- 4-cylinder, 4-stroke
- Liquid-cooled
- Dual ignition
- Electric start
- Used in heavier PPCs
- Excellent reliability

### Rotax 582 Engine (Detailed)

**Specifications**:
- **Displacement**: 580 cc (35.4 cubic inches)
- **Configuration**: 2-cylinder, 2-stroke, in-line
- **Cooling**: Liquid-cooled (radiator and coolant)
- **Ignition**: Dual CDI (Capacitor Discharge Ignition)
- **Starting**: Electric starter with manual backup
- **Carburetion**: Dual Bing carburetors
- **Lubrication**: Oil injection system
- **Power Output**: 65 hp @ 6,500 RPM
- **Weight**: Approximately 80 lbs (dry)

**Major Components**:

1. **Crankcase**:
   - Houses crankshaft and connecting rods
   - Sealed unit (pressure and vacuum pulses)
   - Contains rotary valve

2. **Cylinders**:
   - Aluminum with Nikasil coating
   - Exhaust and transfer ports machined in walls
   - Cooling jacket for liquid cooling

3. **Pistons**:
   - Two-ring design
   - Aluminum alloy
   - Dome-top for compression

4. **Crankshaft**:
   - Forged steel
   - Counterweighted for balance
   - Supported by ball bearings

5. **Connecting Rods**:
   - Steel construction
   - Needle bearings at both ends

**Engine Limits**:
- **Maximum RPM**: 6,800 (avoid sustained operation above 6,500)
- **Maximum EGT**: 1,200°F (Exhaust Gas Temperature)
- **Maximum CHT**: 300°F (Cylinder Head Temperature)
- **Maximum Coolant**: 175°F (Coolant Temperature)

### Engine Break-In

**Critical Process**: Proper break-in ensures long engine life

**Break-In Procedure** (per Rotax manual):
1. **First 2 hours**: Vary RPM between 3,000-5,000
2. **2-5 hours**: Vary RPM between 3,000-6,000
3. **5-10 hours**: May run at full power for short periods
4. **After 10 hours**: Normal operation permitted

**During Break-In**:
- DO NOT use sustained full power
- DO NOT use sustained constant RPM
- DO vary throttle settings frequently
- DO monitor temperatures closely
- DO check for leaks after each run

**Why Break-In Matters**:
- Allows piston rings to seat properly
- Allows bearings to wear in
- Establishes proper clearances
- Prevents future problems

### Fuel System

**Fuel Type**: Automotive gasoline (minimum 89 octane)

**DO NOT USE**:
- Automotive fuel with ethanol (E10, E15, etc.) - causes problems
- Aviation fuel (100LL) - may cause excessive deposits
- Lower than 89 octane - may cause detonation

**Fuel System Components**:

1. **Fuel Tank**:
   - **Capacity**: 10 gallons typical (15 gallon optional)
   - **Material**: Aluminum or composite
   - **Venting**: Must be vented to atmosphere
   - **Location**: Behind seat, below wing attachment

2. **Fuel Cap**:
   - Secure seal to prevent spillage
   - Vented or uses separate vent line
   - Must be tightened before flight

3. **Fuel Lines**:
   - **Material**: Fuel-rated rubber or synthetic
   - **Size**: 1/4" or 5/16" typical
   - **Clamps**: Must be secure at all connections
   - **Inspection**: Check for cracks, softening, leaks

4. **Fuel Filter/Gascolator**:
   - **Purpose**: Removes water and debris from fuel
   - **Location**: Lowest point in system
   - **Drain Valve**: Allows sampling and draining
   - **Inspection**: Check for water, contamination

5. **Fuel Pump**:
   - **Type**: Diaphragm pulse pump (typical)
   - **Operation**: Uses engine pulse to pump fuel
   - **Pulse Line**: Connects to engine crankcase
   - **Maintenance**: Replace/rebuild annually

6. **Carburetors**:
   - **Type**: Bing CV (Constant Velocity) carburetors
   - **Number**: Two (one per cylinder)
   - **Adjustment**: Mixture needles control fuel/air ratio
   - **Synchronization**: Must be balanced for smooth operation

**Fuel System Maintenance**:
- Drain gascolator before each flight
- Check for water contamination
- Inspect lines and connections
- Verify fuel cap is secure
- Check fuel level with visual gauge or dipstick
- Use fuel stabilizer for extended storage

### Ignition System

**Dual Ignition**: Two independent ignition systems

**Components per System**:
- **Magneto/CDI Module**: Generates high voltage
- **Spark Plug**: Creates spark in cylinder
- **High-Tension Lead**: Connects CDI to spark plug
- **Kill Switch/Mag Switch**: Grounds ignition to stop engine

**Advantages of Dual Ignition**:
- Redundancy (if one fails, engine runs on other)
- Better combustion (two sparks per cylinder)
- Smoother operation
- Increased safety

**Magneto Check** (Pre-Flight):
1. Warm engine to operating temperature
2. Run at 3,000-3,500 RPM
3. Turn off one magneto
4. Note RPM drop (100-300 RPM typical)
5. Turn magneto back on
6. Repeat for other magneto
7. Both must function correctly

**Ignition System Maintenance**:
- Keep mag switches clean and functional
- Check spark plug condition every 25 hours
- Replace spark plugs every 100 hours or per manual
- Inspect high-tension leads for cracks/damage
- Ensure kill switch functions properly

### Cooling System (Liquid-Cooled Engines)

**Components**:

1. **Coolant**:
   - **Type**: 50/50 mix of antifreeze and distilled water
   - **Antifreeze**: Silicate-free, phosphate-free ONLY
   - **Why Distilled Water**: Prevents mineral deposits
   - **Capacity**: Approximately 3 quarts

2. **Radiator**:
   - **Type**: Automotive-style heat exchanger
   - **Location**: Behind/beside engine
   - **Air Flow**: Propeller wash provides cooling air
   - **Drain**: Should have drain cock at bottom

3. **Water Pump**:
   - **Type**: Centrifugal pump
   - **Drive**: Belt or gear-driven from engine
   - **Seal**: Mechanical seal must not leak

4. **Thermostat**:
   - **Purpose**: Regulates coolant temperature
   - **Operation**: Opens at approximately 140°F
   - **Effect**: Allows engine to warm quickly, maintains optimal temp

5. **Hoses**:
   - **Material**: Automotive-grade rubber
   - **Clamps**: Must be tight (check regularly)
   - **Inspection**: Look for cracks, soft spots, leaks

6. **Overflow Bottle**:
   - **Purpose**: Catch overflow, allow expansion
   - **Level**: Should be at "full" line when cold
   - **Cap**: Usually vented

**Temperature Monitoring**:
- **Normal**: 140-160°F
- **Maximum**: 175°F
- **Overheating Signs**:
  - Temperature above 175°F
  - Steam from overflow
  - Loss of power
  - Unusual engine sounds

**Cooling System Maintenance**:
- Check coolant level before every flight
- Inspect hoses and clamps regularly
- Check for leaks
- Change coolant every 2 years
- Flush system when changing coolant
- Verify thermostat operation

### Exhaust System

**Components**:
- **Exhaust Manifold**: Collects exhaust from cylinder
- **Expansion Chamber**: Tuned chamber (2-stroke engines)
- **Muffler**: Reduces noise
- **Exhaust Springs**: Allow flex, absorb vibration
- **Exhaust Pipe**: Directs exhaust away from aircraft

**Exhaust System Functions**:
1. Direct exhaust gases away from pilot/aircraft
2. Reduce noise
3. Tune engine (2-stroke expansion chambers)
4. Allow thermal expansion

**Inspection**:
- Check for cracks (especially at welds and springs)
- Verify springs are intact
- Look for exhaust leaks
- Check mounting hardware
- Ensure exhaust directs away from fabric/wing
- Verify exhaust isn't contacting other components

**Safety Note**: Exhaust systems get extremely hot (600°F+)
- Allow to cool before touching
- Keep wing and lines clear
- Check for heat damage to nearby components

### Oil System

**Two-Stroke Oil Requirements**:
- **Type**: TCW-3 rated two-stroke oil
- **Quality**: Use high-quality, aviation-suitable oil
- **Consistency**: Don't mix brands if possible

**Oil Injection System** (582):
1. **Oil Tank**:
   - Capacity: Approximately 1 gallon
   - Transparent for level check
   - Fill before every flight

2. **Oil Pump**:
   - Meters oil into fuel system
   - Driven by engine
   - Ratio varies with RPM (more oil at higher RPM)

3. **Oil Lines**:
   - Small diameter tubing
   - Must be clear and unobstructed
   - Check for leaks

4. **Rotary Valve Lubrication** (separate):
   - Small reservoir (250ml typical)
   - Uses same 2-stroke oil
   - Lubricates rotary valve only
   - Must be kept full

**Oil Consumption**:
- Varies with RPM and load
- Approximately 1 quart per 2-3 hours typical
- Always check level before flight
- Refill oil tank with every fuel fill

**Pre-Mix Alternative** (if injection fails):
- 40:1 or 50:1 ratio typical
- Mix thoroughly before adding to tank
- Used as emergency measure or on non-injection engines

## Section 3: Propeller System

### Propeller Basics

**Function**: Convert engine torque into thrust

**Design Elements**:
- **Blades**: Typically 2 or 3 blades
- **Pitch**: Angle of blades relative to rotation plane
- **Diameter**: Overall size of propeller arc
- **Material**: Wood, composite, or metal

**Propeller Terminology**:
- **Leading Edge**: Front edge of blade
- **Trailing Edge**: Rear edge of blade
- **Blade Face**: Flat or slightly concave surface (front)
- **Blade Back**: Curved surface (rear)
- **Hub**: Center section, attaches to engine
- **Tip**: Outer end of blade

### Propeller Types for PPCs

**Composite Propellers** (most common):
- **Advantages**:
  - Lightweight
  - Adjustable pitch
  - Durable
  - Cost-effective
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Can be damaged by FOD (Foreign Object Damage)
  - Requires periodic inspection
  - UV degradation over time

**Wood Propellers**:
- **Advantages**:
  - Traditional
  - Smooth operation
  - Self-limiting (breaks before damaging engine)
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Moisture sensitive
  - Fixed pitch
  - Can delaminate
  - Requires coating maintenance

### Ground-Adjustable Pitch

Many PPC propellers allow pitch adjustment on ground

**Purpose**: Optimize propeller for weight/altitude conditions

**Adjustment**:
- Done with engine off
- Loosening clamps and rotating blades
- Must be done equally on all blades
- Requires re-torquing hardware

**Pitch Settings**:
- **Finer Pitch (flatter)**: More RPM, better static thrust
  - Use for: Heavy loads, high density altitude, short fields
- **Coarser Pitch (steeper)**: Lower RPM, better cruise efficiency
  - Use for: Light loads, low density altitude, cruise optimization

**Target RPM**: Approximately 6,350-6,450 at full throttle in flight

### Propeller Maintenance and Inspection

**Daily Inspection**:
- Check for nicks, cracks, damage
- Verify all mounting bolts are tight
- Check for delamination or separation
- Ensure blade balance (no excessive vibration)
- Clean bugs and debris

**Periodic Maintenance**:
- Torque check bolts (per manufacturer specs)
- Clean propeller (bathroom cleaner with scrubbing bubbles works well)
- Apply protective coating if needed
- Check blade tracking (blades in same plane)

**Damage Assessment**:
- **Minor Nicks**: Can be filed smooth (leading edge)
- **Gouges**: May require professional repair
- **Cracks**: Propeller must be replaced
- **Delamination**: Propeller must be replaced

**Safety Notes**:
- Propeller is extremely dangerous when running
- Never stand in propeller arc with engine running
- Always assume engine may start when near propeller
- Brief passengers about propeller danger

## Section 4: Gearbox/Reduction Drive

### Purpose

Aircraft engines turn at high RPM (6,000-7,000), but propellers are efficient at lower RPM (2,000-2,500). The gearbox reduces engine RPM to optimal propeller RPM.

**Reduction Ratio**: Typically 2.58:1 or 3.0:1
- 2.58:1 = engine turns 2.58 times for each propeller revolution
- At 6,450 engine RPM: propeller turns 2,500 RPM (2.58:1 ratio)

### Gearbox Types

**Belt Drive**:
- **Advantages**: Smooth, quiet, absorbs shock
- **Disadvantages**: Requires tension adjustment, belts wear
- **Maintenance**: Check belt tension, condition, alignment

**Gear Drive** (most common in PPCs):
- **Advantages**: Direct, efficient, reliable
- **Disadvantages**: More noise, no shock absorption
- **Types**: Spur gears or planetary gears

### Rotax "E" Gearbox (Common in 582)

**Specifications**:
- Reduction ratio: 3.0:1 (B model) or 2.58:1 (E model)
- Lubrication: Splash lubrication, oil bath
- Capacity: Approximately 200ml oil
- Type: Spur gear design

**Components**:
- **Input Shaft**: Connects to engine crankshaft
- **Output Shaft**: Connects to propeller
- **Gears**: Hardened steel gears
- **Bearings**: Support shafts
- **Housing**: Aluminum case, sealed

**Gearbox Oil**:
- **Type**: SAE 30 or SAE 40 (per Rotax specifications)
- **Capacity**: Approximately 200ml (7 oz)
- **Check Level**: Dipstick or sight glass
- **Change Interval**:
  - First change: 10 hours
  - Subsequent changes: Every 100 hours or annually

**Inspection**:
- Check for oil leaks
- Listen for unusual noises
- Check backlash (slight movement when turning prop)
- Verify secure mounting
- Check alignment with engine

## Section 5: Electrical System

### Components

1. **Battery**:
   - **Type**: 12-volt sealed lead-acid or AGM
   - **Capacity**: 12-18 amp-hours typical
   - **Location**: Behind seat, secured in battery box
   - **Function**: Start engine, power accessories

2. **Stator/Alternator**:
   - **Function**: Generate electricity when engine running
   - **Type**: Permanent magnet alternator (typical)
   - **Output**: 12-14 volts DC, 10-20 amps
   - **Mounting**: On engine

3. **Regulator/Rectifier**:
   - **Function**: Convert AC to DC, regulate voltage
   - **Output**: 13.5-14.5 volts DC
   - **Protection**: Prevents overcharging battery

4. **Wiring**:
   - **Gauge**: Appropriate for amperage
   - **Protection**: Fuses or circuit breakers
   - **Routing**: Away from hot components, protected from chafing

5. **Switches**:
   - **Ignition/Key Switch**: Controls starter and accessories
   - **Mag Switches**: Control each ignition system
   - **Master Switch**: May have separate master

6. **Starter Motor**:
   - **Type**: Electric starter (if equipped)
   - **Operation**: Engages flywheel to crank engine
   - **Current Draw**: High (50-100 amps momentarily)

### Electrical System Operation

**Normal Operation**:
1. Turn on master switch
2. Check voltage (12+ volts indicates charged battery)
3. Start engine
4. Voltage should rise to 13.5-14.5 volts (charging system working)

**Low Voltage**: May indicate:
- Weak battery
- Charging system failure
- Excessive electrical load
- Wiring problem

**Electrical Failure in Flight**:
- Engine will continue running (magnetos independent)
- Loss of electric accessories
- Cannot restart engine if shut down
- Land as soon as practical

### Accessories

**Strobe Light**:
- Anti-collision light
- Required for twilight operations
- Typically on top of aircraft

**Intercom**:
- Allows pilot/passenger communication
- Reduces noise exposure
- Usually operates on 12 volts

**GPS/Instruments**:
- May be powered by electrical system
- Should have internal backup battery

**Engine Instruments**:
- May be electrical (EIS system) or direct-reading

## Section 6: Instruments

### Engine Instruments

**Tachometer** (RPM gauge):
- Shows engine RPM
- Used for power settings
- Critical for monitoring engine speed
- Redline typically at 6,800 RPM

**Exhaust Gas Temperature (EGT)**:
- Monitors combustion temperature
- Normal: 1,100-1,200°F
- Maximum: 1,200-1,220°F
- High EGT: Lean mixture or overheating

**Cylinder Head Temperature (CHT)**:
- Monitors cylinder temperature
- Normal: 250-280°F
- Maximum: 300°F
- High CHT: Inadequate cooling or over-working engine

**Coolant Temperature**:
- Monitors coolant temperature (liquid-cooled engines)
- Normal: 140-160°F
- Maximum: 175°F
- Low: Thermostat not opening or inadequate warm-up

**Oil Temperature** (if equipped):
- Monitors oil temperature
- Important for 4-stroke engines
- Normal range: Per manufacturer

**Fuel Gauge**:
- Shows fuel quantity
- May be analog or digital
- Verify with visual inspection (dipstick)

**Voltmeter**:
- Shows electrical system voltage
- Should show 13.5-14.5V when engine running
- 12V or lower when engine off

### Flight Instruments

**Altimeter**:
- Shows altitude above sea level
- Must be set to local altimeter setting
- Pressure-based instrument
- Not required by Part 103, but highly recommended

**Airspeed Indicator** (ASI):
- Shows airspeed
- Critical for avoiding stalls
- May be mechanical or electronic
- Not required by Part 103, but recommended

**Compass**:
- Shows magnetic heading
- Simple magnetic compass typical
- GPS may provide heading also
- Useful for navigation

**GPS**:
- Shows position, ground speed, track
- Very useful for navigation and safety
- Not required, but common
- May have moving map

**Vertical Speed Indicator** (VSI):
- Shows rate of climb or descent
- Uncommon in PPCs
- Can help with performance monitoring

### Engine Information System (EIS)

Modern PPCs often use digital EIS that combines multiple functions:
- RPM
- EGT
- CHT
- Coolant temperature
- Voltmeter
- Hobbs meter (engine hours)
- May include alarms for out-of-range values

**Advantages**:
- Compact
- Easy to read
- Alarms for problems
- Data logging (some models)

## Section 7: The Wing (Ram-Air Parachute)

### Wing Construction

**Canopy (Fabric)**:
- **Material**: Ripstop nylon (typically zero-porosity)
- **Weight**: 1.1 to 1.4 oz per square yard
- **Coating**: Polyurethane or silicone for air-tightness
- **Colors**: Bright colors for visibility

**Cells**:
- **Number**: 24-32 cells typical
- **Function**: Form airfoil shape when inflated
- **Opening**: Leading edge openings allow air entry

**Ribs**:
- **Material**: Same fabric as canopy
- **Function**: Maintain cell shape, define airfoil
- **Attachment**: Sewn to upper and lower surfaces

**Lines**:
- **Material**: Braided nylon or Spectra/Dacron
- **Diameter**: 1-3mm typical
- **Strength**: Each line rated for 200-500 lbs
- **Organization**: A, B, C, D lines (from front to back)
- **Total**: Hundreds of lines on typical wing

**Attachment Points**:
- **Carabiners**: Steel, load-rated connectors
- **Risers**: Heavy webbing connecting lines to carabiners
- **Quick Links**: Connect risers to aircraft (certified only)

### Wing Aerodynamics (Review)

**Airfoil Design**:
- Creates pressure differential (low on top, high on bottom)
- Lift generated by pressure difference
- Drag created as byproduct

**Angle of Attack**:
- Determined by trim settings and control inputs
- Fixed by attachment points (trim)
- Modified by brake inputs (pilot control)

**Aspect Ratio**:
- Relationship between span and chord
- Higher aspect = more efficient
- PPCs typically have moderate aspect ratio

### Wing Sizes and Loading

**Common Wing Sizes**:
- 450 sq ft: Very light pilots or minimum loading
- 500 sq ft: Most common, versatile
- 550 sq ft: Heavier pilots or dual operations
- 600+ sq ft: Tandem or heavy loads

**Wing Loading Calculation**:
- Total weight ÷ Wing area = Wing loading (lbs/sq ft)
- Example: 900 lbs ÷ 500 sq ft = 1.8 lbs/sq ft

**Wing Loading Effects**:
- Lower loading: Gentler, slower, more turbulence-sensitive
- Higher loading: Faster, better wind penetration, steeper angle

### Trim System

**Purpose**: Adjust angle of attack and speed

**How It Works**:
- Trim adjusts riser length
- Shortening front risers: Speed increases, nose lower
- Lengthening front risers: Speed decreases, nose higher

**Trim Positions**:
- Numbered settings (1-5 or 1-8 typical)
- Never trim past manufacturer recommendations
- Start conservatively (less speed)
- Adjust based on experience and conditions

**In-Flight Trim Adjustment**:
- Some wings allow in-flight trim changes
- Others require landing to adjust
- Know your wing's capabilities

### Control System

**Steering Lines** (brake lines, toggle lines):
- **Routing**: From pilot's feet/hands to wing trailing edge
- **Function**: Pull down trailing edge, increase angle of attack
- **Response**: Aircraft turns, slows, descends (depending on input)

**Control Inputs**:
- **Left steering**: Aircraft turns left
- **Right steering**: Aircraft turns right
- **Both steering**: Aircraft slows, descends, flares for landing

**Steering Bar System** (foot controls):
- Bars pivot at center
- Pushing forward on bar pulls down corresponding trailing edge
- Connected via steering lines through pulleys

**Trim Lock/Cleat**:
- Holds steering lines at desired length
- Must release some trim for takeoff
- Can re-trim in flight (if wing allows)

### Wing Inspection

**Before Every Flight**:
1. **Layout**: Lay wing out correctly (no twists)
2. **Lines**: Check every line for damage, tangles
3. **Fabric**: Inspect canopy for tears, holes
4. **Attachment**: Verify carabiners locked, risers secure
5. **Inflation**: Check cells inflate properly during kiting

**Periodic Inspection**:
- Check line lengths (stretch over time)
- Inspect stitching (especially load-bearing seams)
- Check fabric for UV damage
- Look for abrasion points
- Verify trim system operation

**Professional Inspection**:
- Annual inspection by qualified rigger recommended
- Required in some insurance policies
- Line measurements checked
- Fabric porosity tested
- Full assessment of airworthiness

### Wing Care and Maintenance

**Storage**:
- Store in bag when not in use
- Keep out of sunlight (UV damage)
- Store dry (moisture causes mildew)
- Cool, dry place

**Cleaning**:
- Spot clean with damp cloth (water only)
- Mild soap for stubborn stains
- Never use solvents or harsh chemicals
- Air dry completely before packing

**Repairs**:
- Small holes: Sail tape (temporary)
- Larger tears: Professional repair required
- Damaged lines: NEVER tie or knot - replace
- Stitching issues: Professional repair

**Never Attempt to Repair**:
- Load-bearing stitching
- Damaged lines (replace only)
- Major tears or damage
- Structural components

## Summary

Understanding your powered parachute's systems is essential for:
1. **Safe Operation**: Knowing limitations and capabilities
2. **Problem Recognition**: Identifying issues before they become critical
3. **Maintenance**: Performing required inspections and upkeep
4. **Decision Making**: Making informed go/no-go decisions
5. **Emergency Response**: Responding appropriately to malfunctions

**Key Takeaways**:
- PPCs are simple but require thorough understanding
- Regular inspection and maintenance are critical
- Each system has specific limitations
- Pilot must monitor all systems during flight
- When in doubt, consult manufacturer or professional
- Proper maintenance prevents most problems

**Remember**: You are the pilot-in-command. The airworthiness and proper operation of your aircraft is YOUR responsibility. Never fly an aircraft you believe has a problem, and never exceed the limitations of any system.
