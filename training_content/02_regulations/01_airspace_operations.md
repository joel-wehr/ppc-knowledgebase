# Airspace Operations for Powered Parachute Pilots

---

## Introduction

Understanding airspace is critical for safe and legal powered parachute operations. While Part 103 ultralights are not required to obtain air traffic control clearances, pilots must still understand and respect airspace boundaries, avoid controlled airspace without permission, and operate safely within the National Airspace System.

This module provides comprehensive coverage of:
- United States airspace classification
- Where powered parachutes can and cannot operate
- Special use airspace
- Temporary flight restrictions
- Chart reading and navigation
- Communication procedures
- Practical decision-making for airspace compliance

**Key Principle**: Ignorance of airspace regulations is not a defense. As pilot-in-command, you are responsible for knowing where you can legally fly.

---

## Section 1: The National Airspace System (NAS)

### What is the National Airspace System?

The National Airspace System (NAS) encompasses all airspace over the United States and its territories. The FAA has divided this airspace into different classes, each with specific rules, requirements, and services.

**Classification System**:
- **Class A**: 18,000 feet MSL and above
- **Class B**: Busiest airports (major hubs)
- **Class C**: Busy airports with radar service
- **Class D**: Airports with control towers
- **Class E**: Controlled airspace (various configurations)
- **Class G**: Uncontrolled airspace

**Special Use Airspace**:
- Military Operations Areas (MOAs)
- Restricted Areas
- Prohibited Areas
- Warning Areas
- Alert Areas

**Other Airspace**:
- Temporary Flight Restrictions (TFRs)
- Terminal Radar Service Areas (TRSAs)
- National Security Areas

## Section 2: Class G Airspace - Where PPCs Primarily Operate

### What is Class G Airspace?

Class G is **uncontrolled airspace** - airspace where air traffic control does not exercise authority. This is the primary operating environment for powered parachutes.

**Characteristics**:
- No ATC clearance required
- No communication required
- No transponder required
- No pilot certificate required (for Part 103)
- Typically surface to 700 or 1,200 feet AGL (but varies)

**Where Class G Exists**:
- Most rural areas from surface upward
- Areas away from airports and controlled airspace
- Above Class E airspace ceilings in some locations
- Small pockets within Class E in certain areas

### Class G Altitudes

The top of Class G airspace varies across the country:

**1,200 feet AGL** (most common):
- Most areas of the United States
- Class G extends from surface to 1,200 feet AGL
- Above 1,200 feet AGL becomes Class E

**700 feet AGL** (near airports):
- Areas with instrument approaches
- Transition areas around airports
- Class G from surface to 700 feet AGL
- Above 700 feet becomes Class E (shown as magenta vignette on charts)

**Surface** (rare):
- Some remote areas in Alaska
- Mountainous regions
- Class E goes to the surface

**14,500 feet MSL**:
- Over mountainous terrain in western states
- Class G can extend much higher
- Designated on charts

### Operating in Class G

**Advantages**:
- Freedom to operate without clearances
- No radio communication required
- Flexibility in route and altitude selection
- No ATC delays or restrictions

**Responsibilities**:
- See and avoid other aircraft
- Comply with visibility and cloud clearance minimums
- Avoid congested areas (Part 103 restriction)
- Stay clear of other airspace classes
- Maintain awareness of position

**Weather Minimums in Class G** (Part 103 requirements):
- **Daytime**: 3 statute miles visibility
- **Below 1,200 feet AGL**: Clear of clouds
- **At or above 1,200 feet AGL**:
  - 1 statute mile visibility
  - Clear of clouds (Part 103)
  - (Standard aircraft: 500 below, 1,000 above, 2,000 horizontal)

**Part 103 is MORE restrictive** - you must remain clear of clouds at all altitudes.

## Section 3: Controlled Airspace - Where You Need Permission

### Class A Airspace

**Definition**: 18,000 feet MSL to FL600 (60,000 feet)

**PPC Operations**: PROHIBITED
- Powered parachutes cannot operate in Class A
- IFR flight plans required
- Pressurized aircraft typically required
- Well above PPC capabilities

### Class B Airspace

**Definition**: Airspace around the nation's busiest airports (major hubs)

**Configuration**:
- Resembles "upside-down wedding cake"
- Multiple shelves extending outward from airport
- Surface area at center
- Upper shelves typically to 10,000 feet MSL

**Examples**:
- Los Angeles (LAX)
- Chicago O'Hare (ORD)
- New York JFK/LaGuardia/Newark
- Atlanta (ATL)
- Dallas/Fort Worth (DFW)

**Chart Depiction**:
- Solid blue lines
- Altitude information for each shelf
- "Mode C Veil" extends 30 nm from primary airport

**PPC Operations**:
- **CLEARANCE REQUIRED** to enter
- ATC must specifically authorize: "Cleared to enter Class B airspace"
- Two-way radio communication required
- Transponder with Mode C required (generally)
- **Practically AVOIDED** by PPC pilots

**Why PPCs Avoid Class B**:
- Complexity of clearance procedures
- High-speed traffic environment
- Radio communication required
- Risk of wake turbulence from large jets
- Better alternatives always available

**Altitude Restrictions**:
- Even if operating below Class B shelves, maintain awareness
- Mode C veil requires transponder within 30 nm
- Stay well clear unless specifically coordinated

### Class C Airspace

**Definition**: Airspace around moderately busy airports with radar service

**Configuration**:
- Two circles (inner and outer)
- Inner "shelf": Surface to 4,000 feet AGL (typically)
- Outer "shelf": 1,200 feet AGL to 4,000 feet AGL (typically)
- 5 nm radius inner circle
- 10 nm radius outer circle

**Examples**:
- Smaller regional airports
- Mid-size city airports
- Military bases with civilian operations

**Chart Depiction**:
- Solid magenta lines
- Two distinct circles
- Altitude limits labeled

**PPC Operations**:
- **RADIO COMMUNICATION REQUIRED** before entering
- Establish two-way communication with ATC
- Transponder with Mode C required
- VFR clearance not required (unlike Class B)
- **GENERALLY AVOIDED** by PPC pilots

**Communication Procedure** (if necessary):
1. Listen to ATIS for airport information
2. Call approach control before entering airspace
3. Identify aircraft type: "Ultralight" or "Powered Parachute"
4. State position, altitude, intentions
5. ATC will respond with your call sign - communication established
6. Follow ATC instructions

**Why PPCs Typically Avoid Class C**:
- Radio communication required
- Faster traffic mixing with slow PPCs
- Better operating areas nearby
- Complexity not worth the benefit

### Class D Airspace

**Definition**: Airspace around airports with operating control towers

**Configuration**:
- Cylinder shape
- Surface to 2,500 feet AGL (typical)
- Approximately 4-5 nm radius
- Only active when tower is operating

**Chart Depiction**:
- Dashed blue lines
- Altitude limit in hundreds of feet (e.g., "25" = 2,500 feet)

**Tower Operating Hours**:
- Class D only exists when tower is open
- Check Chart Supplement for tower hours
- When tower closed, usually becomes Class E or G
- **CRITICAL**: Know the tower hours

**PPC Operations**:
- **RADIO COMMUNICATION REQUIRED** when tower operating
- Establish two-way communication before entering
- No transponder required (unless otherwise specified)
- **OFTEN AVOIDED**, but more accessible than B or C

**Communication Procedure**:
1. Listen to ATIS for airport information
2. Call tower: "[Airport] Tower, Powered Parachute [location], request permission to transition Class D airspace"
3. ATC will respond with your call sign and instructions
4. Follow instructions precisely
5. Report exiting the airspace

**When Class D is Viable**:
- Small airport with light traffic
- Transiting through to reach destination
- Tower controller familiar with ultralights
- Good weather and visibility
- PPC pilot comfortable with radio procedures

**After Tower Closes**:
- Airspace reverts to Class E or G (check Chart Supplement)
- No communication required
- Still must avoid runway operations
- See and avoid traffic

### Class E Airspace

**Definition**: Controlled airspace that is not Class A, B, C, or D

**Types of Class E**:

**1. Class E to Surface** (dashed magenta line):
- Designated areas with instrument approaches
- Surface to overlying controlled airspace
- Communication not required for VFR
- Primarily for IFR operations

**2. Class E starting at 700 feet AGL** (magenta vignette/shading):
- Transition areas around airports
- Extends upward from 700 feet AGL
- Class G below 700 feet
- Common around airports

**3. Class E starting at 1,200 feet AGL** (no specific depiction):
- Most of the United States
- Unless depicted otherwise, Class E starts at 1,200 feet AGL
- Class G below 1,200 feet

**4. Class E starting at 14,500 feet MSL**:
- Over mountainous terrain
- Class G can extend much higher in these areas

**PPC Operations in Class E**:
- **NO CLEARANCE REQUIRED** for VFR operations
- No communication required
- Transponder not required (below 10,000 feet MSL)
- Must comply with VFR weather minimums

**Weather Minimums in Class E**:
- **Below 10,000 feet MSL**:
  - 3 statute miles visibility
  - 500 feet below clouds
  - 1,000 feet above clouds
  - 2,000 feet horizontal from clouds

**Part 103 Restriction**:
- Part 103 requires clear of clouds at all times
- More restrictive than standard Class E minimums
- Practically, operate in visual conditions well clear of clouds

**Practical Consideration**:
- Most PPC operations remain below 700-1,200 feet AGL
- Typically in Class G airspace
- Class E often overhead, not an operational concern

## Section 4: Special Use Airspace (SUA)

### Overview of Special Use Airspace

Special Use Airspace (SUA) is airspace where activities must be confined because of their nature, or where limitations are imposed on aircraft not part of those activities.

**Types**:
- Prohibited Areas
- Restricted Areas
- Warning Areas
- Military Operations Areas (MOAs)
- Alert Areas
- Controlled Firing Areas

**Why PPCs Must Know SUA**:
- Serious legal consequences for violations
- Physical danger (military operations, weapons firing)
- National security concerns
- Some are ALWAYS prohibited, others have active times

### Prohibited Areas

**Definition**: Airspace where flight is PROHIBITED for security or safety reasons.

**Examples**:
- P-56 (Camp David, Maryland)
- P-73 (White House/Capitol, Washington DC)
- Sensitive military installations
- Nuclear facilities

**Chart Depiction**:
- Blue hatching with "P-XX" designation
- Altitude limits shown

**PPC Operations**:
- **NEVER ENTER**
- No exceptions
- Severe legal and security consequences
- Plan routes to avoid entirely

### Restricted Areas

**Definition**: Airspace where flight is restricted during specific times due to hazardous activities.

**Activities**:
- Artillery firing
- Missile launches
- Military training
- Weapons testing

**Chart Depiction**:
- Blue hatching with "R-XXXX" designation
- Altitude limits shown

**PPC Operations**:
- **PROHIBITED** during active times
- Check NOTAMs for active times
- May be available when not active ("cold")
- Severe danger if entered when active

**How to Check Status**:
1. Check NOTAMs before flight
2. Call Flight Service (1-800-WX-BRIEF)
3. Check with local airport or FBO
4. Some have published schedules

**Consequences of Violation**:
- Physical danger (flying through weapons firing range)
- Legal consequences
- Possible interception
- Pilot certificate action (if applicable)

### Warning Areas

**Definition**: Airspace over domestic or international waters with hazardous activities.

**Location**: Typically offshore (beyond 3 nautical miles)

**Activities**: Similar to Restricted Areas (weapons firing, military exercises)

**Chart Depiction**:
- Blue hatching with "W-XXX" designation
- Over water

**PPC Operations**:
- Generally not a concern (PPCs don't fly over water)
- Same hazards as Restricted Areas if encountered
- Avoid when active

### Military Operations Areas (MOAs)

**Definition**: Airspace where military training activities occur.

**Activities**:
- Air combat maneuvering
- Formation flights
- Aerobatics
- High-speed operations

**Chart Depiction**:
- Magenta hatching with "MOA NAME"
- Altitude limits shown
- Boundary clearly depicted

**PPC Operations**:
- **NOT PROHIBITED**, but strongly discouraged when active
- VFR flight allowed
- Extreme caution required
- Military aircraft exempt from see-and-avoid rules
- High-speed, unpredictable traffic

**Recommended Practice**:
- Avoid MOAs when active
- Check NOTAMs for activation schedule
- If transiting, maintain vigilant lookout
- Consider flying around rather than through
- Use radio to contact controlling agency if available

**Safety Considerations**:
- Military jets may be traveling 400+ knots
- Combat maneuvering is unpredictable
- Slow PPCs are difficult for fast jets to see
- "See and avoid" breaks down with speed differential
- Not worth the risk

### Alert Areas

**Definition**: Airspace with high volume of pilot training or unusual aerial activity.

**Activities**:
- Student pilot training
- Glider operations
- Parachute jumping
- Aerobatic practice

**Chart Depiction**:
- Magenta hatching with "A-XXX" designation

**PPC Operations**:
- Flight is **ALLOWED**
- Extra vigilance required
- Expect high traffic density
- See and avoid responsibility remains

**Recommended Practice**:
- Heightened awareness
- Maintain slower speed near boundaries
- Look for traffic
- Use radio to announce position (if applicable)

### Controlled Firing Areas (CFAs)

**Definition**: Areas where activities could be hazardous to non-participating aircraft.

**Special Feature**: Activities cease when aircraft approach (radar surveillance)

**Chart Depiction**: NOT depicted on VFR charts (because activities stop when you approach)

**PPC Operations**: No specific restrictions (activities will cease)

## Section 5: Temporary Flight Restrictions (TFRs)

### What are TFRs?

Temporary Flight Restrictions are temporary restrictions on flight in specific areas for specific reasons.

**Duration**: Temporary (hours, days, or weeks)

**Why Issued**:
- Presidential movements
- Sporting events (stadiums)
- Disaster areas (wildfires, floods)
- National security events
- Space launches
- VIP movements
- Aerial demonstrations

### Types of TFRs

**1. Presidential TFRs**:
- Most restrictive
- Move with the President
- Inner ring: No flight allowed
- Outer ring: Restricted operations
- Constantly changing locations

**2. Stadium TFRs** (FAR 91.145):
- 3 nm radius, surface to 3,000 feet AGL
- Active 1 hour before to 1 hour after event
- Major League Baseball, NFL, NCAA Division 1 football
- 30,000+ attendance required

**3. Disaster TFRs**:
- Firefighting operations
- Search and rescue
- Humanitarian operations
- Keep airspace clear for emergency aircraft

**4. National Security TFRs**:
- Special security events
- Large gatherings (political conventions, inaugurations)
- Can be very large areas

**5. Space Operations TFRs**:
- Rocket launches
- Reentry operations
- Large areas around launch sites

### How to Check for TFRs

**CRITICAL**: TFRs can pop up with little notice. ALWAYS check before every flight.

**Methods to Check TFRs**:

**1. FAA TFR Website**:
- tfr.faa.gov
- Graphical display of all active TFRs
- Click for details
- Free, updated continuously

**2. Flight Service** (1-800-WX-BRIEF):
- Call for weather briefing
- FSS will inform you of TFRs along route
- Ask specifically about TFRs

**3. ForeFlight/Other Apps**:
- Display TFRs on moving map
- Alerts when approaching
- Updated in real-time

**4. NOTAM System**:
- TFRs issued as NOTAMs
- Text format
- Contains lat/long coordinates

**Best Practice**:
- Check TFRs before EVERY flight
- Check within 1 hour of departure
- Recheck if delayed
- Save/print TFR information
- Plan route to avoid

### Consequences of TFR Violations

**Legal Consequences**:
- FAA enforcement action
- Civil penalties up to $30,000+
- Criminal prosecution (intentional violations)
- Temporary flight restriction on your operations

**Security Consequences**:
- Military interception (fighter jets)
- Forced landing
- Armed law enforcement response
- Federal investigation

**Safety Consequences**:
- Interference with emergency operations
- Risk to public safety
- Risk to yourself

**Even Accidental Violations are Serious**:
- Ignorance is not a defense
- Pilot responsibility to check
- Penalties can still apply

**Real Example**: Multiple ultralight/Part 103 pilots have been intercepted by F-16 fighters for inadvertent TFR violations. The fighters escorted them to a forced landing where they were met by armed federal agents.

## Section 6: Chart Reading and Navigation

### VFR Sectional Charts

**What are Sectional Charts?**
- Primary chart for VFR navigation
- Scale: 1:500,000
- Updated every 6 months
- Show topography, airports, airspace, obstacles

**Chart Coverage**:
- Each sectional covers large geographic area
- Named after major city (e.g., "Seattle Sectional")
- Adjacent charts overlap
- Available in paper or digital format

**Information on Sectionals**:
- Terrain elevation and contours
- Cities and towns
- Highways and railroads
- Bodies of water
- Airports (public and private)
- Airspace boundaries
- Special use airspace
- Navigation aids
- Obstacles (towers, stacks)
- Maximum elevation figures (MEF)

### Reading Airspace on Charts

**Class B** (Solid Blue):
- Multiple rings/shelves
- Altitude shown as fractions: "70/SFC" = 7,000 feet to surface
- "80/40" = 8,000 feet to 4,000 feet
- Mode C veil shown (30 nm circle)

**Class C** (Solid Magenta):
- Two circles
- Altitude shown as fractions
- Outer shelf starts at 1,200 feet typically

**Class D** (Dashed Blue):
- Cylinder shape
- Altitude in box: "25" = 2,500 feet MSL
- Only when tower operating

**Class E Surface** (Dashed Magenta):
- Extensions around airports
- Surface to overlying airspace

**Class E 700 feet AGL** (Magenta Vignette/Shading):
- Fuzzy magenta shading
- Indicates Class E starts at 700 feet AGL
- Common around airports

**Class G**:
- No specific color
- Areas without magenta shading = Class E starts at 1,200 feet AGL
- Below that is Class G

**Special Use Airspace**:
- Blue or magenta hatching
- Designated with letters and numbers (R-2501, MOA NAME)
- Altitude limits shown

### Maximum Elevation Figures (MEF)

**What is MEF?**
- Large number in quadrant on chart
- Represents highest terrain or obstacle in that area
- Given in thousands and hundreds of feet MSL

**Example**: "37" means 3,700 feet MSL

**How MEF is Determined**:
- Highest terrain or obstacle in quadrant
- Rounded up to next hundred feet
- Plus 100 feet for obstacle, 200 feet for terrain
- Conservative estimate

**Use for PPCs**:
- Quick reference for terrain clearance
- Helps plan safe altitudes
- Avoid flying below MEF in unfamiliar terrain

### Terrain and Elevation

**Terrain Colors**:
- White/light tan: Low elevation (sea level to ~2,000 feet)
- Darker tan/brown: Higher elevation
- Various shades indicate elevation changes
- Contour lines show topography

**Why Important for PPCs**:
- Limited climb performance
- Need to avoid rising terrain
- Emergency landing site availability
- Wind patterns affected by terrain

**Using Terrain Information**:
- Plan routes through low terrain
- Avoid mountain passes unless experienced
- Identify safe landing areas (flat, open)
- Understand wind effects (rising terrain = updrafts/turbulence)

### Obstacles and Towers

**Chart Depiction**:
- Small symbols indicating obstacle type
- Height shown in feet MSL and AGL
- Example: "1534 (602)" = 1,534 MSL, 602 AGL
- Towers over 1,000 feet AGL shown in magenta

**Types**:
- Radio/TV towers
- Power plant stacks
- Wind turbines
- Buildings

**Why Critical for PPCs**:
- Low operating altitudes
- Guy wires nearly invisible
- Updrafts/downdrafts near structures
- Emergency landing considerations

**Best Practices**:
- Identify all towers along route
- Give wide berth (1/2 mile minimum)
- Be aware of guy wires extending far from tower
- Consider sun angle (towers harder to see with sun behind)

### Airports on Charts

**Public Airports**:
- Magenta circle (uncontrolled)
- Blue circle (towered)
- Hard-surface runway ≥ 8,069 feet: tick marks on circle
- Airport name and identifier

**Private Airports**:
- Magenta "R" in circle
- Not for public use without permission

**Airport Data**:
- Elevation
- Runway length (longest runway)
- Lighting available
- Control tower frequency (if applicable)

**Chart Supplement** (formerly A/FD):
- Complete airport information
- Frequencies, runway details, services
- NOTAMs and remarks
- Tower hours for Class D

**PPC Use**:
- Identify potential landing sites
- Know where traffic will be
- Avoid areas near airport traffic patterns
- Use private fields only with permission

## Section 7: Practical Airspace Decision-Making

### Pre-Flight Airspace Planning

**Steps for Every Flight**:

**1. Review Current Chart**:
- Ensure chart is current (check expiration date)
- Identify all airspace along route
- Note special use airspace
- Mark obstacles

**2. Check NOTAMs**:
- TFRs along route
- Restricted area activation times
- MOA activation schedules
- Airport closures or changes

**3. Check Weather**:
- Visibility and cloud clearances
- Will you be able to maintain Part 103 minimums?
- Winds aloft affecting altitude over terrain

**4. Plan Route**:
- Stay in Class G if possible
- Avoid Class B, C, D unless necessary
- Avoid restricted areas and MOAs when active
- Route around TFRs with margin
- Identify visual checkpoints

**5. Identify Outs**:
- Emergency landing sites
- Alternate routes if weather changes
- Where to land if conditions deteriorate

### In-Flight Airspace Awareness

**Maintain Situational Awareness**:
- Know your position at all times
- Identify landmarks and cross-reference with chart
- Monitor altitude (MSL and AGL)
- Track time and distance
- Watch for airspace boundaries ahead

**Tools for Awareness**:
- GPS (if equipped) - know position
- Chart on lap or accessible
- Altitude awareness (altimeter)
- Visual checkpoints (roads, towns, towers)
- Time and distance calculations

**Before Entering Different Airspace**:
- Positive identification of position
- Verification you're clear to enter
- Communication established (if required)
- Traffic scan complete

**If Uncertain of Position**:
- Do NOT proceed toward questionable airspace
- Identify known landmarks
- Backtrack to known position
- Climb for better visibility (if safe)
- Use GPS or other navigation aid
- Land and regroup if necessary

### Avoiding Airspace Violations

**Common Causes of Violations**:
1. Poor pre-flight planning
2. Map reading errors
3. Wind drift (off planned course)
4. Not checking for TFRs
5. Misidentifying position
6. Distraction
7. Equipment failure (GPS, altimeter)

**Prevention Strategies**:

**1. Conservative Planning**:
- Give airspace boundaries wide berth (1-2 miles)
- Don't plan to fly "right along the edge"
- Account for wind drift
- Build in margins for error

**2. Continuous Position Awareness**:
- Identify position every 5-10 minutes
- Use multiple methods (visual landmarks, GPS)
- Track on chart with pencil if possible
- Know distance from airspace boundaries

**3. Altitude Management**:
- Know MSL altitude (not just AGL)
- Set altimeter properly before flight
- Re-check altimeter periodically
- Stay well below airspace floors (500 feet margin)

**4. Weather Vigilance**:
- Monitor clouds and visibility
- Stay VFR with margins
- Turn back if conditions deteriorate
- Don't continue toward worsening weather

**5. Communication** (when required):
- Establish contact before entering
- Listen to frequency before calling
- Use proper phraseology
- Acknowledge and comply with instructions

**6. Technology Backup**:
- Use GPS if available
- Set proximity alerts for airspace
- But don't rely solely on technology
- Always have paper chart backup

### What to Do If You Accidentally Violate Airspace

**Immediate Actions**:

**1. Leave the Airspace Immediately**:
- Safest, most direct route out
- Don't compound the error

**2. If in Controlled Airspace** (B, C, D):
- Establish communication immediately
- Inform ATC of your position and intentions
- Follow instructions
- Apologize, explain briefly if asked

**3. After Landing**:
- Document the incident (time, location, circumstances)
- Review what went wrong
- Report if required (if intercepted or contacted by authorities)
- Learn from the mistake

**4. If Contacted by FAA**:
- Be honest and cooperative
- Explain circumstances
- Show your preparation (charts, planning)
- Demonstrate understanding of error
- Emphasize lessons learned

**NASA ASRS Report**:
- Aviation Safety Reporting System
- Voluntary, confidential safety report
- Can provide some protection from penalties (not immunity)
- Submit within 10 days of incident
- Available at asrs.arc.nasa.gov

**Penalties Can Include**:
- Warning letter
- Remedial training
- Civil penalties
- In extreme cases, criminal prosecution

**Best Defense**:
- Demonstrate you were trying to comply
- Show evidence of planning
- Explain what went wrong
- Commitment to not repeat

## Section 8: Communication Procedures

### When Communication is Required

**Part 103 ultralights are NOT required to have radios**, but communication is required to enter certain airspace:

**Class B Airspace**:
- Must have two-way radio
- Must receive clearance
- "Cleared to enter Class B airspace"

**Class C Airspace**:
- Must establish two-way radio communication
- Call before entering

**Class D Airspace**:
- Must establish two-way radio communication
- Call before entering

**Class E and G**:
- No communication required
- Radio helpful for safety

### Basic Radio Phraseology

**If you choose to equip with radio:**

**Calling ATC**:
1. Who you're calling: "[Facility name] [Type]"
2. Who you are: "Ultralight [location or identifier]"
3. Where you are: "[Direction] of [landmark], [altitude]"
4. What you want: "Request [your request]"

**Example**:
"Seattle Tower, Ultralight five miles south of Boeing Field, two thousand five hundred feet, request permission to transit your Class Delta airspace to the north."

**ATC Response**:
Listen for your call sign (or description). Once ATC says your call sign and gives you instructions, communication is established.

**Read Back**:
Read back:
- Clearances
- Hold short instructions
- Frequency changes
- Headings and altitudes

**Example**:
ATC: "Ultralight, Seattle Tower, cleared to transit Class Delta north of the field, maintain VFR, report exiting."

You: "Cleared to transit north of the field, maintain VFR, will report exiting, Ultralight."

**Exiting**:
"Seattle Tower, Ultralight is clear of your airspace to the north."

### Radio Equipment for PPCs

**Not Required** for Part 103 operations (unless entering controlled airspace)

**If Installing Radio**:

**VHF Aviation Radio**:
- 118.0 to 136.975 MHz range
- Handheld or panel-mount
- Battery or aircraft power

**Popular Handheld Models**:
- Yaesu FTA-750L
- Icom IC-A25
- Sporty's SP-400

**Considerations**:
- Weight (Part 103 limitations)
- Power source
- Antenna installation
- Headset compatibility
- Noise (engine and wind)

**Headsets**:
- Aviation headset with boom mic
- Noise-canceling recommended
- Lightweight for comfort

**Testing**:
- Test before relying on it
- Practice communications on ground
- Monitor frequency to learn procedures
- Consider handheld for flexibility

### Frequencies to Know

**Tower Frequencies**:
- Listed on sectional chart near airport
- Also in Chart Supplement

**Common Traffic Advisory Frequency (CTAF)**:
- Uncontrolled airports
- Pilots announce position and intentions
- Listed on chart (usually 122.8 or 123.0)

**Flight Service** (Radio):
- 122.2 MHz (standard FSS frequency)
- For weather briefings, NOTAMs

**Emergency**:
- 121.5 MHz (guard frequency)
- Monitored by ATC and many aircraft

**Multicom**:
- 122.9 MHz
- Air-to-air communication (not at airports)

## Section 9: Special Situations

### Flying Near Your Home Airport

**Know Your Local Airspace**:
- Class of airspace
- Airport traffic pattern altitude and direction
- Frequencies
- Instrument approaches (where Class E starts)

**Avoid Traffic Pattern**:
- PPCs typically don't operate from airports
- If near airport, avoid pattern altitudes
- Fly well to side of pattern, not underneath

**Coordination**:
- If regularly flying near airport, contact airport manager
- Inform them of your operations
- Establish communication if possible
- Build good relationship with local pilots

**Best Practices**:
- Operate away from airport when possible
- Avoid runway approaches and departures
- Maintain altitude below or above pattern (above is better)
- Monitor CTAF if equipped with radio
- Increase vigilance near airports

### Cross-Country Flights

**Extended Planning Required**:

**1. Chart Coverage**:
- Ensure you have all sectional charts for route
- Check expiration dates
- Study entire route

**2. Airspace Review**:
- Identify ALL airspace along route
- Note altitudes and boundaries
- Check for TFRs entire route
- Check NOTAMs for entire route

**3. Checkpoints**:
- Identify visual checkpoints every 5-10 miles
- Landmarks easy to identify from air
- Write down headings and distances

**4. Alternates**:
- Landing sites along route
- Escape routes if weather deteriorates
- Airports for emergency

**5. Weather**:
- Weather briefing for route and destination
- Winds aloft for each leg
- Forecast for arrival time

**6. Communication**:
- Frequencies for airports along route
- Flight Service frequencies
- Emergency contact plan

**7. Fuel**:
- Calculate fuel for entire route
- Plan fuel stops
- Account for headwinds
- Maintain reserve

**En Route**:
- Track progress on chart
- Mark position every 10-15 minutes
- Recalculate ETA
- Monitor weather ahead
- Confirm position at each checkpoint
- Don't continue if uncertain of position

### Operations Near Borders

**International Borders**:
- Canada, Mexico, maritime borders

**Restrictions**:
- Do NOT cross international borders in Part 103 aircraft
- Complex customs and immigration requirements
- Aircraft registration required
- Severe penalties for violations

**ADIZ (Air Defense Identification Zones)**:
- Areas near borders
- Special requirements for flight plans
- Part 103 generally cannot comply
- Avoid ADIZ unless fully compliant

**Best Practice**:
- Stay well away from international borders
- 10+ miles is prudent
- Know where borders are
- Sectional charts show international boundaries

### Flying in Congested Areas

**Part 103 Restriction**:
- Cannot operate over "congested areas"
- Congested area: any concentration of persons (cities, towns, settlements)

**What This Means**:
- No flying over cities or towns
- No flying over outdoor gatherings (fairs, races, sporting events)
- No flying over beaches with people
- No flying over highways with traffic

**Practical Application**:
- Operate in rural areas
- Fly well clear of populated areas
- If near town, maintain distance
- Conservative interpretation recommended

**Why This Rule Exists**:
- Engine failure could endanger people below
- No "glide" capability (wing collapses)
- Public safety

## Section 10: Summary and Best Practices

### Airspace Decision Flowchart

**Before Every Flight**:
1. ☑ Current sectional chart available?
2. ☑ Airspace along route identified?
3. ☑ TFRs checked (within 1 hour of flight)?
4. ☑ NOTAMs checked (restricted areas, MOAs)?
5. ☑ Route stays in Class G or uncontrolled airspace?
6. ☑ Clearance obtained if entering controlled airspace?
7. ☑ Radio available and tested (if needed)?
8. ☑ Backup plan if airspace becomes unavailable?

**If Answer is NO to Any**:
- Resolve the issue before flight
- Revise route or plans
- Delay flight if necessary

### Top 10 Airspace Rules for PPC Pilots

1. **Always operate in Class G airspace when possible** - it's simple, legal, and safe

2. **Check for TFRs before EVERY flight** - they can appear with little notice

3. **Give controlled airspace wide berth** - don't fly "right along the edge"

4. **Know your position at all times** - if uncertain, land and regroup

5. **Use current charts** - check expiration dates, update regularly

6. **Avoid congested areas** - Part 103 requirement, safety issue

7. **Plan conservatively** - margins for error, wind drift, altitude

8. **Respect special use airspace** - serious danger when active

9. **Communication when required** - if entering controlled airspace, establish contact

10. **When in doubt, stay out** - better to land early than violate airspace

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not checking for TFRs**
   - Always check, every flight

2. **Relying solely on GPS**
   - Technology fails, batteries die
   - Always have paper chart

3. **Poor map reading**
   - Practice chart reading on ground
   - Verify position frequently

4. **Ignoring winds**
   - Wind drift can push you off course
   - Account for wind in planning

5. **Entering controlled airspace "just for a minute"**
   - Always illegal without clearance
   - Not worth the risk

6. **Flying too close to airspace boundaries**
   - Give wide berth
   - Altitude and lateral margins

7. **Not knowing tower hours**
   - Class D reverts to E or G when tower closed
   - Check Chart Supplement

8. **Continuing when uncertain**
   - If lost, land
   - Don't guess your way into controlled airspace

## Conclusion

Airspace knowledge is fundamental to safe and legal powered parachute operations. While Part 103 ultralights enjoy freedom from many regulations, pilots still have the responsibility to understand and respect the airspace system.

**Key Takeaways**:
- **Class G airspace** is where PPCs primarily operate - uncontrolled, flexible, simple
- **Controlled airspace** (B, C, D) requires clearances and communication - avoid unless necessary
- **TFRs** can pop up anytime - always check before flight
- **Special use airspace** can be dangerous - respect boundaries and activation times
- **Chart reading** is essential - practice, stay current, verify position
- **Conservative decision-making** keeps you safe and legal

**Remember**: The goal is to enjoy flying while operating safely and legally within the National Airspace System. When in doubt, ask questions, seek training, and make conservative decisions. No flight is worth violating airspace regulations or putting yourself or others at risk.

**Fly smart. Fly safe. Fly legal.**
