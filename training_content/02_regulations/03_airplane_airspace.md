## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **Airspace Operations** chapter covers the class structure, special use airspace, TFRs, chart reading, and communication procedures — all of which apply to airplanes as written. This chapter adds the **airplane-specific equipment requirements and operating practice**: airplanes routinely operate into and out of controlled airspace, so the transponder/ADS-B rules and clearance procedures matter on essentially every flight near busier airports.

Sources: 14 CFR §§ 91.215, 91.225, 91.131, 91.130, 91.129 (eCFR); the *Aeronautical Information Manual* (AIM); and the *Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge* (FAA-H-8083-25C, airspace chapter). Where a requirement is nuanced, that is flagged.

## Transponder and ADS-B Out Equipment

Unlike a Part 103 ultralight, an airplane is expected to carry the equipment that controlled airspace requires.

### Transponder with Mode C (14 CFR § 91.215)
A Mode C transponder is required:
- In **Class A, B, and C** airspace;
- **Within the Class B Mode C veil** (30 nm of the primary Class B airport, surface to 10,000 ft MSL);
- **Above the ceiling and within the lateral boundaries of Class B/C up to 10,000 ft MSL**;
- **At and above 10,000 ft MSL** (excluding airspace at and below 2,500 ft AGL).

### ADS-B Out (14 CFR § 91.225)
ADS-B Out is required in essentially the **same airspace where a Mode C transponder is required** — Class A, B, and C; within the Class B Mode C veil; and at and above 10,000 ft MSL (excluding ≤ 2,500 ft AGL). *(Uncertainty flag: §§ 91.215 and 91.225 have specific carve-outs and altitude details — verify the exact wording in eCFR for your operation.)*

**Takeaway**: an airplane operating near Class B/C or above 10,000 ft MSL must have a working Mode C transponder **and** ADS-B Out. This is the single biggest day-to-day airspace difference from ultralight operations.

## Entering Controlled Airspace (Routine for Airplanes)

Airplanes commonly base at, transit, and land in controlled airspace. The entry rules from the shared chapter apply, with these airplane realities:

- **Class B (§ 91.131)**: an explicit ATC clearance ("Cleared into the Class Bravo") is required — *establishing communication is not enough*. A private pilot may operate to/from a primary Class B airport; student-pilot operations into certain busy Class B airports require a specific endorsement and some are prohibited entirely. *(Uncertainty flag: the list of student-prohibited Class B airports is in 14 CFR Part 91 Appendix D — verify currency.)*
- **Class C (§ 91.130)**: two-way radio communication must be **established** (ATC using your call sign) before entering; a clearance is not separately required.
- **Class D (§ 91.129)**: two-way communication established before entering the surface area when the tower is in operation; comply with traffic-pattern and tower instructions.

### Speed limits relevant to airplanes
- **Below 10,000 ft MSL**: maximum 250 knots indicated.
- **At or below 2,500 ft AGL within 4 nm of a Class C or D primary airport**: maximum 200 knots.
- **Beneath Class B shelves / in a VFR corridor through Class B**: maximum 200 knots.
*(14 CFR § 91.117 — verify against eCFR.)*

## VFR vs. IFR in Class E

For a VFR airplane, Class E behaves as in the shared chapter (no clearance/communication, standard VFR weather minimums). The difference at the Private level is awareness that **Class E is where most IFR traffic transitions** to and from the en-route structure — so a VFR airplane in Class E shares airspace with IFR arrivals/departures and must keep a vigilant lookout, especially in the vicinity of instrument approaches (Class E to the surface).

## VFR Cruising Altitudes

Above 3,000 ft AGL, VFR cruise altitude depends on magnetic course (14 CFR § 91.159):
- **0°-179°**: odd thousands + 500 ft (e.g. 3,500, 5,500).
- **180°-359°**: even thousands + 500 ft (e.g. 4,500, 6,500).
This hemispherical rule is an airplane operating reality at typical cruise altitudes; PPCs rarely reach it.

## Operating Practice for Airplane Pilots

1. **Equip and check** your transponder + ADS-B Out before flights near Class B/C or above 10,000 MSL.
2. **Get the clearance for Class B** — "cleared into the Bravo," not just two-way contact.
3. **Establish communication for Class C/D** before entering.
4. **Mind the speed limits** (250 below 10,000 MSL; 200 near C/D and under Class B shelves).
5. **Fly the hemispherical VFR cruising altitudes** above 3,000 ft AGL.
6. Everything in the shared chapter — TFR checks, SUA, conservative planning, position awareness — still applies.
