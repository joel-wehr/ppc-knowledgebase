# Weather Minimums and Decision-Making for Powered Parachute Pilots
## The PPC-Specific Numbers That Keep You Safe

---

## Why Weather Matters More for PPCs

Weather is the single most important factor in powered parachute flight safety. Unlike more robust aircraft, PPCs are highly sensitive to wind, turbulence, and atmospheric conditions. The general meteorology theory — how the atmosphere, wind, turbulence, clouds, thunderstorms, fog, stability, and fronts work — is covered in the shared Weather and Meteorology chapter. This chapter adds the **PPC-specific limits and framing** you must apply on top of that theory.

**Key Principle**: When in doubt about weather conditions, don't fly. No flight is worth compromising safety.

---

## Wind: The Critical Factor for PPCs

### The Low-Level Wind Gradient

The increase of wind speed with altitude in the first 100-200 feet is a **critical concept for PPC operations** because PPCs spend their entire flight in that shear-prone layer and climb through it on every takeoff. It is most pronounced with strong surface winds. A surface wind of 5 mph can be 12 mph at 100 feet — a gradient that creates significant shear and turbulence right where a PPC is most vulnerable.

The gradient wind above the friction layer (2,000+ feet AGL) is largely **less relevant to PPC operations**, since PPCs rarely operate that high.

### Crosswind Limits

- **Maximum recommended crosswind**: 5 mph for most PPC operations
- A crosswind requires a crab angle to maintain ground track and complicates both takeoff and landing
- Exceeding crosswind limits is dangerous on a PPC, which has no aerodynamic crosswind-correction authority comparable to a rudder-and-aileron airplane

### Recommended Wind Limits for PPCs

- **Student / New Pilots**: 5 mph maximum, no gusts
- **Experienced Pilots**: 8-10 mph, light gusts acceptable
- **Maximum for any operation**: 15 mph with no gust factor
- **Gusty conditions**: Reduce limits or cancel the flight

### Tailwind

**Never take off or land with a tailwind.** On a PPC the increase in takeoff distance and the loss of climb angle over the ground are dramatic, and landing distance increases significantly.

## PPC-Specific Hazards Within General Weather

### Wind Shear and Wing Collapse

Beyond the general dangers of wind shear (loss of airspeed, altitude loss, loss of control), a PPC faces an additional, category-specific danger: **wing collapse potential**. The ram-air canopy depends on steady airflow and internal pressure; a sudden shear or strong gust can partially or fully collapse the wing. This is why PPC wind and gust minimums are far tighter than for rigid-wing aircraft.

### Clouds with Vertical Development

Cumulus, towering cumulus, and cumulonimbus are the **most important cloud types for PPC pilots to recognize**, because the thermals and convective turbulence they mark are exactly the conditions a PPC cannot tolerate.

### VFR Cloud Clearance in Practice

PPCs operate almost entirely in **Class G airspace, day, below 1,200 feet AGL**, where the VFR rule is simply **clear of clouds** (14 CFR 91.155). Above 1,200 feet AGL in Class G during the day the requirement becomes **500 feet below, 1,000 feet above, and 2,000 feet horizontal** from clouds; controlled airspace is more restrictive. The full table is in the Airspace material — but for typical PPC operations, "stay clear of clouds and keep the surface in sight" is the practical rule.

### Stability and PPC Flying

- **Stable air** is generally good for PPC flying (if visibility is acceptable): smooth air, stratiform clouds.
- **Unstable air** is bad for PPC flying: turbulent, cumuliform clouds, thunderstorm potential — the kind of air a PPC should stay out of.

### Temperature Inversions — PPC Considerations

- Morning inversions = smooth air but often poor visibility
- A low-level inversion creates wind-shear potential at its boundary
- A morning inversion can be a good time to fly *if* visibility is acceptable

### Fog — PPC Operations

- **Never take off** into fog
- If fog develops while airborne: climb to stay above it (if safe), turn back if it's localized, or land immediately if it's extensive
- **Do not attempt to descend through fog** on a PPC

### Fronts — PPC Operations

- **Cold front**: don't fly near an approaching cold front (strong winds, turbulence, thunderstorms, wind shear). Good flying after it passes and weather clears.
- **Warm front**: don't fly in warm-front conditions (low clouds, fog, rain, poor visibility). Better flying after it passes.
- **Stationary front**: expect extended periods of unflyable weather; wait for it to move or dissipate.
- **Occluded front**: treat as severe weather and avoid flying.

### Winds Aloft for PPCs

Winds-aloft forecasts (FB) are generally **not critical** for PPC pilots because operations are low-altitude. They can still help you understand the wind gradient you'll climb through, and matter more if you fly at the higher end of PPC altitudes.

## PPC Personal Minimums

Develop your own standards, stricter than the regulatory minimums.

**Example Student / New Pilot Minimums**:
- Wind: 5 mph maximum, no gusts
- Visibility: 5 statute miles minimum
- Clouds: Ceiling 3,000 feet AGL or higher
- Temperature: Between 50-85°F
- Time of day: 2 hours after sunrise to 2 hours before sunset
- Weather trend: Stable or improving only

**Example Experienced Pilot Minimums**:
- Wind: 10 mph maximum, light gusts OK
- Visibility: 3 statute miles minimum
- Clouds: Ceiling 2,000 feet AGL or higher
- Temperature: Between 40-95°F
- Time of day: Sunrise to sunset (twilight if equipped)
- Weather trend: Not rapidly deteriorating

**Adjust for Conditions** (raise minimums): unfamiliar area, fatigue, carrying a passenger, or multiple risk factors stacking up.

### Go/No-Go: PPC Red Flags

In addition to the general no-go conditions (thunderstorms, approaching fronts, deteriorating conditions, low ceilings/visibility, temperature extremes, a bad feeling), the PPC-specific hard limits are:
- Winds exceeding your personal limits
- **Gusts exceeding 5 mph**

## Summary: PPC Weather Numbers

1. **Weather is the #1 safety factor for PPCs** — never compromise.
2. **Fly early or late** — avoid midday thermals and turbulence.
3. **Wind is critical** — maximum 15 mph, lower limits for less experienced pilots, max crosswind 5 mph.
4. **Gusts over 5 mph are a no-go** for students and new pilots.
5. **Always take off and land into the wind** — tailwind operations are dangerous.
6. **Stay away from thunderstorms** — minimum 20 miles, preferably more.
7. **A ram-air wing can collapse** in shear or strong gusts — this is why PPC limits are so tight.
8. **When in doubt, don't go out** — no flight is worth unnecessary risk.

**Remember**: Your life and the lives of any passengers depend on making sound, PPC-appropriate weather decisions every time you fly.
