## Wind and the Airplane

### Crosswind: the Demonstrated Crosswind Component

Unlike a powered parachute, an airplane corrects for crosswind aerodynamically — typically a **crab on final transitioning to a wing-low (sideslip) touchdown**, holding the longitudinal axis aligned with the runway using rudder while aileron controls drift (Airplane Flying Handbook, FAA-H-8083-3C, "Crosswind Approach and Landing").

Each airplane lists a **maximum demonstrated crosswind component** in its POH. Important nuances:
- It is the **maximum crosswind a test pilot demonstrated during certification** — it is **not a regulatory limit and not an aerodynamic maximum** (PHAK / AFH). A proficient pilot may legally exceed it; a student should treat it as a firm personal limit.
- Typical light single-engine trainers show a demonstrated crosswind component on the order of **12-17 knots**, but the exact figure is **airplane-specific — read your POH.** *(Uncertainty flag: do not memorize a single number; it varies by make/model.)*

This is a fundamental difference from PPC operations, where the recommended maximum crosswind is only a few mph.

### Wind Tolerance vs. Light Sport / PPC

Airplanes — being faster, heavier, and rigid-wing — tolerate considerably higher steady winds and gusts than powered parachutes or weight-shift aircraft. There is no fixed "airplane wind limit": the operative limits are the **demonstrated crosswind component**, the pilot's proficiency, gust spread, and runway alignment. Personal minimums still apply, and a student airplane pilot should set conservative wind and gust ceilings with their CFI.

### Gust Factor and Approach Speed

In gusty conditions, airplane technique calls for adding a margin to approach speed. A common guideline is to **add half the gust factor to the approach speed** (e.g., if the wind is 15 gusting 25 knots, add ~5 knots), per the Airplane Flying Handbook. *(Uncertainty flag: this is a widely taught rule of thumb; follow your POH and CFI guidance for your airplane.)* This guards against a stall when a gust suddenly drops the headwind component.

## Stalls, Wind Shear, and the Airplane

Wind shear is hazardous to every aircraft, but the airplane-specific failure mode differs from a PPC's wing collapse. In an airplane, a sudden loss of headwind (or a microburst's downdraft and tailwind shift) reduces airspeed toward the **stall**, and a low-altitude stall on approach or departure can be unrecoverable. Recognition and the decision to **go around / not attempt the approach** are the defenses (PHAK; AFH "Wind Shear").

## Structural Icing (Private-depth)

Powered parachutes do not fly in conditions where structural icing forms; airplane pilots must understand it.

- **Structural icing** requires visible moisture (clouds, rain, drizzle) **and** an aircraft surface temperature at or below freezing (PHAK, FAA-H-8083-25C, "Icing").
- **Types**: *rime* (rough, milky, from small supercooled droplets), *clear/glaze* (smooth, heavy, from large droplets — most dangerous), and *mixed*.
- **Effects**: ice disrupts airflow over the wing, **increases stall speed, increases drag, reduces lift and thrust, and adds weight** — performance degrades rapidly.
- **Most light training airplanes are not certified for flight into known icing (FIKI).** The correct response to inadvertent icing is to **exit the icing conditions** (change altitude or course, often a descent to warmer air or a climb above the layer if able) and land as soon as practical.
- **Carburetor icing** is a separate, common hazard in carbureted engines even in above-freezing, humid air; it is covered with airplane powerplant/systems but is a weather-driven risk — use carburetor heat per the POH.

*(Uncertainty flag: icing certification and procedures are airplane-specific — confirm against the POH and the Aviation Weather Handbook, FAA-H-8083-28.)*

## IFR Weather Awareness (Private-depth)

A Private Pilot — Airplane applicant must understand weather well enough to **stay VFR and recognize when to divert** (Private Pilot Airplane ACS, FAA-S-ACS-6C, Areas of Operation on Preflight Preparation — Weather Information). Key airplane-relevant points beyond the shared core:

- **Marginal VFR (MVFR) and IFR ceilings/visibility**: ceilings 1,000-3,000 ft and/or visibility 3-5 SM are MVFR; below that is IFR. A VFR-only pilot must not press into deteriorating conditions.
- **Inadvertent VFR-into-IMC** is a leading cause of fatal GA accidents; the defense is conservative personal minimums, a solid weather briefing, and a willingness to turn around.
- The deeper IFR weather material (icing in IMC, embedded thunderstorms, freezing-level analysis, ceiling/visibility forecasting for approaches) belongs to the **Instrument** track and is out of scope for the Private airplane student. *(This chapter intentionally stops at "recognize and avoid"; full IFR weather is Phase 4 / Instrument content.)*

## Density Altitude and the Airplane

High density altitude (hot, high, humid) reduces engine power, propeller thrust, and wing lift — lengthening takeoff roll and flattening climb. This is shared physics, but the airplane consequences are quantified with **POH takeoff/landing distance and climb charts** (see the airplane Performance chapter). Always run the numbers on a hot day at a high-elevation airport before committing to a takeoff.

## Summary: Airplane Weather Takeaways

1. **Crosswind is corrected aerodynamically** (crab → wing-low); respect the POH **demonstrated crosswind component** as a personal limit.
2. **Add half the gust factor** to approach speed in gusty conditions (rule of thumb — verify with your POH/CFI).
3. **Structural icing** degrades performance fast; most trainers are not FIKI — exit icing and land.
4. **Stay VFR**: recognize MVFR/IFR conditions and divert early — VFR-into-IMC is a killer.
5. **Run density-altitude performance numbers** from the POH on hot, high days.
