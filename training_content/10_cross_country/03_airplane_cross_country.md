## Airplane Cross-Country Characteristics

- **Speed**: a typical light single cruises **~90-130 kt** (≈ 100-150 mph), so a 100 nm leg is roughly an hour. *(Uncertainty flag: model-specific — use your POH.)*
- **Range**: commonly **3-5+ hours / 300-600+ nm** on full tanks, so airplanes routinely fly real cross-countries to distant airports.
- **Altitude**: typically cruise **3,000-10,000 ft MSL**, high enough to use the **VFR hemispherical cruising altitudes** (odd+500 eastbound / even+500 westbound above 3,000 AGL, 14 CFR § 91.159) and to consider winds aloft seriously.
- **Airports**: airplanes operate airport-to-airport, so destination/alternate planning, runway length, and pattern entry are central (vs. the land-anywhere flexibility of an ultralight).

## Fuel Planning and the VFR Reserve Rule

Airplane fuel planning uses the same formula (time × burn + reserve), but the **reserve is regulatory** for airplanes:
- **14 CFR § 91.151 (VFR)**: enough fuel to fly to the first point of intended landing and, assuming normal cruise, **30 minutes (day)** or **45 minutes (night)** beyond. *(Verify in eCFR.)*

Use the POH fuel-flow vs. power/altitude tables, correct groundspeed for winds aloft, and always plan a fuel alternate. Lean the mixture per the POH at cruise altitude to get book fuel flows.

## Performance Calculations (POH-Driven)

Unlike an ultralight's rules of thumb, an airplane cross-country uses **published POH charts**:
- **Takeoff and landing distance** charts corrected for weight, pressure altitude, temperature (density altitude), wind, and runway surface/slope — confirm the runway is adequate at *both* ends.
- **Cruise performance** tables for TAS and fuel flow at the chosen power setting and altitude.
- **Climb performance** for time, fuel, and distance to climb.
- **Weight and balance** computed with the loading graph / CG envelope — and rechecked as fuel burns (CG shifts).

## Navigation and the Cross-Country ACS

Pilotage, dead reckoning, and GPS are as in the shared chapter, with airplane realities:
- **Navigation log / flight plan**: the Private Airplane ACS expects a completed nav log with headings (true → magnetic → compass), groundspeeds, leg times, and fuel for each leg, plus a filed VFR flight plan and a weather briefing (FAA-S-ACS-6C).
- **VOR / GPS** course tracking and **lost-procedures** are tested; diversion to an alternate with a reasonable estimate of heading, time, and fuel is an ACS task.
- **§ 91.103** requires the PIC to become familiar with **all available information** for the flight (weather, runway lengths, takeoff/landing distance, alternatives).

## Airplane Engine-Out En Route

An airplane **glides** — establish **best-glide speed (V_G, from the POH)**, trim for it, pick the best field within glide range, run the engine-failure/restart flow, and execute. The detailed engine-failure procedure and best-glide figure are airplane-model-specific and are in the airplane emergencies chapter and your POH. (This is a key difference from a PPC, which lands essentially beneath itself.)

## Private Pilot Cross-Country Experience (context)

The Private Pilot — Airplane certificate requires specific cross-country experience, including a **solo cross-country of at least 150 nm total** with full-stop landings at three points and one leg of at least 50 nm straight-line distance (14 CFR § 61.109(a)). *(Uncertainty flag: confirm exact distances/landings in § 61.109.)* Sport Pilot — Airplane has its own, shorter cross-country requirements.
