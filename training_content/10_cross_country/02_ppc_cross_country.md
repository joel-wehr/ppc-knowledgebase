# Cross-Country in a Powered Parachute
## PPC Speeds, Ranges, and Numbers

---

## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **Cross-Country Operations** chapter covers the method — the six-step planning process, pilotage/dead-reckoning/GPS navigation, en-route procedures, and emergencies — and that method is the same for every aircraft. This chapter adds the **PPC-specific characteristics and numbers** you plug into it.

Cross-country flying in a powered parachute opens new horizons — literally. While PPCs are not typically used for long cross-country flights like conventional aircraft, flights of **25-100 miles** are certainly possible and rewarding.

---

## PPC Cross-Country Characteristics

**Slow speed**:
- **25-30 mph cruise** = long flight times
- **50 miles takes nearly 2 hours**
- Wind has a major impact on groundspeed

**Limited fuel**:
- Typical tank: **5-7 gallons**
- Fuel consumption: **2-3 gph**
- Range: **2-3 hours** flight time
- Practical range: **40-60 miles** with reserve

**Weather sensitive**:
- More affected by wind than faster aircraft
- Must maintain VFR (clear of clouds)
- Limited climb performance (can't fly over weather)

**Low-altitude operations**:
- Typically **500-1,500 feet AGL** (often 500-1,500 ft on cross-country)
- Better landmarks, but limited visibility ahead
- Terrain obstacles more significant

**Landing flexibility**:
- Can land almost anywhere (fields, roads) — no airport required
- Emergency landing options abundant

---

## PPC Fuel Planning Example

**Route**: 50 statute miles · **cruise (groundspeed)**: 25 mph · **flight time**: 50 ÷ 25 = **2.0 hours**.
**Fuel burn**: 2.5 gph · **fuel required**: 2.0 hr × 2.5 = **5.0 gallons**.
**Reserve**: 1.0 hour ≈ 2.5 gallons → **total needed 7.5 gallons**.
**Capacity**: 6 gallons → **not enough for a round trip with reserve.** Options: one-way with refuel at destination, a shorter route, an auxiliary tank within weight limits, or cancel.

---

## PPC Performance Numbers

- **Weight and balance**: fuel weighs **6 lb/gallon**; a typical PPC limit is around **500 lb** all-up. Example: pilot 180 + passenger 160 + 6 gal fuel (36 lb) = 376 lb, within a 500 lb limit.
- **Cruise**: TAS ~28 mph; groundspeed ~25 mph accounting for a headwind component.
- **Cruise power**: typically **5,500-6,000 RPM for a Rotax 582**; monitor EGT, CHT, and coolant temperature.
- **Takeoff/landing**: short — on the order of a few hundred feet at sea level; always check against your POH and the day's density altitude.

---

## PPC Engine-Out and Terrain

- **Best glide speed ≈ 25 mph** — establish it immediately on an engine failure, pick a field ahead within glide range, attempt a restart if altitude permits, then commit and flare.
- **Water**: a PPC is **not a seaplane** — landing in water means sinking. Avoid extended overwater flight; cross rivers/lakes at the narrowest point and higher altitude.
- A PPC has **no glide-to-the-airport reserve** like an airplane and an aggressive maneuver can collapse the wing, so "always be within reach of a survivable landing area" is a hard planning rule.

---

## PPC Time-of-Day and Night

Best flying is morning (cool, light winds, less turbulence); avoid midday thermals and the wind shifts near sunset. **Night** sport-pilot PPC flight is now permitted under MOSAIC (§ 61.329) with the required training, endorsement, and medical path — see the certification material; plan night cross-country only once night-qualified and current.

## A PPC Note

"Time to spare, go by air. Time to spare, go by PPC!" The slow speed, low altitude, and open cockpit give an intimate connection with the landscape that faster aircraft can't match — the journey is the destination.
