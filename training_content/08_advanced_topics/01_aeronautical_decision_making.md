# Aeronautical Decision Making and Risk Management
## Advanced Decision-Making for Powered Parachute Pilots

---

## Introduction

Aeronautical Decision Making (ADM) is the systematic approach to the mental process used by pilots to consistently determine the best course of action in response to circumstances. Good decision-making is the cornerstone of safe flying - more accidents are caused by poor decisions than by lack of skill or knowledge.

This module provides comprehensive training in:
- Risk assessment and management
- Decision-making models and frameworks
- Human factors affecting decisions
- Situational awareness
- Practical decision-making scenarios
- Personal minimums development
- Continuous improvement

**Key Principle**: Most aviation accidents are preventable. They result from a chain of poor decisions, each of which could have been broken by better decision-making.

---

## Section 1: The Nature of Aeronautical Decision Making

### What is ADM?

Aeronautical Decision Making is a systematic approach to:
- **Identify** personal attitudes hazardous to safe flight
- **Learn** behavior modification techniques
- **Learn** how to recognize and cope with stress
- **Develop** risk assessment skills
- **Use** all resources in a multicrew (or solo) environment
- **Evaluate** the effectiveness of one's ADM skills

**For Powered Parachute Pilots**, ADM is particularly important because:
- You're often flying alone (no copilot to catch errors)
- Limited performance envelope (slow speed, limited climb)
- Weather-dependent operations (more weather decisions)
- No "instruments" to fall back on (VFR only)
- Engine failure = immediate landing (no "glide to airport")

### The Decision-Making Process

**Classic Decision-Making Steps**:

1. **Detect** - Recognize that a change has occurred
2. **Estimate** - Assess the significance of the change
3. **Choose** - Determine a course of action
4. **Identify** - Select the best option
5. **Do** - Implement the chosen action
6. **Evaluate** - Monitor and assess the outcome

**Example - Weather Deteriorating**:
1. **Detect**: Notice clouds lowering, visibility decreasing
2. **Estimate**: Conditions may go below VFR minimums soon
3. **Choose**: Options are turn back, land now, or continue
4. **Identify**: Land now is safest option
5. **Do**: Execute approach to nearest suitable field
6. **Evaluate**: After landing, assess decision - was it timely? Safe?

### Time-Critical vs. Non-Critical Decisions

**Time-Critical Decisions** (seconds to minutes):
- Engine failure on takeoff
- Sudden wind shift
- Obstacle appears ahead
- Weather suddenly deteriorates

**Response**:
- Trained responses (checklists, procedures)
- Quick assessment
- Decisive action
- No time for lengthy analysis

**Non-Critical Decisions** (minutes to hours):
- Pre-flight go/no-go
- Route planning
- Fuel stops
- Maintenance scheduling

**Response**:
- Thorough analysis
- Consult resources
- Consider all options
- Make informed, unhurried decision

**Key Difference**:
- Non-critical decisions made with time allow you to avoid time-critical emergencies
- Good pre-flight decision-making prevents in-flight emergencies

## Section 2: Risk Management

### Understanding Risk

**Risk** = Likelihood of Event × Severity of Consequences

**In Aviation**:
- **Likelihood**: How probable is the hazard to occur?
- **Severity**: How bad would the outcome be if it did occur?

**Example - Flying in High Winds**:
- **Likelihood**: High (if forecast is for strong winds)
- **Severity**: High (loss of control, structural damage, injury)
- **Risk**: UNACCEPTABLE - Don't fly

**Example - Flying with Adequate Fuel Reserve**:
- **Likelihood**: Low (well-planned flight, known fuel consumption)
- **Severity**: Low (extra weight, minor performance impact)
- **Risk**: ACCEPTABLE - Carry the reserve

### The Risk Management Process

**5-Step Process**:

**1. Identify Hazards**:
- What could go wrong?
- Systematically review all aspects of flight
- Weather, aircraft, pilot, environment

**2. Assess Risk**:
- For each hazard, determine likelihood and severity
- Use risk matrix (see below)

**3. Analyze Risk Control Measures**:
- What can be done to reduce or eliminate the risk?
- Controls might eliminate, reduce, or accept risk

**4. Make Control Decisions**:
- Choose which controls to implement
- Balance risk vs. benefit
- Accept, mitigate, or reject

**5. Implement Controls and Supervise**:
- Put controls in place
- Monitor effectiveness
- Adjust as needed

### Risk Assessment Matrix

**Likelihood** (across top):
- **Probable**: Expected to occur
- **Occasional**: Likely to occur
- **Remote**: Unlikely but possible
- **Improbable**: Very unlikely

**Severity** (down side):
- **Catastrophic**: Death, total loss
- **Critical**: Severe injury, major damage
- **Marginal**: Minor injury, minor damage
- **Negligible**: No injury, minimal damage

**Risk Levels**:
- **Red (High)**: UNACCEPTABLE - Don't proceed
- **Yellow (Medium)**: CAUTION - Mitigate risk, management decision
- **Green (Low)**: ACCEPTABLE - Monitor

**Example Application - Weather Decision**:

**Scenario**: Winds forecast 15 mph, gusting to 22 mph

**Hazard**: Loss of control in high winds

**Assessment**:
- **Likelihood**: Probable (winds forecast to be above limits)
- **Severity**: Critical (potential crash, injury)
- **Risk Level**: **HIGH (RED)** - Do not fly

**Control Measure**: Wait for better conditions

**Revised Risk**: By not flying, risk eliminated

### The PAVE Checklist

**PAVE** is a risk assessment framework examining four areas:

**P - Pilot**:
- Illness, medication, stress, fatigue
- Currency, proficiency, experience
- Personal minimums
- "IMSAFE" factors

**A - Aircraft**:
- Airworthiness
- Maintenance status
- Equipment
- Fuel, oil, coolant

**V - enVironment**:
- Weather (current and forecast)
- Terrain
- Airspace
- Airport/field conditions

**E - External Pressures**:
- Time pressure ("get-there-itis")
- Social pressure (passengers, friends waiting)
- Financial pressure (event, commitment)
- Self-imposed pressure (ego, proving yourself)

**How to Use PAVE**:
1. Before each flight, assess each category
2. Identify risks in each area
3. Evaluate combined risk
4. Make go/no-go decision based on total risk

**Example PAVE Assessment**:

**Pilot**:
- Well-rested, healthy ✓
- Current and proficient ✓
- No stress ✓
- → Low risk

**Aircraft**:
- Pre-flight complete ✓
- No discrepancies ✓
- Fuel adequate ✓
- → Low risk

**enVironment**:
- Winds 12 mph, gusting 18 (marginal)
- Clouds 2,500 feet scattered ✓
- Visibility 10 miles ✓
- → Medium risk (winds)

**External Pressures**:
- Friends waiting to watch (mild pressure)
- No time constraints ✓
- → Low-medium risk

**Combined Assessment**:
- One medium risk factor (winds near limits)
- Mild external pressure
- **Decision**: If winds are at top of personal minimums and there's any external pressure, consider delaying. Remove external pressure factor by explaining decision, or wait for winds to decrease.

### The 3P Model (Perceive, Process, Perform)

**Perceive** - Identify Hazards:
- Systematically observe the situation
- Use PAVE or other framework
- Look for deviations from normal
- Trust your instincts (if something feels wrong, investigate)

**Process** - Assess Risk:
- Evaluate each hazard
- Determine risk level
- Identify risk controls
- Make decision

**Perform** - Implement and Monitor:
- Execute the decision
- Continuously monitor
- Reassess as situation changes
- Be willing to change decision if needed

**Continuous Cycle**:
- Not a one-time process
- Continuously perceive, process, perform throughout flight
- Especially important when conditions change

**Example - In-Flight Weather Decision**:

**Perceive**:
- Notice clouds lowering ahead
- Visibility appears to be decreasing
- Wind picking up

**Process**:
- Clouds may go below legal minimums
- Visibility may drop below 3 miles
- Wind may exceed my limits
- Options: Turn back, land now, continue and monitor
- Risk of continuing: Getting trapped in deteriorating weather

**Perform**:
- Decision: Land at next suitable field
- Execute: Identify field, enter pattern, land
- Monitor: After landing, observe weather - was decision correct?

## Section 3: Human Factors in Decision Making

### The IMSAFE Checklist

Personal fitness assessment before every flight:

**I - Illness**:
- Any symptoms of illness?
- Cold, flu, nausea, headache?
- Impaired judgment or reaction time?
- **If yes**: Don't fly

**M - Medication**:
- Taking any medications?
- Do they cause drowsiness, dizziness, impairment?
- Even over-the-counter meds can be impairing
- **If yes to impairing effects**: Don't fly

**S - Stress**:
- Work stress, family issues, financial problems?
- Distracted thinking about problems?
- Unable to focus fully on flying?
- **If high stress**: Don't fly or fly conservatively

**A - Alcohol**:
- Any alcohol in last 24 hours?
- Part 103 requires NO alcohol before flight
- Residual effects can last longer than you think
- **If any alcohol in system**: Don't fly

**F - Fatigue**:
- Did you get adequate sleep (7-8 hours)?
- Feeling tired or drowsy?
- Decreased alertness or reaction time?
- **If fatigued**: Don't fly

**E - Eating (or Emotion)**:
- Have you eaten recently?
- Low blood sugar impairs judgment
- Strong emotions (anger, excitement, sadness)?
- **If hungry or emotional**: Address before flying

**Application**:
- Run through IMSAFE before every flight
- Be honest with yourself
- One "yes" may be acceptable with mitigation
- Multiple "yes" answers = Don't fly

### Hazardous Attitudes

The FAA identifies five hazardous attitudes that contribute to poor decision-making:

**1. Anti-Authority**:
- "Don't tell me what to do!"
- Resentment of rules and regulations
- Belief that rules don't apply to you

**Example**: "Part 103 says stay clear of clouds, but I'll just duck through this thin layer - no big deal."

**Antidote**: "Follow the rules. They're usually right."

**2. Impulsivity**:
- "Do something - quickly!"
- Acting without thinking
- Failure to consider consequences

**Example**: "Looks like weather is moving in - I'll just take off quickly before it gets here" (without checking forecast, doing preflight, etc.)

**Antidote**: "Not so fast. Think first."

**3. Invulnerability**:
- "It won't happen to me."
- Belief that accidents happen to others, not you
- Underestimating risk

**Example**: "Other pilots might crash in high winds, but I'm good enough to handle it."

**Antidote**: "It could happen to me."

**4. Macho**:
- "I can do it!"
- Taking unnecessary risks to prove something
- Showing off

**Example**: "My friends are watching - I'll show them how well I can handle this gusty wind."

**Antidote**: "Taking chances is foolish."

**5. Resignation**:
- "What's the use?"
- Belief that you have no control
- Leaving decisions to others or fate

**Example**: "The weather looks bad, but I already told people I'd fly today, so I guess I have to."

**Antidote**: "I'm not helpless. I can make a difference."

**Self-Assessment**:
- Which attitudes do you recognize in yourself?
- When have you exhibited these attitudes?
- Practice recognizing them and using antidotes
- Ask trusted friends/instructors for honest feedback

### Stress and Decision Making

**Types of Stress**:

**Acute Stress** (sudden, short-term):
- Engine failure
- Sudden weather change
- Emergency situation

**Effects**:
- Tunnel vision (focus narrows)
- Reduced cognitive function
- Increased reaction time
- Possible panic

**Management**:
- Training (procedural responses)
- Deep breath, focus
- Fly the aircraft first
- Use checklists

**Chronic Stress** (ongoing, long-term):
- Work problems
- Family issues
- Financial concerns
- Health worries

**Effects**:
- Distraction
- Reduced attention
- Poor decision-making
- Increased errors

**Management**:
- Recognize when stressed
- Postpone flight if highly stressed
- Focus techniques
- Address sources of stress

**Optimal Stress Level**:
- Some stress enhances performance (alertness, focus)
- Too little stress = complacency, boredom
- Too much stress = degraded performance
- Aim for moderate stress (engaged but not overwhelmed)

### Fatigue

**Types of Fatigue**:

**Acute Fatigue** (short-term):
- From single activity or period
- Lack of sleep last night
- Long day
- Physical exertion

**Chronic Fatigue** (cumulative):
- Accumulated over days/weeks
- Ongoing sleep deprivation
- Sustained high workload
- Burnout

**Effects on Decision Making**:
- Slower reaction time
- Reduced attention and vigilance
- Impaired judgment
- Decreased motivation
- Increased risk-taking

**Symptoms**:
- Yawning, drowsiness
- Difficulty focusing
- Irritability
- Forgetting things
- Making simple mistakes

**Prevention**:
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
- Regular sleep schedule
- Avoid flying when tired
- Take breaks during long days
- Listen to your body

**If Fatigue Sets In**:
- Land as soon as practical
- Take a break
- Nap if possible
- Don't continue flying while fatigued

### Get-There-Itis (Plan Continuation Bias)

**Definition**: The tendency to continue a plan despite changing circumstances that make it unwise.

**Also Called**:
- Plan continuation bias
- Press-on-itis
- Target fixation

**Why It Happens**:
- Emotional investment in the plan
- External pressures (people waiting, event scheduled)
- Ego (don't want to "give up")
- Optimism bias (hoping it will work out)
- Sunk cost fallacy (already committed time/money)

**Warning Signs**:
- Continuing despite deteriorating weather
- Pressing on despite mechanical issues
- Ignoring personal fatigue or illness
- Rationalizing deviations from personal minimums
- Thinking "I've come this far, might as well continue"

**Prevention**:
- Recognize external pressures before flight
- Have abort criteria predetermined
- Make conservative decisions early
- Remember: You can always fly another day

**Antidote**:
- "Landing early is better than landing late (in bad conditions)"
- "There's no prize for getting there, but there are consequences for crashing"
- Build in flexibility (don't commit to rigid schedules)

**Example**:
You planned a flight to a fly-in 50 miles away. Friends are meeting you there. Halfway, weather is deteriorating - clouds lowering, visibility decreasing.

**Get-there-itis response**: "I'm halfway there, friends are waiting, I'll just continue and hope weather holds."

**Good ADM response**: "Weather is deteriorating. I'll land now at this field, call my friends, and drive the rest of the way. I can fly back when weather improves."

## Section 4: Situational Awareness

### What is Situational Awareness?

**Definition**: Knowing what's going on around you at all times.

**Three Levels**:

**Level 1 - Perception**:
- Sensing cues in the environment
- "What is happening?"
- Observing instruments, weather, aircraft behavior

**Level 2 - Comprehension**:
- Understanding the meaning of cues
- "What does this mean?"
- Interpreting what you're sensing

**Level 3 - Projection**:
- Predicting future states
- "What will happen next?"
- Anticipating future situations

**Example - Weather Situational Awareness**:

**Level 1** (Perception):
- See clouds building to the west
- Notice wind increasing
- Observe temperature dropping

**Level 2** (Comprehension):
- Building cumulus clouds = convective activity
- Increasing wind = front approaching
- Temperature drop = cold front

**Level 3** (Projection):
- Thunderstorms likely to develop in 30-60 minutes
- Wind will continue increasing
- Conditions will deteriorate

**Decision**: Land soon, before weather arrives

### Maintaining Situational Awareness

**OODA Loop** (Boyd's Decision Cycle):

**O - Observe**:
- Gather information
- Scan instruments
- Look outside
- Listen to engine
- Feel aircraft response

**O - Orient**:
- Interpret information
- Relate to current situation
- Understand context

**D - Decide**:
- Determine best course of action
- Choose among options

**A - Act**:
- Implement decision
- Execute actions

**Then Loop Back** to Observe:
- Continuous cycle
- Never stops
- Always reassessing

**Application to PPC Flying**:

**Observe**:
- Every 10-15 seconds: Scan instruments (RPM, EGT, CHT, alt, airspeed)
- Every 30 seconds: Check position, landmarks
- Continuously: Look for traffic, weather changes, terrain

**Orient**:
- Interpret: "RPM is good, temps normal, on course, weather stable, no traffic"

**Decide**:
- "Continue as planned" or "Adjust course" or "Land"

**Act**:
- Execute decision

**Repeat**

### Threats to Situational Awareness

**1. Task Saturation**:
- Too many things happening at once
- Overloaded
- Can't process all information

**Example**: Trying to navigate, avoid weather, watch for traffic, monitor instruments, and talk on radio simultaneously

**Solution**:
- Prioritize: Fly aircraft first
- Simplify: Reduce workload (land if overwhelmed)
- Delegate: If passenger, have them watch for traffic

**2. Distraction**:
- Attention diverted from flying
- Focusing on one thing to exclusion of others

**Example**: Fixating on GPS or chart, not looking outside

**Solution**:
- Timebox tasks (look at chart for 10 seconds, then back outside)
- Use checklists to stay on track
- "Fly the aircraft" reminder

**3. Fixation**:
- Locking onto one source of information
- Ignoring other cues

**Example**: Focusing only on wind, ignoring fuel state

**Solution**:
- Systematic scan
- Force yourself to check all instruments/conditions
- Ask: "What am I not looking at?"

**4. Complacency**:
- Overconfidence in routine situations
- "Automation" of thinking
- Not actively monitoring

**Example**: Flying same route many times, not checking weather

**Solution**:
- Treat every flight as unique
- Use checklists even for routine flights
- Stay engaged

**5. Fatigue**:
- Reduced vigilance
- Missing cues
- Slower processing

**Solution**: (see fatigue section above)

### Recovering Lost Situational Awareness

**Signs You've Lost SA**:
- "How did I get here?"
- Uncertain of position
- Confused about what's happening
- Behind the aircraft (reacting, not anticipating)

**Recovery Steps**:

**1. Fly the Aircraft**:
- Maintain control
- Level flight, safe altitude
- Don't make situation worse

**2. Simplify**:
- Reduce task load
- Stop non-essential tasks
- Focus on basics

**3. Reassess**:
- What do I know for certain?
- What don't I know?
- What information do I need?

**4. Rebuild SA**:
- Systematic scan
- Reorient yourself
- Check position, fuel, weather, aircraft status

**5. Decide**:
- Based on current situation (not old plan)
- Conservative choice
- Landing is always an option

**Example**:
You're confused about your position - landmarks don't match chart.

**1. Fly Aircraft**: Maintain altitude and heading, don't wander
**2. Simplify**: Stop trying to navigate, focus on safe flight
**3. Reassess**: What do I know? I have fuel for 1 hour. Weather is VFR. Aircraft is operating normally.
**4. Rebuild**: Look for major landmarks (highway, town, lake). Consult GPS if available. Climb for better visibility.
**5. Decide**: If unable to reorient in 10 minutes, land in safe field and figure it out on the ground.

## Section 5: Practical Decision-Making Scenarios

### Pre-Flight Go/No-Go Decisions

**Scenario 1 - Marginal Weather**:

**Situation**:
- Forecast: Winds 10 mph, gusting to 16 mph
- Current: Winds 8 mph, gusting to 12 mph
- Your personal max: 12 mph sustained, 15 mph gusts
- Flight duration: 1 hour local

**Analysis**:
- Current conditions acceptable
- Forecast shows increasing winds
- Winds may exceed your limits during flight
- Worsening trend

**Decision Options**:
A. Go now, fly shortened flight, return before winds increase
B. Wait and see if winds actually increase
C. Don't go - forecast shows exceeding limits

**Best Decision**: **A or C**
- **A** if you can complete flight before forecast increase time, have outs (landing sites), and will commit to landing if winds increase
- **C** if forecast timing is uncertain or you're uncomfortable

**Poor Decision**: **B** (waiting while hoping) - reactive, not proactive

---

**Scenario 2 - Mechanical Discrepancy**:

**Situation**:
- During run-up, notice EGT on one cylinder 100°F higher than normal
- Engine seems to run fine otherwise
- You're at home field, friends want to fly
- Weather is perfect

**Analysis**:
- EGT deviation indicates possible problem (lean mixture, ignition, exhaust leak)
- Engine "seems fine" is not diagnostic
- Friends waiting = external pressure
- Perfect weather = temptation

**Decision Options**:
A. Go fly - it's probably nothing
B. Do additional diagnostics (mag check, look for exhaust leak)
C. Don't fly until problem is identified and fixed

**Best Decision**: **C**, possibly **B** if you can diagnose and fix immediately
- Engine anomalies should be investigated
- Not worth risking engine failure for a recreational flight

**Poor Decision**: **A** - ignoring mechanical warning sign

---

**Scenario 3 - Personal Fitness**:

**Situation**:
- You slept poorly (4 hours)
- Feeling tired but not exhausted
- You're scheduled to fly with a friend (external pressure)
- It's been 3 weeks since you last flew

**Analysis (IMSAFE)**:
- **I**llness: None
- **M**edication: None
- **S**tress: None significant
- **A**lcohol: None
- **F**atigue: YES - only 4 hours sleep
- **E**ating: Okay

**Decision Options**:
A. Fly as planned - you can handle it
B. Fly a shorter, simpler flight
C. Postpone until better rested

**Best Decision**: **C**
- Fatigue degrades decision-making and reaction time
- 4 hours sleep is significantly inadequate
- Risk of in-flight errors

**Acceptable Alternative**: **B** if friend can fly the aircraft and you're passenger-only (remove pilot responsibility)

**Poor Decision**: **A** - flying impaired by fatigue

---

### In-Flight Decision Scenarios

**Scenario 4 - Deteriorating Weather**:

**Situation**:
- 20 minutes into cross-country flight
- Clouds ahead lowering
- Visibility decreasing
- Still legal (3 miles vis, clear of clouds)
- Destination is 30 minutes ahead

**Analysis (3P)**:
- **Perceive**: Weather ahead is worsening
- **Process**: May go below VFR minimums before reaching destination. Options: Continue, turn back, land now, divert around.
- Risk: Getting caught in IMC
- **Perform**: ?

**Decision Options**:
A. Continue - you can make it
B. Turn back now to better conditions
C. Land at nearest suitable site
D. Divert around weather

**Best Decision**: **C** (land now) or **B** (turn back)
- Weather deteriorating = unsafe to continue
- Don't count on reaching destination before conditions worsen

**Acceptable**: **D** if diversion clearly avoids weather and doesn't add significant time/distance

**Poor Decision**: **A** - hoping to outrun weather

---

**Scenario 5 - Engine Running Rough**:

**Situation**:
- In flight, 800 feet AGL
- Engine starts running rough
- RPM drops 200
- EGT fluctuating
- Still producing power, not quitting

**Analysis**:
- Engine problem developing
- May worsen or fail completely
- Altitude gives some time but not much
- Need to land soon

**Decision Options**:
A. Continue to destination - engine is still running
B. Land immediately in best available field
C. Troubleshoot while flying (check fuel, switch mags, etc.)

**Best Decision**: **B** (land immediately)
- Land while you still have power and control
- Engine may fail completely at any moment

**Acceptable**: **C** only if you simultaneously set up for immediate landing (heading toward good field, descending, preparing)

**Poor Decision**: **A** - continuing with sick engine

---

**Scenario 6 - Lost/Uncertain Position**:

**Situation**:
- Flying cross-country
- Landmarks don't match chart
- Not sure of position
- Fuel for 45 more minutes
- Weather is good

**Analysis**:
- Situational awareness lost
- Fuel is limited but adequate for decision-making
- Weather gives you time

**Decision Options**:
A. Continue on current heading - hoping to see landmark
B. Circle area looking for landmarks
C. Land in safe field, figure out position on ground
D. Climb for better view (if safe)

**Best Decision**: **D** first (if altitude allows), then **C** if still uncertain
- Climbing improves visibility and radio range
- If can't reorient within 10 minutes, land

**Acceptable**: **B** if combined with **D** and time-limited

**Poor Decision**: **A** - aimless continuation while lost wastes fuel and compounds problem

---

### Post-Flight Decision Evaluation

After every flight (especially after difficult decisions):

**Debrief Questions**:
1. What decisions did I make?
2. What information did I base them on?
3. What was the outcome?
4. Would I make the same decision again?
5. What would I do differently?
6. What did I learn?

**Example Debrief**:

**Flight**: Local flight, returned early due to winds increasing

**Decisions**:
- Took off with winds near my personal maximum
- Monitored winds in flight
- Landed after 20 minutes when winds exceeded limits

**Information**:
- Pre-flight forecast and current winds
- In-flight observation of windsock, aircraft behavior
- Personal minimums

**Outcome**:
- Safe flight, conservative decision to land early

**Same Decision Again?**
- Yes on landing early
- Maybe on taking off - winds were already near limits

**Differently**:
- Next time, if winds are near max before takeoff, don't go
- Better to scrub flight than land early

**Learned**:
- Conservative pre-flight decisions avoid in-flight pressure to land
- Personal minimums should be adhered to strictly before takeoff

## Section 6: Developing Personal Minimums

### What are Personal Minimums?

**Definition**: Self-imposed limitations that are more restrictive than regulations, based on personal experience and comfort.

**Why Personal Minimums?**
- Regulations are minimum standards - not necessarily safe for everyone
- Your experience, currency, and proficiency vary
- Personal minimums ensure you fly within YOUR capabilities

**Categories of Personal Minimums**:
- Weather (wind, visibility, clouds, temperature)
- Aircraft (equipment requirements, maintenance status)
- Pilot (currency, rest, stress)
- Environment (field size, terrain, congestion)

### Setting Personal Minimums

**Process**:

**1. Assess Your Experience**:
- Total hours
- Hours in type
- Recent experience (last 30/60/90 days)
- Training received
- Types of conditions experienced

**2. Identify Comfort Levels**:
- What conditions make you uncomfortable?
- When have you felt task-saturated?
- What weather have you successfully handled?
- What was too challenging?

**3. Set Conservative Limits**:
- Start more restrictive than you think necessary
- Build in margins
- Leave room for improvement as experience grows

**4. Document Minimums**:
- Write them down
- Review before every flight
- Share with instructor or mentor

**5. Adjust with Experience**:
- As you gain experience, revisit minimums
- Relax limits gradually
- NEVER relax limits due to external pressure

### Example Personal Minimums (Beginning PPC Pilot)

**Weather**:
- Wind: 8 mph maximum, 10 mph gusts maximum
- Visibility: 5 miles minimum
- Ceiling: 2,500 feet AGL minimum (well above Class G requirement)
- Temperature: 45°F to 85°F
- No approaching weather systems
- No fog, mist, or low clouds
- Time: 2 hours after sunrise to 2 hours before sunset

**Aircraft**:
- Pre-flight completed with no discrepancies
- No recent maintenance issues
- Fuel for flight + 1 hour reserve
- All instruments functioning

**Pilot**:
- Minimum 7 hours sleep night before
- No alcohol in last 48 hours
- No stress or distractions
- Current (flown within last 21 days)
- Completed at least 3 landings in last 21 days

**Environment**:
- Familiar field/area (within 10 miles of home base)
- Clear of controlled airspace
- No TFRs
- No congested areas

### Example Personal Minimums (Experienced PPC Pilot)

**Weather**:
- Wind: 15 mph maximum, 18 mph gusts maximum
- Visibility: 3 miles minimum (Part 103 requirement)
- Ceiling: No specific limit (as long as clear of clouds)
- Temperature: 35°F to 95°F
- No thunderstorms within 20 miles
- Time: 1 hour after sunrise to 1 hour before sunset

**Aircraft**:
- Pre-flight completed
- Minor discrepancies acceptable if non-safety-related
- Fuel for flight + 30 minute reserve

**Pilot**:
- Minimum 6 hours sleep
- No alcohol in last 24 hours
- Low stress
- Current (flown within last 45 days)

**Environment**:
- Familiar or unfamiliar areas
- Class D airspace acceptable with communication
- No TFRs along route

**Note**: Even experienced pilots maintain personal minimums - they're just less restrictive based on greater experience.

### Using Personal Minimums

**Pre-Flight**:
- Review your personal minimums
- Compare current conditions to minimums
- If conditions exceed ANY minimum = No-go

**No Exceptions**:
- Personal minimums are not negotiable
- Don't "fudge" because you want to fly
- Don't relax minimums for external pressure

**Example**:
Your wind maximum is 12 mph. Winds are 13 mph.

**Wrong**: "It's only 1 mph over - close enough"
**Right**: "Exceeds my minimum. No-go."

**Re-Evaluating Minimums**:
- Review minimums every 6-12 months
- After gaining experience in new conditions (with instructor)
- If you want to relax a limit, fly with instructor in those conditions first
- NEVER adjust minimums on the day of flight to justify flying

## Section 7: ADM Tools and Techniques

### The 5 Ps (Expanded PAVE)

A structured way to assess situations in-flight:

**Plan**:
- What's my current plan?
- Is it still valid?
- Do I need to revise?

**Plane**:
- How's the aircraft performing?
- Any system issues?
- Fuel state?

**Pilot**:
- How am I doing?
- Fatigue, stress, workload?
- Am I ahead of or behind the aircraft?

**Passengers**:
- If carrying passenger, how are they?
- Sick, uncomfortable, afraid?
- Do I need to land?

**Programming**:
- (GPS/Navigation) Am I on course?
- Do I know my position?
- Any need to revise route?

**Use Every 15-20 Minutes** in flight, or when situation changes

### The DECIDE Model

Structured framework for decision-making:

**D - Detect** change:
- Something is different
- What changed?

**E - Estimate** significance:
- How important is this change?
- What are the effects?

**C - Choose** desired outcome:
- What do I want to achieve?
- What's my goal?

**I - Identify** actions:
- What are my options?
- List all possible actions

**D - Do** the best action:
- Select best option
- Execute

**E - Evaluate** the outcome:
- Did it work?
- What's the result?
- Need to adjust?

**Example - Engine Running Rough**:

**Detect**: Engine RPM drop, rough running
**Estimate**: Significant - possible engine failure developing
**Choose**: Safe landing while I have power
**Identify**:
  - Continue to destination (rejected - too risky)
  - Land immediately in best field (selected)
  - Troubleshoot in flight (secondary - while setting up approach)
**Do**: Set up approach to nearby field, troubleshoot while descending
**Evaluate**: Made it to field safely with power, good decision

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

When you identify risk, you can:

**1. Avoid** - Don't do it:
- Most conservative
- Eliminates risk
- Example: Don't fly in high winds

**2. Reduce** - Lower the risk:
- Mitigating actions
- Makes situation safer
- Example: Winds are high but below limits - fly shorter flight, stay closer to home field

**3. Accept** - Proceed with caution:
- Risk is low enough
- Benefits outweigh risk
- Monitor carefully
- Example: Winds are 2 mph above normal, but well within limits - accept and monitor

**4. Transfer** - Let someone else handle:
- Not usually applicable to solo PPC flying
- Example: If flying tandem, let more experienced pilot fly in challenging conditions

**Hierarchy**:
- Avoid is best (eliminates risk)
- Reduce is good (lowers risk)
- Accept is okay (if risk is truly acceptable)
- Transfer rarely applicable

**Never**:
- Ignore risk
- Hope risk goes away
- Underestimate risk

### The "What If" Game

**Mental Exercise** to build decision-making skills:

**How to Play**:
- While flying (or in chair at home), pose scenarios
- "What if [situation] happened right now?"
- Mentally walk through response

**Examples**:

"What if the engine quit right now?"
- Where would I land? (scan for fields)
- What's my glide range?
- What emergency procedures?

"What if weather deteriorated suddenly?"
- Where could I land?
- How would I get to that field?
- What's my out?

"What if I lost my position?"
- What are my major landmarks?
- How would I reorient?
- What would I do if I couldn't?

**Benefits**:
- Builds muscle memory for decision-making
- Reduces reaction time in actual emergency
- Increases situational awareness
- Identifies gaps in knowledge or planning

**Practice**:
- Play "What If" on every flight
- Review scenarios on ground
- Discuss with other pilots
- Make it habit

## Section 8: Continuous Improvement

### Post-Flight Analysis

**After Every Flight**:

**1. What Went Well?**:
- Decisions that worked out
- Good planning
- Skills demonstrated

**2. What Could Be Improved?**:
- Mistakes made
- Close calls
- Things that felt uncomfortable

**3. Decisions Review**:
- What decisions did I make?
- Were they timely?
- Based on good information?
- Would I decide the same way again?

**4. Lessons Learned**:
- What did I learn today?
- How will this change future flights?
- What will I do differently?

**5. Action Items**:
- Skills to practice
- Knowledge to review
- Procedures to improve

**Document**:
- Keep a flight log with notes
- Record lessons learned
- Review periodically

### Learning from Others

**Accident and Incident Reports**:
- Read NTSB reports (ntsb.gov)
- Aviation Safety Network (aviation-safety.net)
- Ultralight accidents reported by USUA, USPPA

**Ask**:
- What decisions led to the accident?
- What could have broken the chain?
- How can I avoid similar situation?
- What lessons apply to me?

**Examples**:
- Pilot continued in deteriorating weather → Landed in field, damaged aircraft
  - Lesson: Turn back earlier, don't press on
- Pilot took off in high winds → Lost control, crashed
  - Lesson: Respect personal minimums, don't yield to external pressure

**Discussion**:
- Talk with other pilots
- Join online forums (respectfully)
- Attend safety seminars
- Share experiences and learn from each other

### Recurrent Training

**Regular Training** maintains and improves skills:

**Annual or Biennial**:
- Fly with instructor
- Review procedures
- Practice emergencies
- Get feedback on decision-making

**New Conditions**:
- Before flying in new weather (crosswinds, gusty), practice with instructor
- Before flying unfamiliar terrain, study and plan
- Before flying new equipment, get training

**Currency**:
- Fly regularly to maintain proficiency
- If you haven't flown in 30+ days, fly conservatively first flight back
- If 90+ days, strongly consider refresher with instructor

**Ground Study**:
- Review regulations periodically (Part 103, airspace)
- Study weather (forecasting, interpretation)
- Read safety publications
- Take online courses

### Building a Safety Culture

**Personal Safety Culture**:

**1. Safety First Mindset**:
- No flight is worth injury
- There's always another day
- Arriving safely is the only goal that matters

**2. Continuous Learning**:
- Every flight is a learning opportunity
- Seek feedback
- Study accidents and incidents
- Read and learn

**3. Humility**:
- Recognize your limits
- Don't let ego drive decisions
- "I don't know" is okay
- Ask for help

**4. Conservative Bias**:
- When in doubt, don't
- Err on the side of safety
- Don't rationalize risks

**5. Preparation**:
- Thorough planning
- Use checklists
- Anticipate problems
- Have backup plans

**Community Safety Culture**:

**Share Knowledge**:
- Discuss lessons learned (without bragging or judging)
- Help new pilots
- Mentor others

**Speak Up**:
- If you see another pilot making unsafe decision, say something (tactfully)
- "Hey, have you considered [concern]?"
- Peer pressure for safety, not for risk-taking

**No Judgment**:
- Pilots should feel safe discussing mistakes
- Learn from each other
- Don't mock or criticize

**Positive Examples**:
- Celebrate good decisions (scrubbing flights, turning back, landing early)
- Reinforce safety culture
- Make conservative decisions the norm

## Section 9: Summary and Key Takeaways

### Core Principles of ADM

**1. Good Decisions Prevent Emergencies**:
- Most accidents are preventable
- Pre-flight decisions are most important
- Conservative go/no-go decisions eliminate in-flight risk

**2. Use Structured Decision-Making**:
- Don't just "wing it"
- Use frameworks (PAVE, 3P, DECIDE)
- Systematic approach reduces errors

**3. Manage Risk Actively**:
- Identify hazards
- Assess risk
- Mitigate or avoid
- Monitor continuously

**4. Know Yourself**:
- IMSAFE before every flight
- Recognize hazardous attitudes
- Set and follow personal minimums
- Be honest about limitations

**5. Maintain Situational Awareness**:
- Know what's happening around you
- OODA loop continuously
- Perceive, process, perform
- Stay ahead of the aircraft

**6. No External Pressure Matters**:
- Friends, events, ego - none are worth crashing for
- You can always fly another day
- Landing early is not "giving up," it's good ADM

**7. Continuous Improvement**:
- Learn from every flight
- Study accidents and incidents
- Seek training and feedback
- Safety is a journey, not a destination

### ADM Quick Reference

**Pre-Flight**:
- [ ] IMSAFE assessment
- [ ] PAVE risk assessment (Pilot, Aircraft, enVironment, External Pressures)
- [ ] Personal minimums reviewed
- [ ] Weather thorough review
- [ ] Go/No-Go decision

**In-Flight**:
- [ ] 5 Ps every 15-20 minutes (Plan, Plane, Pilot, Passengers, Programming)
- [ ] OODA loop continuously (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act)
- [ ] Situational awareness maintained
- [ ] "What if" game

**When Things Change**:
- [ ] 3P Model (Perceive, Process, Perform)
- [ ] DECIDE model if structured approach needed
- [ ] Conservative decision bias
- [ ] Landing is always an option

**Post-Flight**:
- [ ] Debrief what went well
- [ ] Identify areas for improvement
- [ ] Document lessons learned
- [ ] Plan action items

### Common ADM Failures and Prevention

| **Failure** | **Prevention** |
|-------------|----------------|
| Continued in deteriorating weather | Turn back early; pre-determined abort criteria |
| Took off in conditions exceeding limits | Strict adherence to personal minimums |
| Flew while fatigued/impaired | IMSAFE assessment, honesty |
| Yielded to external pressure | Recognize pressure pre-flight, remove it |
| Lost situational awareness | Continuous OODA loop, simplify when overloaded |
| Fixated on destination (get-there-itis) | Build in flexibility, focus on safe flight not arrival |
| Ignored mechanical warning signs | Conservative approach to discrepancies |
| Attempted flight beyond experience | Respect personal limits, seek training |

## Conclusion

Aeronautical Decision Making is the single most important skill for safe flying. Technical skills - flying the aircraft, navigation, weather interpretation - are all essential, but good decision-making is what keeps you alive.

**The Good News**: Decision-making can be learned, practiced, and improved. By using structured frameworks, managing risk systematically, maintaining self-awareness, and continuously learning, you can develop excellent ADM skills.

**The Challenge**: Good ADM sometimes means NOT flying. It means disappointing people, missing events, and accepting that some days aren't meant for flying. But pilots who master this are the ones who fly for decades without incident.

**Your Commitment**:
- Use ADM tools on every flight
- Set and follow personal minimums
- Learn from every experience
- Build a personal safety culture
- Help others fly safely

**Remember**: The goal isn't to fly as much as possible. The goal is to fly safely, enjoyably, and for a lifetime. Good aeronautical decision-making is how you achieve that goal.

**Final Thought**:
*"Superior pilots use their superior judgment to avoid situations requiring their superior skills."*

Fly smart. Fly safe. Make good decisions.
