# Aeromedical Exposure for Powered Parachute Pilots
## How the Open, Engine-Adjacent PPC Cockpit Changes Your Risk

---

## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **Aeromedical Factors and Human Factors** chapter covers the physiology — hypoxia, CO, disorientation, vision, noise, dehydration/heat/cold, alcohol/drugs, fatigue, and IMSAFE — and that physiology is identical for every pilot. This chapter adds the **PPC-specific exposure**: a powered parachute puts a particular stamp on aeromedical risk because of how and where you sit.

Powered parachutes put a specific stamp on aeromedical risk. You fly in the **open air** (sun, wind, cold, dehydration), you sit **close to a running engine** (carbon monoxide, noise, vibration), and you fly **alone** most of the time (no one to notice you're getting fuzzy).

---

## Altitude and Hypoxia — the PPC Envelope

Sport-pilot PPCs are capped at **10,000 ft MSL or 2,000 ft AGL, whichever is higher** (§ 61.315). Most flying happens well below that, so the regulatory oxygen rules won't be reached — but in **high-elevation terrain** (mountain-west valleys and plateaus) 10,000 MSL can be reached on a normal local flight, and hypoxia contributors (fatigue, dehydration, smoking, the night-before drink) still apply. Apply the shared hypoxia guidance with that envelope in mind.

---

## Carbon Monoxide — A PPC-Specific Hazard

The CO physiology is in the shared chapter; the **exposure** is what makes CO one of a PPC pilot's *primary* aeromedical hazards rather than a fixed-wing footnote.

A powered parachute pilot **sits in the open airflow of a running engine and its exhaust.** On many PPC layouts the pilot is just ahead of or beside the powerplant, with exhaust gases swirling in the prop wash. A cracked exhaust, a downwind taxi, or a long idle on the ground can put CO right where you're breathing — and at low altitude you wouldn't otherwise suspect oxygen deficiency.

**PPC-specific practice** (on top of the shared inspect-exhaust / detector / land-if-suspected guidance):
- **Minimize prolonged idling** with the exhaust blowing toward you; avoid long downwind ground runs.
- Treat an **unexplained in-flight headache or nausea at low altitude** as possible CO — get fresh airflow, reduce power/exposure, land and shut down, and inspect the exhaust before flying again.

> **Bottom line for PPC pilots**: your seating position makes CO more relevant to you than to most cockpit-enclosed pilots. Treat it as a primary hazard.

## Noise — A Two-Stroke Inches From Your Head

A two-stroke engine inches from your head is **loud** — easily into the range that causes permanent hearing damage over time, and fatiguing within a single long flight. On a PPC, **hearing protection is not optional gear — it's aeromedical equipment.** Wear a proper aviation headset (ideally with comms) or, at minimum, quality earplugs, every flight.

---

## Spatial Disorientation — Pendulum Stability and the Visual-Reference Rule

A PPC is relatively **pendulum-stable**, which helps resist disorientation. But you have **no attitude instruments to fall back on**, so your defense is maintaining visual reference to the horizon and surface at all times — which is also exactly what the regulations require: **§ 103.21 visual reference** and **§ 61.315** prohibits operating without visual reference to the surface. The moment you lose the horizon (haze, dusk, glassy water, cloud), disorientation risk spikes.

---

## Cold and Wind Chill — Open-Air Flight

A PPC at altitude is in a **wind chill** even on a mild day — the air cools with altitude and your airspeed adds wind chill on top. Cold-numbed hands handle the throttle and steering poorly. Dress in layers for the coldest part of the flight and protect your hands and face. (Heat and dehydration on a hot ramp during a long wing layout are the flip side — see the shared chapter.)

---

## Night Vision — Newly Relevant Under MOSAIC

Night-vision physiology (look off-center, ~30 minutes to dark-adapt, protect adaptation from bright lights) is now directly relevant to sport-pilot PPC operations because **MOSAIC allows sport-pilot night flight under § 61.329** (with the required training, endorsement, and medical path). Apply the shared night-vision guidance if you pursue the night privilege.

## Worked Scenario: The unexplained headache

You launch on a warm afternoon for a local flight at 1,200 ft AGL. After 20 minutes you notice a dull headache and feel a little "off" — foggy, not sharp. You're well below any altitude where hypoxia would be expected.

**Reason it through**: hypoxia is unlikely at 1,200 AGL; dehydration/heat is possible on a warm open-air day; but **carbon monoxide is very possible** — you're in the engine's airflow, and "headache + foggy + low altitude" is a classic CO presentation.

**Right move**: get fresh airflow, reduce power/exposure, and **land and shut down.** On the ground, hydrate, get out of the sun, and — before you fly again — **inspect the exhaust system for leaks.** Don't write off an unexplained in-flight headache as "just warm out."
