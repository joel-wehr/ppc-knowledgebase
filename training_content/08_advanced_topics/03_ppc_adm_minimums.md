# ADM and Personal Minimums for Powered Parachute Pilots
## PPC-Specific Risk Thresholds

---

## How This Chapter Fits

The shared **Aeronautical Decision Making and Risk Management** chapter covers the universal frameworks — PAVE, 3P, DECIDE, the 5 Ps, IMSAFE, hazardous attitudes, situational awareness, and the personal-minimums *process*. This chapter adds the **PPC-specific reasons ADM matters so much**, the concrete **personal-minimums numbers** for a PPC, and the **PPC scenario thresholds** that plug into those frameworks.

---

## Why ADM Matters So Much for PPCs

For powered parachute pilots, ADM is particularly important because:
- You're often flying alone (no copilot to catch errors)
- Limited performance envelope (slow speed, limited climb)
- Weather-dependent operations (more weather decisions, tighter wind limits)
- No "instruments" to fall back on (VFR only)
- **Engine failure = immediate landing** — there is no "glide to the airport," and an aggressive maneuver can collapse the wing

These category traits mean the go/no-go decision (especially on weather and wind) carries more weight on a PPC than on a faster, rigid-wing aircraft, and the personal-minimums numbers below are correspondingly conservative.

## Part 103 Alcohol Rule

Part 103 requires **no alcohol before flight**. Treat any alcohol in your system as a no-go (this is stricter than the general "8 hours bottle-to-throttle / < 0.04% BAC" rule). Residual effects last longer than you think.

## PPC Scenario Thresholds

The shared chapter's scenarios use "above/near your limits" generically. For a PPC, those limits are tight, and a wind that a trainer airplane would shrug off is a hard no-go for a PPC. Concrete examples:

- **Marginal weather**: winds forecast ~15 mph gusting ~22 mph against a personal max of ~12 mph sustained / 15 mph gusts → loss-of-control likelihood **Probable**, severity **Critical** → **RED, do not fly**.
- **Run-up anomaly**: an EGT/CHT deviation on a two-stroke PPC engine warrants grounding the flight until diagnosed — a PPC has no second engine and no glide.
- **In-flight rough engine at 800 ft AGL**: land immediately in the best field while you still have power — a PPC's low altitude and lack of glide give very little time.

## PPC Personal Minimums — Example Values

These are starting points for the personal-minimums *process* in the shared chapter, sized for a PPC.

### Beginning PPC Pilot

**Weather**:
- Wind: 8 mph maximum, 10 mph gusts maximum
- Visibility: 5 miles minimum
- Ceiling: 2,500 feet AGL minimum (well above the Class G requirement)
- Temperature: 45°F to 85°F
- No approaching weather systems; no fog, mist, or low clouds
- Time: 2 hours after sunrise to 2 hours before sunset

**Aircraft**: pre-flight complete with no discrepancies; no recent maintenance issues; fuel for the flight + 1 hour reserve; all instruments functioning.

**Pilot**: minimum 7 hours sleep; no alcohol in last 48 hours; no stress/distractions; current (flown within last 21 days) with at least 3 landings in last 21 days.

**Environment**: familiar field/area (within 10 miles of home base); clear of controlled airspace; no TFRs; no congested areas.

### Experienced PPC Pilot

**Weather**:
- Wind: 15 mph maximum, 18 mph gusts maximum
- Visibility: 3 miles minimum (Part 103 requirement)
- Ceiling: no specific limit as long as clear of clouds
- Temperature: 35°F to 95°F
- No thunderstorms within 20 miles
- Time: 1 hour after sunrise to 1 hour before sunset

**Aircraft**: pre-flight complete; minor non-safety discrepancies acceptable; fuel for the flight + 30 minute reserve.

**Pilot**: minimum 6 hours sleep; no alcohol in last 24 hours; low stress; current (flown within last 45 days).

**Environment**: familiar or unfamiliar areas; Class D airspace acceptable with communication; no TFRs along route.

> Even experienced pilots keep personal minimums — they're just less restrictive based on greater experience. If any minimum is exceeded, it's a no-go: e.g. your wind max is 12 mph and winds are 13 mph → "Exceeds my minimum. No-go." No day-of fudging, no relaxing limits for external pressure.

## Learning From PPC Accidents

In addition to NTSB reports, review ultralight/PPC accidents reported by the USUA and USPPA. Common chains: a pilot continued in deteriorating weather and damaged the aircraft (lesson: turn back earlier); a pilot took off in winds above limits and lost control (lesson: respect personal minimums, don't yield to external pressure).
