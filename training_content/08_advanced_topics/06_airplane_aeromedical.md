## Altitude and Hypoxia — the Airplane Envelope

Airplanes routinely climb high enough that the **regulatory oxygen rules of 14 CFR § 91.211** actually apply:
- **12,500-14,000 ft MSL**: the required crew must use supplemental oxygen for any portion of the flight at those altitudes lasting **more than 30 minutes**.
- **Above 14,000 ft MSL**: required crew must use oxygen the entire time.
- **Above 15,000 ft MSL**: oxygen must be **provided to every occupant**.

*(Uncertainty flag: confirm the exact altitudes/durations in § 91.211.)* A normally aspirated trainer rarely reaches these, but a cross-country over high terrain or a climb for winds can — know the rule.

## Carbon Monoxide — the Cabin-Heat Path

In an airplane the dominant CO source is the **cabin-heat system**: most light singles route cabin heat through a shroud around the **exhaust muffler**, so a **cracked muffler or exhaust leak can feed CO directly into the cabin** with the heater on (PHAK Ch. 17).

**Airplane-specific practice** (on top of the shared inspect-exhaust / detector / land-if-suspected guidance):
- Be especially alert to CO **in cold weather with cabin heat on** — that's when the exhaust-shroud path is active.
- If you suspect CO: **turn cabin heat OFF, open fresh-air vents/window, and land** as soon as practical; have the exhaust/muffler inspected before the next flight.
- A panel or portable CO detector is strongly recommended.

## Spatial Disorientation — Higher Stakes in an Airplane

An airplane has full three-axis control and can readily enter unusual attitudes, so an instructor-induced or inadvertent loss of visual reference is more consequential than in a pendulum-stable aircraft. Two airplane realities:
- **VFR-into-IMC + the leans/graveyard spiral** is a classic fatal chain — without an instrument scan, a disoriented VFR pilot in cloud can spiral. The defense is to **not enter IMC** and, if inadvertently entered, to use the partial-panel / 180° level turn taught for that emergency.
- **Somatogravic illusion on a go-around or takeoff** (strong acceleration felt as a pitch-up) can prompt a dangerous nose-down "correction" at low altitude — trust the attitude indicator and the visual horizon.

The shared chapter's illusions (false horizon, black-hole approach, landing illusions) apply directly; airplane night and IFR operations raise their stakes.

## Vision, Noise, Fatigue

- **Vision/scan** and **night vision** are as in the shared chapter; the enclosed cabin reduces wind/glare but windshield reflections and panel lighting can impair dark adaptation at night.
- **Noise**: an enclosed cabin is quieter than an open cockpit but still warrants a headset; cumulative hearing loss is the same concern.
- **Fatigue/stress/medication**: identical to the shared chapter. Note the airplane medical basis — a Private Pilot exercises privileges under a **third-class medical or BasicMed** (14 CFR § 61.23), which still relies on honest IMSAFE self-assessment between exams.
