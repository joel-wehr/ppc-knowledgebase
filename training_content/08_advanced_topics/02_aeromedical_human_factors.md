# Aeromedical Factors and Human Factors
## The Pilot as a Physiological System

---

## Introduction

The ADM chapter covers how you *think* — judgment, risk, hazardous attitudes, personal minimums. This chapter covers how your *body* performs: at altitude, in heat and cold, around engine noise, and after a poor night's sleep. The FAA treats aeromedical factors as a distinct knowledge area for good reason: a perfectly maintained aircraft flown by an impaired pilot is an impaired aircraft.

This is the shared, category-agnostic physiology every pilot needs. The category-specific *exposure* — how your particular cockpit and operating altitudes change your carbon-monoxide, noise, and oxygen risk — lives in your aircraft's aeromedical chapter.

**Key Principle**: You cannot feel most of these conditions coming on. Hypoxia, carbon monoxide, and the disorientation illusions all degrade *the very judgment you'd use to detect them*. The defense is not "noticing in the moment" — it's knowing the risk factors and ruling them out *on the ground*.

> Authoritative source: **PHAK FAA-H-8083-25C, Chapter 17 (Aeromedical Factors)**. Regulatory hooks to § 91.17 (alcohol/drugs) and § 61.53 (fitness to fly) are covered in the General Operating Rules and Certification material.

## Section 1: Oxygen and Altitude — Hypoxia

**Hypoxia** is a state of oxygen deficiency sufficient to impair function. The most relevant form for VFR pilots is **hypoxic hypoxia** — at altitude the air pressure is lower, so each breath delivers less oxygen to your blood even though the air is still 21% oxygen.

Hypoxia symptoms can begin subtly as low as **5,000-8,000 ft**, especially with contributing factors: smoking, anemia, fatigue, dehydration, or alcohol the night before (carbon monoxide from cigarettes already occupies hemoglobin).

**Recognition** (the insidious part) — symptoms are individual and you often **feel fine** (even euphoric) while your performance drops:
- Impaired judgment and slowed thinking
- Tingling, lightheadedness, headache
- Visual changes (especially loss of night vision and color perception)
- Cyanosis (blue fingernails/lips)
- A false sense of well-being or belligerence

The first thing hypoxia degrades is your ability to recognize hypoxia — you won't reliably catch it in yourself.

**Prevention**: know the regulatory oxygen rules (§ 91.211); **descend** if you suspect hypoxia (altitude is the cause, descent is the cure); and eliminate contributors — don't fly hung over, dehydrated, exhausted, or immediately after smoking.

## Section 2: Hyperventilation

**Hyperventilation** is over-breathing — too rapid or too deep — usually from stress, anxiety, or fear. It blows off too much carbon dioxide and upsets blood chemistry.

**Recognition** (it mimics hypoxia, which is the trap): lightheadedness, dizziness, tingling in fingers/toes/around the mouth, visual disturbances, anxiety, eventually muscle spasm.

**Response**:
1. **Consciously slow your breathing rate** — talk out loud, or breathe in a slow, controlled rhythm.
2. If unsure whether it's hypoxia or hyperventilation at altitude, treat for hypoxia first (descend, oxygen if available), then slow the breathing. Hyperventilation resolves quickly once breathing normalizes.

## Section 3: Carbon Monoxide

**Carbon monoxide (CO)** is a colorless, odorless, tasteless gas produced by incomplete combustion. CO binds to hemoglobin **200+ times more readily than oxygen**, so even a small concentration starves your blood of oxygen — producing a hypoxia that **altitude doesn't explain**.

**Recognition**: headache, drowsiness, dizziness, nausea, confusion and impaired judgment, and in severe cases loss of consciousness. These overlap with hypoxia and mild illness, which is why CO is dangerous on the ground and at low altitude where you wouldn't suspect oxygen deficiency.

**Prevention and response**:
- **Inspect the exhaust system** every preflight — cracks, leaks, loose clamps. CO from a failing exhaust is a maintenance problem first.
- **Consider a CO detector** (chemical-spot card or electronic) in the cockpit area.
- **If you suspect CO** (unexplained headache/nausea in flight): get fresh air, reduce exposure, **land and shut down**, and don't fly again until the exhaust system is inspected.

> How exposed you are to CO depends heavily on aircraft layout — your category's aeromedical chapter covers your specific exposure.

## Section 4: Spatial Disorientation and Visual Illusions

**Spatial disorientation** is the inability to correctly perceive your position, attitude, and motion relative to the earth. It happens when the **vestibular system** (inner ear) and **proprioception** (seat-of-the-pants feel) disagree with reality — without a clear visual horizon, you may believe the false signals.

A VFR pilot's defense is **maintaining visual reference to the horizon and the surface at all times**. The moment you lose the horizon (haze, dusk, glassy water, cloud), disorientation risk spikes.

**The classic illusions** — recognize the situations that produce them:
- **The leans**: after a prolonged, gentle turn, leveling can feel like banking the other way. Trust the visual horizon, not the feeling.
- **Somatogravic illusion**: acceleration feels like pitching up; deceleration like pitching down.
- **False horizon**: a sloping cloud deck, shoreline, or line of ground lights mistaken for the true horizon.
- **Featureless-terrain / "black hole"**: over water, snow, or unlit terrain at dusk, depth and height cues vanish.
- **Autokinesis**: a single fixed light stared at in the dark appears to move.

**Landing illusions** (directly affect your flare):
- **Up-sloping** runway/terrain makes you feel too high → tendency to fly low.
- **Down-sloping** makes you feel too low → tendency to fly high.
- **Narrow** field/strip feels too high; **wide** feels too low.
- **Rain, haze, or low light** make distances look greater → flare late or land long.

**Defense**: know your field, cross-check your sight picture against known references, fly a stabilized approach, and respect the conditions (haze, dusk, glassy surfaces) that distort what you see.

## Section 5: Vision and Visual Scanning

Vision is your primary flight instrument under VFR:
- **See-and-avoid depends on an effective scan.** The eye detects motion and contrast poorly when stationary. Use a series of short, regularly spaced eye movements (roughly 10° segments, pausing to focus) rather than sweeping the sky. An aircraft on a collision course appears motionless and grows slowly — exactly what the eye misses.
- **Night vision** depends on the rods at the edge of your retina; look slightly off-center to see dim objects at night, allow ~30 minutes to dark-adapt, and protect that adaptation from bright lights.

Sun glare, fatigue, dehydration, hypoxia, and CO all degrade vision — another reason these factors compound.

## Section 6: Noise, Vibration, and Hearing

Aircraft engines are loud — easily into the range that causes permanent hearing damage over time, and fatiguing within a single long flight.
- **Always wear hearing protection** — a proper aviation headset (ideally with comms) or, at minimum, quality earplugs.
- **Vibration and noise cause fatigue** that accumulates across a flight, degrading attention and decision-making late in the day.
- Noise-induced hearing loss is cumulative and irreversible — protect your hearing for the long run.

## Section 7: Dehydration, Heat, and Cold

### Dehydration and heat stress
- Sun, wind, and engine heat dehydrate you faster than you notice.
- **Symptoms**: fatigue, headache, dizziness, cramping, reduced alertness — overlapping again with hypoxia and CO.
- **Prevention**: hydrate *before* flying (you can't catch up in the air), wear sun protection, and limit time aloft on hot days.
- Heat exhaustion and heatstroke are real risks during a long preflight on a hot ramp — pace yourself and drink water.

### Cold and hypothermia
- Air cools with altitude (standard lapse rate ~3.5°F per 1,000 ft), and airspeed adds wind chill.
- **Symptoms**: shivering, numb extremities, clumsiness, then impaired judgment and slowed thinking.
- **Prevention**: dress in layers for the *coldest* part of the flight, protect hands and face, and recognize that cold-numbed hands handle controls poorly.

## Section 8: Alcohol, Drugs, and Medication

The physiology is here; the regulations (§ 91.17, § 61.53) are in the General Operating Rules material.
- **Alcohol**: impairs judgment, coordination, and balance, and worsens hypoxia. The regulatory floor is **8 hours "bottle to throttle," BAC below 0.04%, and not "under the influence"** (§ 91.17) — but impairment and hangover can outlast 8 hours. Treat 12+ hours and full sobriety as the practical standard.
- **Over-the-counter medications**: many are disqualifying. First-generation antihistamines (e.g. diphenhydramine/Benadryl) and many sleep aids impair performance for 12+ hours; decongestant + sedating-antihistamine combos are a common trap.
- **The underlying illness matters as much as the drug.** A head cold bad enough to need medication is often bad enough to ground you — blocked sinuses and ears cause severe pain and possible eardrum injury on descent (**barotrauma**).
- **Prescription medications and cannabis**: when in doubt, don't fly; consult an AME. Cannabis is federally prohibited regardless of state law.

---

## Section 9: Fatigue and Stress

- **Fatigue** — acute (one bad night) or chronic (accumulated) — degrades attention, reaction time, and judgment as much as alcohol does, and you habituate to it so you under-rate your own impairment. There is no in-flight fix; the only remedy is rest *before* the flight.
- **Stress** (life stress, get-there pressure, a tight schedule) narrows attention and feeds the hazardous attitudes covered in the ADM chapter. Stress + fatigue + dehydration is a common, quietly dangerous combination.

## Section 10: IMSAFE — Tying It Together

Before **every** flight, run the **IMSAFE** personal checklist — the practical, on-the-ground defense against every hazard in this chapter.

| Letter | Ask yourself | Ground yourself if… |
|---|---|---|
| **I**llness | Am I sick? | Any symptom that distracts or impairs — even a cold |
| **M**edication | Am I on anything? | Taking any drug (OTC or Rx) that affects faculties |
| **S**tress | Am I under pressure? | Life/financial/relationship stress is occupying my mind |
| **A**lcohol | When did I last drink? | Within 8 hrs (12+ to be safe), or any residual effect |
| **F**atigue | Am I rested? | Tired, under-slept, or worn down |
| **E**motion | Am I emotionally fit? | Angry, grieving, or rattled — emotion impairs judgment |

If any item is in doubt, **delay or cancel.** Most aeromedical accidents trace back to a pilot who knew an IMSAFE item was off and flew anyway.

## Worked Scenario: The leveled-off "lean"

On a hazy evening you make a long, gentle 180° turn back toward the field. As you roll level, your inner ear insists you're now banking the other way, and you feel the urge to "correct" with opposite input.

**Reason it through**: this is **the leans** — a vestibular illusion after a prolonged turn. The haze has weakened your horizon, so the false sensation is winning.

**Right move**: **trust the visual horizon**, hold the wings level visually, and let the false sensation fade (it will, in seconds). Do not chase the feeling. The bigger lesson: hazy/low-light conditions that erode the horizon are exactly when to be most conservative — they also feed landing illusions and reduce see-and-avoid.

## Summary

| Hazard | The trap | Your defense |
|---|---|---|
| Hypoxia | You feel fine while impaired | Know contributors; descend |
| Hyperventilation | Mimics hypoxia | Slow your breathing |
| Carbon monoxide | Looks like illness at low altitude | Inspect exhaust; detector; land if suspected |
| Spatial disorientation | Inner ear lies without a horizon | Maintain visual reference; trust the horizon |
| Visual illusions | Sight picture distorted by terrain/light | Know your field; stabilized approach |
| Noise | Cumulative, irreversible loss | Always wear hearing protection |
| Dehydration/heat/cold | Onset is gradual | Hydrate/dress before flight |
| Alcohol/drugs/meds | "I'm under the limit" | 12+ hrs sober; check every medication |
| Fatigue/stress | You under-rate your own impairment | Rest and resolve stress before, not during |

**Remember**: nearly every hazard here degrades the judgment you'd use to catch it, and several — hypoxia, CO, dehydration, fatigue — present with the same foggy, headachy, "off" feeling. That overlap is why the defense is a disciplined **IMSAFE check on the ground** plus a low threshold to **land and sort it out** when something feels wrong in the air. You are the most safety-critical, and the most failure-prone, component of the aircraft.
