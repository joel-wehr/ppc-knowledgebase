# Aeromedical Factors and Human Factors
## The Pilot as a Physiological System

---

## Introduction

The companion lesson in this module (`01_aeronautical_decision_making.md`)
covers how you *think* — judgment, risk, hazardous attitudes, personal
minimums. This lesson covers how your *body* performs at altitude, in
heat and cold, behind a noisy two-stroke engine, and after a poor night's
sleep. The FAA Sport Pilot ACS treats these as a distinct area
(Aeromedical Factors) for good reason: a perfectly maintained aircraft
flown by an impaired pilot is an impaired aircraft.

Powered parachutes put a specific stamp on aeromedical risk. You fly in
the **open air** (sun, wind, cold, dehydration), you sit **close to a
running engine** (carbon monoxide, noise, vibration), and you fly **alone**
most of the time (no one to notice you're getting fuzzy). This lesson
covers the physiological hazards, how to recognize them in yourself, and
the **IMSAFE** self-check that ties them together before every flight.

**Key Principle**: You cannot feel most of these conditions coming on.
Hypoxia, carbon monoxide, and the disorientation illusions all degrade
*the very judgment you'd use to detect them*. The defense is not
"noticing in the moment" — it's knowing the risk factors and ruling them
out *on the ground*.

> Authoritative source for this lesson: **PHAK FAA-H-8083-25C, Chapter 17
> (Aeromedical Factors)**, with PPC-specific application. Regulatory hooks
> to § 91.17 (alcohol/drugs) and § 61.53 (fitness to fly) are covered in
> the General Operating Rules and Certification modules.

## Section 1: Oxygen and Altitude — Hypoxia

### What hypoxia is

**Hypoxia** is a state of oxygen deficiency in the body sufficient to
impair function. The most relevant form for VFR pilots is **hypoxic
hypoxia** — at altitude, the air pressure is lower, so each breath
delivers less oxygen to your blood even though the air is still 21%
oxygen.

### Why it matters even for low-and-slow PPCs

Sport-pilot PPCs are capped at **10,000 ft MSL or 2,000 ft AGL, whichever
is higher** (§ 61.315). Most flying happens well below that. But:

- In **high-elevation terrain** (mountain-west valleys, plateaus), 10,000
  MSL can be reached on a normal local flight.
- Hypoxia symptoms can begin subtly as low as **5,000–8,000 ft**,
  especially with contributing factors: smoking, anemia, fatigue,
  dehydration, alcohol the night before, or simply being a smoker (carbon
  monoxide from cigarettes already occupies hemoglobin).

### Recognition (the insidious part)

Symptoms are **individual** and you often **feel fine** — euphoric, even —
while your performance drops:

- Impaired judgment and slowed thinking
- Tingling, lightheadedness, headache
- Visual changes (especially loss of night vision and color perception)
- Cyanosis (blue fingernails/lips)
- A false sense of well-being or belligerence

The danger is that the *first thing hypoxia degrades is your ability to
recognize hypoxia.* You won't reliably catch it in yourself.

### Prevention

- Know the **regulatory oxygen rules** (Part 91.211 for higher altitudes)
  — though a sport-pilot PPC won't reach them, the principle stands: above
  ~10,000 ft, supplemental oxygen is a real consideration.
- **Descend** if you suspect hypoxia — altitude is the cause and descent
  is the cure.
- Eliminate contributors: don't fly hung over, dehydrated, exhausted, or
  immediately after smoking.

## Section 2: Hyperventilation

**Hyperventilation** is over-breathing — too rapid or too deep — usually
from stress, anxiety, or fear (a first solo, an emergency, turbulence).
It blows off too much carbon dioxide and upsets blood chemistry.

**Recognition** (it mimics hypoxia, which is the trap):

- Lightheadedness, dizziness
- Tingling in fingers, toes, around the mouth
- Visual disturbances, anxiety, eventually muscle spasm

**Response**:

1. **Consciously slow your breathing rate.** Talk out loud, or breathe in
   a slow, controlled rhythm.
2. **If unsure whether it's hypoxia or hyperventilation at altitude**,
   treat for hypoxia first (descend, oxygen if available) — then slow the
   breathing. Hyperventilation resolves quickly once breathing normalizes.

## Section 3: Carbon Monoxide — A PPC-Specific Hazard

### Why PPC pilots are especially exposed

**Carbon monoxide (CO)** is a colorless, odorless, tasteless gas produced
by incomplete combustion — and a powered parachute pilot **sits in the
open airflow of a running engine and its exhaust.** On many PPC layouts
the pilot is just ahead of or beside the powerplant, with exhaust gases
swirling in the prop wash. A cracked exhaust, a downwind taxi, or an
idle on the ground can put CO right where you're breathing.

CO binds to hemoglobin **200+ times more readily than oxygen**, so even a
small concentration starves your blood of oxygen — producing a hypoxia
that **altitude doesn't explain.**

### Recognition

- Headache, drowsiness, dizziness
- Nausea
- Confusion, impaired judgment
- In severe cases, loss of consciousness

These overlap with hypoxia and even mild illness, which is why CO is so
dangerous on the ground and at low altitude where you wouldn't suspect
oxygen deficiency.

### Prevention and response

- **Inspect the exhaust system** every preflight — cracks, leaks, loose
  clamps. CO from a failing exhaust is a maintenance problem first.
- **Consider a CO detector** (the inexpensive chemical-spot cards or an
  electronic one) in the cockpit area.
- **Minimize prolonged idling** with the exhaust blowing toward you;
  avoid long downwind ground runs.
- **If you suspect CO** (unexplained headache/nausea in flight): get
  fresh air, reduce exposure, **land and shut down**, and don't fly again
  until the exhaust system is inspected.

> **Bottom line for PPC pilots:** treat CO as one of your *primary*
> aeromedical hazards, not a fixed-wing footnote. Your seating position
> makes it more relevant to you than to most cockpit-enclosed pilots.

## Section 4: Spatial Disorientation and Visual Illusions

### Spatial disorientation

**Spatial disorientation** is the inability to correctly perceive your
position, attitude, and motion relative to the earth. It happens when the
**vestibular system** (inner ear) and **proprioception** (seat-of-the-
pants feel) disagree with reality — and without a clear visual horizon,
you may believe the false signals.

A PPC is relatively pendulum-stable, which helps. But you have **no
attitude instruments to fall back on**, so your defense is **maintaining
visual reference to the horizon and the surface at all times** — which is
also exactly what the regulations require (Part 103 § 103.21 visual
reference; § 61.315 prohibits operating without visual reference to the
surface). The moment you lose the horizon (haze, dusk, glassy water,
cloud), disorientation risk spikes.

### The classic illusions

You don't need to memorize the textbook list, but you should recognize
the situations that produce them:

- **The leans**: after a prolonged, gentle turn, leveling the wings can
  feel like banking the *other* way. Trust your visual horizon, not the
  feeling.
- **Somatogravic illusion**: acceleration feels like pitching *up*;
  deceleration feels like pitching *down*. A power surge can fool you into
  thinking you're climbing.
- **False horizon**: a sloping cloud deck, a shoreline, or a line of
  ground lights at night can be mistaken for the true horizon.
- **Featureless-terrain / "black hole"**: over water, snow, or unlit
  terrain at dusk, depth and height cues vanish.
- **Autokinesis**: a single fixed light stared at in the dark appears to
  move.

### Landing illusions (directly affect your flare)

- **Up-sloping runway/terrain** makes you feel **too high** → tendency to
  fly a **low** approach.
- **Down-sloping** makes you feel too low → tendency to fly **high**.
- **Narrow field/strip** makes you feel too high; **wide** feels too low.
- **Rain, haze, or low light** make distances look greater → you flare
  late or land long.

**Defense**: know your field, cross-check your sight picture against
known references, fly a stabilized approach, and respect the conditions
(haze, dusk, glassy surfaces) that distort what you see.

## Section 5: Vision and Visual Scanning

Vision is your primary flight instrument in a VFR PPC. Two practical
points:

- **See-and-avoid depends on an effective scan.** The eye detects motion
  and contrast poorly when it's stationary. Use a **series of short,
  regularly spaced eye movements** (roughly 10° segments, pausing to
  focus) rather than sweeping the sky. An aircraft on a collision course
  appears *motionless* and grows slowly — exactly what the eye misses.
- **Night vision** depends on the rods at the edge of your retina; look
  slightly *off-center* to see dim objects at night, allow ~30 minutes to
  dark-adapt, and protect that adaptation from bright lights. (Relevant
  now that MOSAIC allows sport-pilot night flight under § 61.329.)

Sun glare, fatigue, dehydration, hypoxia, and CO all degrade vision —
another reason these factors compound.

## Section 6: Noise, Vibration, and Hearing

A two-stroke engine inches from your head is **loud** — easily into the
range that causes permanent hearing damage over time, and fatiguing
within a single long flight.

- **Always wear hearing protection** — a proper aviation headset (ideally
  with comms) or, at minimum, quality earplugs. This is not optional gear
  on a PPC; it's aeromedical equipment.
- **Vibration and noise cause fatigue** that accumulates across a flight,
  degrading attention and decision-making late in the day.
- Protect your hearing for the long run — noise-induced hearing loss is
  cumulative and irreversible.

## Section 7: Dehydration, Heat, and Cold

Open-air flight exposes you directly to the environment.

### Dehydration and heat stress

- Sun, wind, and engine heat dehydrate you faster than you notice.
- **Symptoms**: fatigue, headache, dizziness, cramping, reduced alertness
  — overlapping again with hypoxia and CO.
- **Prevention**: hydrate *before* flying (you can't catch up in the air),
  wear sun protection, and limit time aloft on hot days.
- **Heat exhaustion / heatstroke** are real risks on a hot ramp during a
  long preflight or wing layout — pace yourself and drink water.

### Cold and hypothermia

- A PPC at altitude is in a **wind chill** even on a mild day — the
  standard lapse rate cools the air ~3.5°F per 1,000 ft, and your
  airspeed adds wind chill on top.
- **Symptoms**: shivering, numb extremities, clumsiness, then impaired
  judgment and slowed thinking (which compounds every other risk here).
- **Prevention**: dress in layers for the *coldest* part of the flight,
  protect hands and face, and recognize that cold-numbed hands handle the
  throttle and steering poorly.

## Section 8: Alcohol, Drugs, and Medication

This is both an aeromedical and a regulatory topic. The rules
(§ 91.17 and § 61.53) are covered in detail in the **General Operating
Rules** module; the physiology is here.

- **Alcohol**: impairs judgment, coordination, and balance, and worsens
  hypoxia. The regulatory floor is **8 hours "bottle to throttle,"
  BAC below 0.04%, and not "under the influence"** (§ 91.17) — but
  impairment and hangover effects can outlast 8 hours. Treat 12+ hours
  and full sobriety as the practical standard.
- **Over-the-counter medications**: many are disqualifying. First-
  generation antihistamines (e.g., diphenhydramine/Benadryl) and many
  sleep aids impair performance for 12+ hours. **Decongestant + sedating
  antihistamine combos** are a common trap.
- **The underlying illness matters as much as the drug.** A head cold bad
  enough to need medication is often bad enough to ground you on its own —
  blocked sinuses and ears cause severe pain and possible eardrum injury
  on descent (a **barotrauma** risk).
- **Prescription medications and cannabis**: when in doubt, don't fly;
  consult an AME. Cannabis is federally prohibited regardless of state
  law.

---

## Section 9: Fatigue and Stress

- **Fatigue** — acute (one bad night) or chronic (accumulated) — degrades
  attention, reaction time, and judgment as much as alcohol does, and you
  habituate to it so you under-rate your own impairment. There is no
  in-flight fix; the only remedy is rest *before* the flight.
- **Stress** (life stress, get-there pressure, a tight schedule) narrows
  attention and feeds the hazardous attitudes covered in the ADM lesson.
  Stress + fatigue + dehydration is a common, quietly fatal combination.

## Section 10: IMSAFE — Tying It Together

Before **every** flight, run the **IMSAFE** personal checklist. It is the
practical, on-the-ground defense against every hazard in this lesson —
and the self-assessment the sport-pilot driver's-license medical assumes
you are doing (§ 61.53).

| Letter | Ask yourself | Ground yourself if… |
|---|---|---|
| **I**llness | Am I sick? | Any symptom that distracts or impairs — even a cold |
| **M**edication | Am I on anything? | Taking any drug (OTC or Rx) that affects faculties |
| **S**tress | Am I under pressure? | Life/financial/relationship stress is occupying my mind |
| **A**lcohol | When did I last drink? | Within 8 hrs (12+ to be safe), or any residual effect |
| **F**atigue | Am I rested? | Tired, under-slept, or worn down |
| **E**motion | Am I emotionally fit? | Angry, grieving, or rattled — emotion impairs judgment |

If any item is in doubt, the answer is to **delay or cancel.** IMSAFE is
not a formality — it's the moment you honestly confront whether *today's
version of you* is fit to fly. Most aeromedical accidents trace back to a
pilot who knew an IMSAFE item was off and flew anyway.

## Worked Scenario 1: The unexplained headache

You launch on a warm afternoon for a local flight at 1,200 ft AGL. After
20 minutes you notice a dull headache and feel a little "off" — not
sharp, just foggy. You're well below any altitude where hypoxia would be
expected.

**Reason it through:**
- Hypoxia? Unlikely at 1,200 AGL.
- Dehydration/heat? Possible — warm day, open air.
- **Carbon monoxide?** Very possible — you're in the engine's airflow,
  and "headache + foggy + low altitude" is a classic CO presentation.

**Right move**: get fresh airflow, reduce power/exposure, and **land and
shut down.** On the ground, hydrate, get out of the sun, and — before you
fly again — **inspect the exhaust system for leaks.** Don't write off an
unexplained in-flight headache as "just warm out."

---

## Worked Scenario 2: The leveled-off "lean"

On a hazy evening you make a long, gentle 180° turn back toward the
field. As you roll level, your inner ear insists you're now banking the
other way, and you feel the urge to "correct" with opposite input.

**Reason it through:** this is **the leans** — a vestibular illusion after
a prolonged turn. The haze has weakened your horizon, so the false
sensation is winning.

**Right move**: **trust the visual horizon**, hold the wings level
visually, and let the false sensation fade (it will, in seconds). Do *not*
chase the feeling. And note the bigger lesson: hazy/low-light conditions
that erode the horizon are exactly when you should be most conservative —
the same conditions feed disorientation, landing illusions, and reduced
see-and-avoid.

## Summary

| Hazard | The trap | Your defense |
|---|---|---|
| Hypoxia | You feel fine while impaired | Know contributors; descend |
| Hyperventilation | Mimics hypoxia | Slow your breathing |
| **Carbon monoxide** | Looks like illness at low altitude | Inspect exhaust; detector; land if suspected |
| Spatial disorientation | Inner ear lies without a horizon | Maintain visual reference; trust the horizon |
| Visual illusions | Sight picture distorted by terrain/light | Know your field; stabilized approach |
| Noise | Cumulative, irreversible loss | Always wear hearing protection |
| Dehydration/heat/cold | Onset is gradual | Hydrate/dress before flight |
| Alcohol/drugs/meds | "I'm under the limit" | 12+ hrs sober; check every medication |
| Fatigue/stress | You under-rate your own impairment | Rest and resolve stress before, not during |

**Remember**: nearly every hazard in this lesson degrades the judgment
you'd use to catch it, and several of them — hypoxia, CO, dehydration,
fatigue — present with the *same* foggy, headachy, "off" feeling. That
overlap is why the defense is a disciplined **IMSAFE check on the ground**
plus a low threshold to **land and sort it out** when something feels
wrong in the air. You are the most safety-critical, and the most
failure-prone, component of the aircraft.
