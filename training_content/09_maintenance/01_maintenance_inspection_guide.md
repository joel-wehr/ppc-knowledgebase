# Maintenance and Inspection Guide for Powered Parachutes
## Keeping Your Aircraft Airworthy and Safe

---

## Introduction

Proper maintenance and regular inspections are essential for safe powered parachute operations. Unlike certified aircraft, Part 103 ultralight vehicles have NO formal maintenance requirements from the FAA. However, this freedom comes with responsibility - **YOU** are responsible for ensuring your aircraft is safe to fly.

This guide provides comprehensive coverage of:
- Pre-flight inspection procedures (detailed)
- Periodic maintenance schedules
- Component-specific maintenance
- Troubleshooting common issues
- Record-keeping best practices
- Condition inspection procedures
- When to seek professional help

**Key Principle**: Your life depends on proper maintenance. Part 103 means "no regulations," not "no maintenance."

---

## Section 1: Maintenance Philosophy for Part 103 Aircraft

### Your Responsibility

**No FAA Requirements** for Part 103 means:
- No required inspections
- No mechanic sign-offs
- No maintenance records (legally)
- No recurring ADs (Airworthiness Directives)

**BUT**, you are responsible for:
- Ensuring aircraft is safe to fly
- Performing all necessary maintenance
- Making airworthiness determinations
- Maintaining safety

**Manufacturer Recommendations**:
- POH contains maintenance schedules
- Follow manufacturer guidance
- More binding than FAA for Part 103

**Best Practice**:
- Treat your PPC as if it were certified
- Keep detailed maintenance records
- Follow a structured inspection program
- Seek qualified help when needed

### Levels of Maintenance

**Owner/Operator Maintenance** (you can do):
- Pre-flight inspections
- Post-flight inspections
- Routine servicing (oil, fuel, coolant)
- Cleaning and protecting
- Minor adjustments (cables, belts)
- Component replacement (spark plugs, filters)
- Basic troubleshooting

**Qualified Mechanic** (consider professional help):
- Engine overhaul
- Gearbox service
- Complex troubleshooting
- Structural repairs
- Propeller work
- Electrical system diagnosis

**Always Beyond Owner**:
- Welding structural components
- Wing repairs (use wing manufacturer/rigger)
- Propeller balancing
- Complex engine work if you lack experience

**When in Doubt**:
- Consult experienced PPC pilots
- Hire A&P mechanic (can work on ultralights)
- Contact manufacturer
- Seek training

## Section 2: Pre-Flight Inspection (Detailed)

A thorough pre-flight inspection is your primary defense against mechanical failure. This section expands on the quick checklist to explain WHAT to look for and WHY.

### Inspection Philosophy

**Every Flight**:
- No exceptions
- Even if you just flew yesterday
- Even if aircraft has been under cover
- Things can change overnight

**Systematic Approach**:
- Follow a pattern (clockwise from nose)
- Same order every time
- Don't skip items
- Touch, look, feel

**What You're Looking For**:
- Security (nothing loose)
- Integrity (nothing broken, cracked, or damaged)
- Condition (wear, corrosion, deterioration)
- Leaks (oil, fuel, coolant)
- Foreign objects (debris, nests, contamination)

**If in Doubt**:
- Investigate further
- Don't fly until resolved
- Better to scrub flight than crash

### Detailed Pre-Flight Inspection Procedure

#### **Documents and Paperwork**

**Check** (if applicable):
- [ ] Pilot proficiency (current? comfortable?)
- [ ] Aircraft documents (registration if applicable, though not required for Part 103)
- [ ] POH or operating limitations
- [ ] Weight and balance (within limits?)
- [ ] Personal minimums (conditions acceptable?)

#### **Nose Wheel and Steering**

**Tire**:
- [ ] Pressure: 8-12 psi (check with gauge, not visual)
- [ ] Tread: Adequate, no bald spots
- [ ] Sidewalls: No cracks, cuts, or bulges
- [ ] Wheel: Secure to fork, no wobble

**Why it Matters**: Under-inflated tire causes poor steering, difficulty controlling on ground. Over-inflated increases bounce, risk of blow-out.

**Steering System**:
- [ ] Steering bar moves smoothly
- [ ] No binding or sticking
- [ ] Bushings/bearings not excessively worn
- [ ] Cables or linkages secure, no fraying

**Brake** (if equipped on nose wheel):
- [ ] Operates smoothly
- [ ] Returns to off position
- [ ] No leaks (hydraulic)
- [ ] Pads have material remaining

#### **Front Left (Pilot Side)**

**Steering Lines**:
- [ ] Attached securely to steering bar
- [ ] Run through pulleys correctly
- [ ] No fraying or damage
- [ ] Proper tension (not slack, not over-tight)

**Trim System**:
- [ ] Set to appropriate position (check POH)
- [ ] Operates smoothly
- [ ] Locks in place
- [ ] Lines or mechanisms secure

**Instrument Panel**:
- [ ] All instruments present and undamaged
- [ ] Lenses clean, readable
- [ ] Mounts secure
- [ ] No cracks in panel

**Switches and Controls**:
- [ ] Ignition switch OFF
- [ ] Key removed (prevents accidental start)
- [ ] All switches functional
- [ ] Wiring secure, no chafing

#### **Seats and Restraints**

**Pilot Seat**:
- [ ] Secure to frame (check bolts)
- [ ] No rips or damage to fabric/cushion
- [ ] Seat belt attached securely at both ends
- [ ] Seat belt webbing not frayed or damaged

**Seat Belt/Harness**:
- [ ] Buckle operates correctly
- [ ] Latches positively
- [ ] Releases positively
- [ ] Adjusts smoothly
- [ ] No twists in webbing

**Why it Matters**: Seat belt is your primary restraint. In crash or hard landing, it keeps you in the aircraft and protects you. If it fails, serious injury or death likely.

**Passenger Seat** (if applicable):
- Same checks as pilot seat

#### **Riser Attachment Points (Both Sides)**

This is **CRITICAL** - riser failure means wing separation and certain death.

**Carabiner** (or attachment method):
- [ ] **LOCKED** - Safety gate closed and secured
- [ ] Not bent, deformed, or damaged
- [ ] Opens and closes smoothly (test it)
- [ ] Properly oriented (load along major axis)
- [ ] No cracks (inspect closely)

**Quick Link** (if used):
- [ ] Screwed tight (finger tight PLUS 1/4 turn with tool)
- [ ] Safety wire in place (if applicable)
- [ ] No cracks or damage
- [ ] Threads in good condition

**Riser**:
- [ ] Webbing in good condition (no fraying, cuts, abrasion)
- [ ] Stitching intact
- [ ] No twists or tangles
- [ ] Connects to correct side (left to left, right to right)

**Frame Attach Point**:
- [ ] Frame mount secure
- [ ] Bolts tight with lock nuts or cotter pins
- [ ] No cracks in frame at attachment
- [ ] No elongation of holes (bearing wear)

**Why it Matters**: Riser failure is catastrophic and non-survivable. These connections carry your entire weight plus flight loads. Check EVERY FLIGHT.

#### **Main Wheels (Both Sides)**

**Tire**:
- [ ] Pressure correct (check POH, usually 10-15 psi)
- [ ] Condition good (no cuts, bulges, bald spots)
- [ ] Sidewalls intact

**Wheel**:
- [ ] Secure to axle
- [ ] Spins freely (no binding)
- [ ] No wobble (wheel bearing okay)

**Axle**:
- [ ] Axle nut tight
- [ ] Cotter pin in place (or other safety device)
- [ ] Axle not bent

**Brake** (if equipped):
- [ ] Brake pads have material
- [ ] No fluid leaks (hydraulic brakes)
- [ ] Linkage secure (mechanical brakes)
- [ ] Operates smoothly when tested

#### **Frame and Structure**

Walk around entire frame, looking for:

**Frame Tubes**:
- [ ] No cracks (especially at welds and bends)
- [ ] No dents or deformation
- [ ] No corrosion (especially at base, where water sits)
- [ ] Welds intact (no cracks in weld beads)

**Bolts and Fasteners**:
- [ ] All present (none missing)
- [ ] Tight (no loose bolts)
- [ ] Lock nuts or cotter pins in place
- [ ] No damaged threads

**Pay Special Attention to**:
- Motor mount bolts
- Landing gear attach points
- Seat mounts
- Riser attach points

**Why it Matters**: Frame is primary structure. Frame failure can be catastrophic. Cracks propagate quickly in aluminum; chromoly is more forgiving but still serious.

#### **Engine - Left Side**

**Battery**:
- [ ] Secure in mount
- [ ] Terminals tight
- [ ] No corrosion on terminals (clean if present)
- [ ] Battery case not cracked
- [ ] Electrolyte level okay (if not sealed battery)

**Coolant System** (liquid-cooled engines):
- [ ] Coolant level in header tank (cold check)
- [ ] Level between MIN and MAX
- [ ] No leaks around hoses, clamps, radiator
- [ ] Hoses not cracked or soft (squeeze them)
- [ ] Clamps tight

**Why it Matters**: Low coolant = overheating = engine damage or failure. Leak will worsen in flight.

**Radiator**:
- [ ] Fins not damaged/bent excessively
- [ ] No leaks
- [ ] Securely mounted
- [ ] Air flow path clear (no debris, mud, grass)

**Exhaust System**:
- [ ] Springs in place and intact (connect exhaust to engine)
- [ ] No cracks in exhaust pipes or muffler
- [ ] Exhaust gaskets present
- [ ] Muffler secure

**Why it Matters**: Exhaust leaks can cause:
- Carbon monoxide poisoning
- Reduced engine performance
- Fire hazard
- Noise increase

**Spark Plugs**:
- [ ] Tight in cylinder head
- [ ] Spark plug wires attached
- [ ] Wires not cracked or damaged
- [ ] Boots secure on plugs

#### **Engine - Top**

**Carburetors**:
- [ ] Securely mounted to intake boots
- [ ] Intake boots not cracked (critical - causes air leak)
- [ ] Clamps tight on boots
- [ ] Throttle linkage secure and moves freely
- [ ] Choke operates (if equipped)

**Fuel System**:
- [ ] Fuel lines secure, not cracked
- [ ] Clamps tight
- [ ] No leaks at fittings
- [ ] Fuel filter clear (visible fuel, no debris)
- [ ] Primer bulb intact (if equipped)

**Ignition System**:
- [ ] Ignition coils secure
- [ ] Wiring in good condition
- [ ] No chafing on wires
- [ ] Mag switches operate correctly

#### **Propeller and Gearbox**

**Propeller**:
- [ ] Blades clean (remove bugs, grass, dirt)
- [ ] No nicks, cracks, or damage to blades
- [ ] Leading edges smooth
- [ ] No delamination (wood props)
- [ ] Hub secure

**Why it Matters**: Damaged propeller can:
- Vibrate excessively (damage engine/gearbox)
- Fail in flight (catastrophic imbalance)
- Reduce performance

**Prop Bolts**:
- [ ] All bolts present
- [ ] Tight (check with wrench)
- [ ] Lock washers or safety wire in place
- [ ] Bolt heads not damaged

**Propeller Cage/Guard**:
- [ ] All tubes present and secure
- [ ] No cracks in tubes
- [ ] Proper clearance between prop and cage (1-2 inches)
- [ ] Mounts secure to frame

**Gearbox**:
- [ ] No oil leaks from seals or case
- [ ] Oil level correct (check sight glass or dipstick per POH)
- [ ] Mounting bolts tight
- [ ] No cracks in gearbox housing

**Why it Matters**: Gearbox failure = instant loss of thrust. Oil loss = bearing failure = gearbox destruction.

#### **Engine - Right Side**

**Fuel System**:
- [ ] Fuel tank secure
- [ ] Cap tight and vented (check vent not plugged)
- [ ] No leaks
- [ ] Fuel level adequate for flight + reserve

**Gascolator** (fuel sediment bowl):
- [ ] **DRAIN** before every flight
- [ ] Catch fuel in clear container
- [ ] Check for water (separates, appears as bubbles or layer)
- [ ] Check for sediment
- [ ] Should be clean, clear fuel only
- [ ] Drain until fuel runs clear

**Why it Matters**: Water in fuel = engine failure. Always drain gascolator.

**Oil Tank** (2-stroke engines):
- [ ] Oil level full
- [ ] Type: 2-stroke oil only (check POH for specification)
- [ ] No leaks from tank or lines

**Rotary Valve Oil** (if equipped - Rotax 582):
- [ ] Level full in reservoir
- [ ] No leaks

**Coolant Overflow**:
- [ ] Not overflowing (indicates overfill or overheating)
- [ ] Level visible in overflow bottle

#### **Rear of Aircraft**

**Tail/Rear Frame**:
- [ ] Structure intact
- [ ] Tail wheel (if equipped) functional
- [ ] No damage

**General Rear Inspection**:
- [ ] All components secure
- [ ] No loose items
- [ ] No debris

#### **Wing Inspection (Before Layout)**

**Wing Bag**:
- [ ] Secure to cart
- [ ] Zippers functional
- [ ] No damage to bag

**Wing** (inspect before removing from bag if possible, or during layout):
- See "Wing Layout and Inspection" section below for detailed wing inspection

#### **Final Walk-Around**

**General**:
- [ ] Remove all covers (pitot, prop, exhaust, carb)
- [ ] Remove tie-downs
- [ ] Check area clear of tools, equipment
- [ ] Nothing left under or around aircraft
- [ ] Area clear for start and taxi

**Documents**:
- [ ] Weight and balance computed
- [ ] Fuel calculated
- [ ] Weather checked
- [ ] Personal minimums reviewed
- [ ] Go/No-Go decision: GO

## Section 3: Wing Inspection and Maintenance

The wing is your most critical component. Without it, you don't fly.

### Wing Inspection During Layout

**Site Selection**:
- Flat, clear area
- Free of debris (sticks, thorns, rocks)
- Downwind of aircraft (for inflation)

**Bag and Storage**:
- [ ] Bag in good condition
- [ ] Wing dry (no moisture in bag)
- [ ] Wing stored properly (loosely packed, not compressed long-term)

**Initial Inspection** (wing still folded/stacked):
- [ ] No unusual odors (mildew, fuel, oil)
- [ ] Fabric dry
- [ ] No stiffness or sticking (indicates contamination)

**Fabric Inspection** (walk length of wing):

**Top Surface**:
- [ ] No tears, punctures, or holes
- [ ] Fabric not faded excessively (UV damage)
- [ ] Stitching intact
- [ ] No abrasion areas

**Bottom Surface**:
- [ ] Same as top surface
- [ ] Check under cells for debris

**Leading Edge**:
- [ ] Openings clear (cells will inflate)
- [ ] Fabric reinforcement intact
- [ ] No damage from impacts

**Trailing Edge**:
- [ ] Stitching intact
- [ ] No tears

**Ribs** (internal structure):
- Hard to inspect fully, but check:
- [ ] No visible damage through cell openings
- [ ] Cells maintain shape

**Lines Inspection**:

This is **CRITICAL**. Line failure = loss of control = crash.

**Line Check Procedure**:
1. Start at risers
2. Follow EVERY line from riser to wing attachment
3. Check both sides completely
4. Look for:
   - Fraying (individual strands broken)
   - Abrasion (shiny spots, wear)
   - Cuts or nicks
   - Knots (indicates repair - have rigger inspect)
   - UV damage (fading, stiffness)
   - Contamination (oil, fuel - serious, have rigger inspect)

**Steering Lines**:
- [ ] Run through pulleys correctly
- [ ] Attached to toggles or steering bar
- [ ] Proper length (equal on both sides)
- [ ] No tangles or twists

**Line-Overs**:
- [ ] Check carefully for lines over top of wing
- [ ] One line looped over wing = serious control problem
- [ ] Must be clear

**Why it Matters**: Line-over or weak line causes asymmetric collapse, spin, or loss of control. Not survivable at low altitude.

### Wing Maintenance

**After Every Flight**:
- [ ] Pack wing properly (loosely, not compressed)
- [ ] Store in bag
- [ ] Keep dry
- [ ] Store in cool, dark place (UV and heat degrade fabric)

**Weekly** (if flying regularly):
- [ ] Check for new damage
- [ ] Look for wear areas
- [ ] Inspect line condition

**Monthly**:
- [ ] Thorough line inspection
- [ ] Fabric condition check
- [ ] Clean if needed (mild soap, water, air dry)

**Annually or 100 Hours**:
- [ ] Complete wing inspection by rigger or experienced pilot
- [ ] Consider line replacement (lines degrade with UV, age)
- [ ] Fabric inspection for porosity
- [ ] Check manufacturer recommendations

**Wing Cleaning**:
- **Mild soap and water only**
- **NO solvents, harsh chemicals**
- Air dry completely before packing
- Never store wet

**Wing Repairs**:
- Minor repairs: Ripstop tape (temporary)
- Significant repairs: **Only by qualified rigger**
- Line replacement: Rigger only (correct length, tension critical)
- Fabric patches: Rigger only

**When to Retire a Wing**:
- Extensive UV damage (fading, stiffness)
- Porosity (fabric allows air through)
- Multiple line replacements needed
- Manufacturer-specified hours/years reached
- Significant damage beyond repair

**Typical Wing Life**:
- 300-500 hours (varies by use, storage, care)
- 5-10 years (even with low hours, UV degrades fabric)
- More if stored indoors, less if left outside

## Section 4: Engine Maintenance

### Engine Break-In (New or Overhauled Engines)

**First 5 Hours**:
- Vary RPM (don't hold constant)
- Avoid full throttle for extended periods
- Change oil after first 5 hours
- Monitor temps closely
- Check for leaks after each flight

**5-25 Hours**:
- Gradually increase to full-power operations
- Continue varying RPM
- Oil change at 25 hours

**After 25 Hours**:
- Normal operations
- Continue monitoring

### Routine Engine Maintenance

**Before Every Flight**:
- [ ] Oil level check (2-stroke: injection tank full)
- [ ] Coolant level check
- [ ] Fuel level and quality check
- [ ] Drain gascolator (check for water)
- [ ] Visual inspection (leaks, security)
- [ ] Gearbox oil level check

**After Every Flight**:
- [ ] Quick visual inspection for leaks
- [ ] Check for unusual sounds during shutdown
- [ ] Note any anomalies in logbook

**Every 10 Hours or Monthly**:
- [ ] Spark plug inspection:
  - Remove and inspect
  - Clean or replace if fouled
  - Gap to specification (usually 0.020")
  - Check for cracks in insulator
  - Electrode wear
- [ ] Carburetor synchronization (2 carbs):
  - Carbs should open equally
  - Adjust if needed
- [ ] Fuel filter inspection/replacement
- [ ] Air filter cleaning or replacement (if equipped)
- [ ] Check all hoses and clamps
- [ ] Inspect exhaust system
- [ ] Clean cooling fins on cylinders (air-cooled) or radiator (liquid-cooled)

**Every 25 Hours**:
- [ ] Gearbox oil change (check POH - some are 50 hrs)
- [ ] Thorough inspection of engine mounts
- [ ] Check propeller torque
- [ ] Inspect drive belt (if belt drive)
- [ ] Rotary valve oil change (Rotax 582)

**Every 50 Hours**:
- [ ] Compression test (if you have gauge)
- [ ] Inspect exhaust gaskets, replace if needed
- [ ] Magneto timing check
- [ ] Inspect magneto points (if point-type ignition)

**Every 100 Hours or Annually**:
- [ ] Complete engine inspection
- [ ] Replace spark plugs
- [ ] Inspect carburetor internals (clean or rebuild)
- [ ] Replace fuel lines and filter
- [ ] Coolant replacement
- [ ] Inspect all engine mounts and fasteners
- [ ] Check for cracks in engine case
- [ ] Consider professional inspection

**Every 300 Hours** (2-stroke) or **Per Manufacturer** (4-stroke):
- [ ] Top-end overhaul (2-stroke):
  - Pistons, rings, cylinders inspected/replaced
  - Crankshaft seals replaced
  - Bearings inspected
  - **Professional mechanic recommended**

**Every 600-1000 Hours** (varies by engine):
- [ ] Complete engine overhaul
- [ ] All internal components inspected/replaced
- [ ] **Professional mechanic required**

### Rotax 582 Specific Maintenance

**Rotary Valve Oil System**:
- Check level before every flight
- Change oil every 25 hours
- Use specified oil (usually ATF - check POH)

**Gearbox "E" Type**:
- Check oil level before every flight (sight glass)
- Change oil every 25-50 hours (check POH)
- Use specified oil (usually 80W90 gear oil)
- Watch for metal particles in oil (bearing wear)

**Coolant System**:
- Check level before every flight
- Burp system after coolant service (remove air pockets)
- Use 50/50 mix of coolant and distilled water
- Replace coolant annually

**Dual Ignition**:
- Two separate magneto systems
- If one fails, engine continues to run (reduced power)
- Mag check before every flight critical

### Troubleshooting Common Engine Issues

**Hard Starting**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Fouled spark plugs
  - Weak battery
  - Bad ignition components
  - Carb adjustment needed
  - Fuel contamination or stale fuel
- **Checks**:
  - Clean/replace spark plugs
  - Charge battery
  - Check mag switches work
  - Adjust carbs
  - Drain old fuel, refill with fresh

**Rough Running**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Fouled plug (one cylinder)
  - Carb sync off
  - Air leak in intake boot
  - Bad fuel
- **Checks**:
  - Mag check (isolate cylinder)
  - Sync carburetors
  - Inspect intake boots
  - Check fuel quality

**Overheating**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Low coolant
  - Radiator blocked
  - Lean fuel mixture
  - Too much load (climbing in hot wx)
- **Checks**:
  - Check/fill coolant
  - Clean radiator fins
  - Adjust carbs (richer)
  - Reduce climb rate, level off

**High EGT**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Lean mixture
  - Exhaust leak
  - Bad temp probe
- **Checks**:
  - Enrich carb adjustment
  - Inspect exhaust
  - Verify gauge accuracy

**Low RPM / Lack of Power**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Prop pitch too coarse
  - Rich mixture
  - Ignition timing off
  - Partial blockage (air or fuel)
- **Checks**:
  - Check static RPM (should reach spec)
  - Adjust carbs
  - Check timing
  - Clean air and fuel filters

**Excessive Vibration**:
- **Possible Causes**:
  - Prop imbalance (damage, dirt)
  - Engine mount loose
  - Gearbox issue
- **Checks**:
  - Inspect and clean prop
  - Tighten motor mounts
  - Check gearbox for noise, oil

## Section 5: Periodic Inspections and Condition Assessment

### Annual Condition Inspection (Recommended)

Even though not legally required for Part 103, an annual condition inspection is highly recommended.

**Inspection Scope**:
- Complete airframe inspection
- Full engine inspection
- Wing inspection (by rigger if possible)
- All systems checked
- Flight test

**Who Can Do It**:
- Experienced PPC pilot/builder
- A&P mechanic
- Manufacturer-certified technician
- You, if you're qualified and thorough

**Checklist for Annual**:

**Airframe**:
- [ ] All frame tubes for cracks, corrosion, damage
- [ ] All welds inspected
- [ ] All bolts torqued and safetied
- [ ] Wheels and tires
- [ ] Seat and restraints
- [ ] Riser attachments (critical)
- [ ] Instrument mounts
- [ ] Fuel tank and mounts
- [ ] Propeller cage

**Engine**:
- [ ] Compression test
- [ ] Spark plugs replaced
- [ ] Carburetor inspection/cleaning
- [ ] Fuel system inspection
- [ ] Ignition system check
- [ ] Exhaust system
- [ ] Cooling system (flush and refill)
- [ ] Engine mounts
- [ ] Gearbox inspection and service
- [ ] Propeller torque check

**Electrical**:
- [ ] Battery condition and charge
- [ ] All wiring for chafing, security
- [ ] Switches functional
- [ ] Instruments functional
- [ ] Lighting (if equipped)

**Wing**:
- [ ] Complete fabric inspection
- [ ] Line inspection (every line)
- [ ] Riser inspection
- [ ] Inflate and check for leaks (cell damage)
- [ ] Line length check (measure)

**Documentation**:
- [ ] Logbook entries
- [ ] Maintenance records reviewed
- [ ] Operating limitations current
- [ ] Weight and balance verified

**Flight Test**:
- [ ] Post-inspection flight test
- [ ] Verify all systems
- [ ] Check for unusual vibrations, noises
- [ ] Sign off inspection as satisfactory

### 25-Hour Inspection

**Quick Overview**:
- [ ] Gearbox oil change
- [ ] Thorough engine and airframe inspection
- [ ] Verify torque on critical bolts
- [ ] Clean and service
- [ ] Address any squawks from flights

### 50-Hour Inspection

**Detailed Check**:
- [ ] Everything from 25-hour
- [ ] Compression test
- [ ] Deeper inspection of engine internals (valves if 4-stroke)
- [ ] Exhaust system service
- [ ] Mag timing check
- [ ] Wing and line inspection
- [ ] Update logs

### 100-Hour Inspection

**Major Inspection**:
- [ ] Approach this as a mini-annual
- [ ] Replace consumables (plugs, filters, fluids)
- [ ] Detailed inspection of all systems
- [ ] Address all deferred maintenance
- [ ] Document thoroughly

## Section 6: Record Keeping

### Why Keep Records (Even Though Not Required)

**Benefits**:
- Track maintenance history
- Identify recurring issues
- Document compliance with manufacturer guidance
- Prove due diligence if accident occurs
- Aid in troubleshooting
- Increase resale value
- Reference for future maintenance

**What to Record**:
- Flight hours (total and since last service)
- Maintenance performed (what, when, by whom)
- Inspections (date, findings, actions)
- Repairs (what was broken, how fixed)
- Parts replaced (what, when, part number)
- Anomalies or discrepancies noted
- Fuel, oil, coolant consumption

### Logbook Systems

**Aircraft Logbook**:
- Record all maintenance actions
- Chronological format
- Date, hours, description, signature

**Engine Logbook**:
- Separate log for engine
- Tracks engine-specific hours
- Maintenance on engine
- Overhaul history

**Maintenance Tracking**:
- Spreadsheet or app
- Tracks recurring items (oil changes, inspections)
- Alerts when due

**Parts and Component Log**:
- Serial numbers
- Install date
- Hours on component
- Replacement history

**Example Logbook Entry**:
```
Date: 2025-11-08
Aircraft Hours: 127.5
Engine Hours: 127.5
Work Performed:
- Pre-flight inspection completed, no discrepancies
- Oil tank filled (added 8 oz 2-stroke oil)
- Coolant level checked, OK
- Gascolator drained, no water
- Flight of 1.2 hours, local area, conditions VFR
- Post-flight inspection, aircraft secure
Signed: [Your Name]
```

**Example Maintenance Entry**:
```
Date: 2025-11-08
Aircraft Hours: 125.0
Engine Hours: 125.0
Maintenance:
- 100-hour inspection performed
- Spark plugs replaced (NGK BR9ES, qty 2)
- Gearbox oil changed (Rotax type E, 80W90)
- Coolant flushed and replaced (50/50 mix)
- Fuel filter replaced
- All systems inspected, no discrepancies found
- Ground run and mag check satisfactory
- Aircraft returned to service
Signed: [Your Name or Mechanic]
```

### Digital vs. Paper

**Paper Logbooks**:
- Traditional
- No power/tech required
- Can't be "deleted"
- Hard to search/analyze

**Digital Logbooks**:
- Apps or spreadsheets
- Easy to search and analyze
- Backup (but MUST back up!)
- Can generate reports

**Best Practice**: Keep both
- Primary: Digital (easy to use)
- Backup: Print yearly summary for paper file

## Section 7: Common Maintenance Tasks (How-To)

### Spark Plug Service

**Removal**:
1. Let engine cool completely
2. Disconnect spark plug wires (note which is which)
3. Clean area around plug (prevent debris falling into cylinder)
4. Use proper spark plug socket
5. Remove plugs

**Inspection**:
- Normal: Light tan/gray, minimal deposits
- Fouled: Black, sooty (too rich or oil)
- Overheated: White, blistered (too lean)
- Wet: Fuel or oil (starting flood or oil leak)

**Cleaning**:
- Wire brush or spark plug cleaner
- Blow out with compressed air
- Do NOT sandblast (damages insulator)

**Gapping**:
- Use feeler gauge
- Typical gap: 0.018-0.020" (check POH)
- Adjust by bending ground electrode (carefully)

**Installation**:
1. Inspect threads, clean if needed
2. Install by hand (cross-threading check)
3. Tighten to torque spec (typically 18-22 ft-lbs for 14mm plugs)
4. Reconnect wires to correct plugs

### Gearbox Oil Change

**Preparation**:
- Warm engine (oil flows better warm)
- Position aircraft level
- Have drain pan ready
- Have correct oil and amount ready

**Drain**:
1. Locate drain plug (bottom of gearbox)
2. Place drain pan underneath
3. Remove drain plug
4. Let oil drain completely (5-10 minutes)
5. Inspect drained oil for metal particles (bearing wear indicator)

**Fill**:
1. Replace drain plug with new washer/gasket, torque to spec
2. Locate fill plug (usually on side of gearbox)
3. Remove fill plug
4. Fill with correct oil (Rotax E gearbox: 80W90 gear oil, 250ml / 8.5 oz)
5. Oil should come to bottom of fill plug hole
6. Replace fill plug

**Check**:
- Start engine, idle briefly
- Shut down, recheck oil level (sight glass or fill plug)
- Top off if needed
- Check for leaks

### Coolant Service

**Draining**:
1. Engine cold
2. Place pan under drain (usually radiator or bottom hose)
3. Open drain valve or remove hose
4. Drain completely

**Flushing** (every 2-3 years):
1. After draining, fill with water
2. Run engine to circulate
3. Drain again
4. Repeat until water runs clear

**Filling**:
1. Close drain
2. Fill with 50/50 mix of coolant and distilled water
3. Fill header tank to MAX line
4. Run engine to operating temp (thermostat opens)
5. Burp system (squeeze hoses, add coolant as level drops)
6. Let cool, recheck level, top off

**Coolant Type**:
- Use automotive antifreeze (ethylene glycol)
- Rotax recommends specific types (check POH)
- Do NOT mix different types

### Carburetor Synchronization (Dual Carb Engines)

**Why Sync?**
- Dual carbs must open equally
- If unsynced, one cylinder works harder
- Causes rough running, poor performance

**Tools Needed**:
- Tachometer
- Screwdriver

**Procedure**:
1. Engine at operating temperature
2. Set idle to spec (usually 1,800-2,000 RPM)
3. Slowly advance throttle to 3,500-4,000 RPM
4. Listen/feel for roughness
5. Adjust sync screw (usually on linkage between carbs)
6. Goal: Smooth running at all RPM ranges
7. Final check: Full throttle should give spec RPM (e.g., 6,400)

**If Unable to Sync**:
- May need internal carb adjustment (jets, idle screws)
- Consider professional carb service

## Section 8: Troubleshooting Guide

### Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

**Step 1: Identify the Symptom**
- What exactly is wrong?
- When does it occur?
- How often?
- Any recent changes or maintenance?

**Step 2: Gather Information**
- Check logs for recent work
- Ask: Has anything changed?
- Inspect related systems

**Step 3: Develop Hypotheses**
- What could cause this symptom?
- List possibilities (most likely first)

**Step 4: Test Hypotheses**
- Check simplest/most likely causes first
- Eliminate possibilities methodically

**Step 5: Fix and Verify**
- Repair or replace faulty component
- Test thoroughly before flight
- Monitor during flight

### Troubleshooting Chart

| **Symptom** | **Possible Causes** | **Checks/Solutions** |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Won't start | Dead battery, fouled plugs, no fuel, mag switch off | Charge battery, clean/replace plugs, check fuel, verify switches |
| Starts then dies | Flooded, fuel starvation, bad idle adjustment | Wait (if flooded), check fuel flow, adjust idle |
| Rough idle | Sync off, fouled plug, air leak | Sync carbs, check plugs, inspect intake boots |
| Rough running | One mag failing, sync off, air leak, bad fuel | Mag check, sync, inspect boots, drain/refill fuel |
| Overheating | Low coolant, blocked radiator, lean mix | Check coolant, clean radiator, enrich mixture |
| Low RPM | Prop pitch wrong, rich mixture, drag | Check static RPM vs. spec, adjust carbs, inspect for damage |
| High EGT | Lean mixture, exhaust leak | Enrich mixture, inspect exhaust |
| Vibration | Prop damaged/dirty, loose mounts | Clean/inspect prop, tighten mounts, check gearbox |
| Oil in air filter | Too much oil, worn rings | Reduce oil ratio, compression test |
| Coolant loss | Leak, blown head gasket | Pressure test system, inspect for leaks |
| Fuel smell | Leak, overflow | Inspect fuel system, check carb float level |

## Section 9: Safety Wire and Lock Wire

### When to Use Safety Wire

**Critical Fasteners** that must not loosen:
- Propeller bolts
- Engine mount bolts
- Landing gear axle nuts (alternative: cotter pins)
- Carabiner gates (some types)
- Quick links

**How Safety Wire Works**:
- Prevents bolt from turning in loosening direction
- Requires bolt to tighten if wire tension is applied
- "Pull to tighten" principle

### Basic Safety Wiring Technique

**Materials**:
- 0.032" stainless steel safety wire
- Safety wire pliers (twist wire)
- Wire cutters

**Procedure** (single wire, two bolts):
1. Tighten both bolts to torque spec
2. Thread wire through hole in first bolt head
3. Bring wire across to second bolt
4. Thread through second bolt
5. Pull tight
6. Twist wires together (6-8 twists per inch)
7. Cut wire, bend end so no sharp point

**Direction**:
- Wire must pull bolts in tightening direction
- Clockwise threads: Wire pulls clockwise when tensioned

**Check**:
- Wire tight (no slack)
- Proper twist (not too loose, not over-twisted)
- No sharp ends

## Section 10: When to Seek Professional Help

### You Should Get Professional Help When:

**Structural Issues**:
- Cracks in frame
- Damage to welds
- Major damage from crash/hard landing

**Engine Overhaul**:
- Top-end or complete overhaul
- Internal engine work beyond your experience
- Gearbox overhaul

**Wing Repairs**:
- Fabric damage
- Line replacement
- Rigging changes

**Electrical Diagnosis**:
- Complex electrical problems
- Charging system issues (if you're not electrically inclined)

**Propeller Work**:
- Prop damage
- Prop balancing

**Unusual or Recurring Problems**:
- Issue you can't diagnose
- Problem that returns after "fix"

**Lack of Confidence**:
- If you're not certain you can do it safely
- Better to ask than guess

### Finding Qualified Help

**Where to Look**:
- Other PPC pilots (ask for recommendations)
- A&P mechanics (can work on ultralights)
- Rotax service centers (for engine work)
- Ultralight repair stations
- Manufacturer support

**What to Ask**:
- Experience with PPCs or ultralights?
- Familiar with your engine type?
- References from other owners?

## Conclusion

Maintenance is not optional - it's essential for safe flight. While Part 103 gives you freedom from FAA-mandated inspections, you carry the full responsibility for ensuring your aircraft is airworthy.

**Key Principles to Remember**:
1. **Pre-flight every flight** - No exceptions
2. **Follow manufacturer guidance** - They know the aircraft best
3. **Keep records** - Track maintenance and history
4. **Be conservative** - If in doubt, don't fly until resolved
5. **Seek help when needed** - Know your limits
6. **Stay ahead of maintenance** - Preventive is cheaper than corrective

**Your Life Depends on Proper Maintenance**: Every bolt, every line, every fluid level matters. A $2 spark plug or $20 oil change can prevent a catastrophic failure.

**Develop a Maintenance Mindset**:
- See maintenance as part of flying, not a chore
- Take pride in a well-maintained aircraft
- Enjoy the knowledge and connection with your machine
- Fly with confidence knowing your aircraft is airworthy

**Remember**: A well-maintained powered parachute is a safe, reliable, enjoyable aircraft. Neglect maintenance, and you're gambling with your life.
